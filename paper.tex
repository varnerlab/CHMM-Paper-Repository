\begin{abstract}
Low-state-count hidden Markov models with Gaussian emissions were long held to fail at reproducing the slow autocorrelation decay of absolute daily equity returns. We revisit that limitation with continuous-emission hidden Markov models that place Gaussian, Student-$t$, Laplace, and generalized-error emissions under one expectation-maximization framework with quantile-based initialization, plus a spectral identity that writes the absolute-return autocorrelation as finitely many geometric decay modes. The main finding, on SPY and a sector-balanced 30-ticker panel, separates the two failure channels: at three states the binding constraint is the marginal mixture, not the decay-mode budget. A heavy-tailed emission substantially improves the marginal fit, the fitted chain reduces in-sample absolute-return autocorrelation error relative to i.i.d.\ baselines at lags $1$--$63$, and converged multistart fits at $K = 18$ do not materially improve the autocorrelation match over three states (better at $7$ of $31$ tickers in sample). The boundary is equally sharp: the fitted models do not reproduce long-horizon persistence (lags beyond roughly three months), and while genuinely non-geometric asymptotics require a different duration law or long-memory latent process, over the finite horizon studied the constraint is the estimation objective rather than the state count --- a direct approximation bound shows two free decay modes already come within $0.004$ MAE of seventeen. Two applications exercise the fitted model: a regime-conditional Value-at-Risk that is not rejected by joint conditional-coverage tests at the $5\%$ level on held-out data and on most walk-forward folds, and a multi-asset elliptical-copula composition in which a suggestive $2 \times 2$ experiment on identical quarter-level targets finds that quarterly refit of the dependence layer reduces out-of-sample dependence error by an order of magnitude more than the Gaussian-versus-Student-$t$ family choice. The evaluation covers daily US equities on a single out-of-sample window containing a vendor feed change; periodic refitting is recommended when regimes drift.
\end{abstract}
