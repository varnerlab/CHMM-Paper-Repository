A continuous HMM trained by Baum-Welch reproduces the three Cont stylized facts simultaneously on SPY at two held-out-clean operating points: at $K^\star = 3$, CHMM-N attains $89.7\%$ IS / $80.1\%$ OoS KS at $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $0.0467$; at $K^\star = 6$ the four emission variants attain $88.3$--$92.6\%$ IS / $76.3$--$79.0\%$ OoS KS, with CHMM-N's simulated kurtosis $5.26$ matching observed OoS $5.29$ within $0.03$. Four emission variants share the same scaffold and land at four distinct kurtosis points without retuning $K$ or the transition structure. The mechanism behind the simultaneous KS / ACF fit is the spectral identity~\eqref{eq:acf_normalised}: at $K \ge 3$ with quantile-initialised Baum-Welch, the absolute-return ACF is a population-level real-coefficient sum of geometric decays at the non-unit eigenvalues of $\mathbf T$.

\paragraph{Replicating the slow-ACF behaviour at $K \ge 3$.}
Two issues compound in the \citet{ryden1998stylized} low-$K$ setup: a single self-transition rate produces a single exponential mode that cannot be simultaneously fast and slow, and the random initialisations standard in the 1990s often converge to degenerate local optima. Our $K = 2$ replication on SPY (Appendix~\ref{sec:ryden_replication}) clarifies the mechanism: ACF-MAE is essentially constant at $\approx 0.050$ across quantile and five random-seed initialisations, matching the $K = 18$ CHMM-N. The binding low-$K$ constraint is therefore distributional (KS at $K = 2$ drops to $67$--$72\%$): with modern initialisation the ACF is reproduced at any $K \ge 2$, while distributional fidelity drives the choice of $K$. The effective-rank diagnostic in Appendix~\ref{sec:spectral_rank} makes this microscopically explicit: at the fitted $\hat{\mathbf T}$ for $K = 18$, a single non-unit eigenvalue carries $93.6\%$ of the lag-1 absolute-return ACF, and at $K = 3$ the dominant non-unit mode alone carries $96.8\%$.

\paragraph{Closing the kurtosis gap with heavy-tailed emissions.}
CHMM-N produces excess kurtosis $5.02$ against observed $7.68$: a Gaussian mixture's kurtosis is bounded by the variance of component means relative to their variances. CHMM-t addresses this through per-state Student-$t$ emissions but at the cost of an IS overshoot to $14.57$ from a single-state ECM lower-bracket pinning artefact (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}); an exponential $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage at rate $\lambda = 20$ brings simulated kurtosis to $\approx 8$ at a $1$pp KS cost. The $\lambda = 20$ choice is held-out-clean: refitting on the strictly pre-2020 estimation slice (2014-01-03 to 2018-06-29) and selecting $\lambda$ by validation log-likelihood on the 2018-07-02 to 2019-12-31 slice yields $\lambda^\star = 20$ on the grid $\{0, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200\}$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:lambda_cv_pre2020}). The penalty is held-out-clean at the SPY level but per-ticker (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, Table~\ref{tab:per_ticker_lambda}) the body uniform $\lambda = 20$ is over-shrunk on three of six tickers; the production recipe is the per-ticker validation rule of Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate}. We note the related question of bracket choice: $\nu_{\min} = 2.1$ in the body construction lies below the kurtosis-finiteness threshold $\nu = 4$, so the lower-bracket pinning is partly an artefact of the bracket itself. A direct $\nu_{\min} = 4$ ablation (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, $\dagger\dagger$ row) brings simulated IS kurtosis from $14.35$ to $13.25$ ($\sim 1$ unit), still well above the bootstrap CI of observed ($7.68$ IS), with IS / OoS KS unchanged within sampling error ($95.5 / 85.0\%$ vs $95.6 / 85.7\%$). The bracket lift alone is therefore not a substitute for the $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage; the kurtosis overshoot is a multi-state pinning effect that the penalty addresses directly and the bracket only partially mitigates. We report the penalised construction in the body for direct comparability with \citet{peel2000robust, liu1995ml} and the bracket-lift row alongside as the structurally-cleaner alternative. CHMM-L sits between CHMM-N and CHMM-t at $6.75$ without any shape parameter; CHMM-GED's bimodal $\hat p_k$ partition (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_p_partition}) is the data-driven analog of a hand-classified Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail hybrid. The four-way variant decision is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:variant_choice}; bootstrap CIs on observed kurtosis (Appendix~\ref{sec:kurtosis_bootstrap_ci}) show that the IS-OoS difference of $\sim 2.4$ units is not statistically distinguishable at conventional levels, so the variant rankings should be read as point-comparisons inside a wide CI envelope.

\paragraph{Practical identifiability and the effective state count at $K = 18$.}
The $K = 18$ operating point is partly nominal: the standardized-distance single-linkage diagnostic of Appendix~\ref{sec:state_distinctness} collapses CHMM-N to $11/18$ effective states and CHMM-t to $12/18$, with $13/18$ CHMM-t states pinned at the upper $\nu$ bracket on SPY. Three implications follow. (i) The numerical full-rank check on $\hat{\mathbf T}$ in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_propositions}, which reports $\mathrm{rank}(\hat{\mathbf T} - \mathbf 1\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}^\top) = K - 1 = 17$ at tolerance $10^{-10}$, exhausts only algebraic rank; the practically identifiable rank is closer to $K_{\text{eff}} - 1 \approx 10$. (ii) The AIC / BIC parameter count at $K = 18$ over-credits the model; an information-criterion penalty computed against $K_{\text{eff}}$ rather than the nominal $K$ would re-rank $K^\star \in \{6, 12\}$ in the held-out-clean panel (Section~\ref{sec:k_selection_results}, Table~\ref{tab:k_selection}) more consistently with each other. (iii) The headline-metrics interpretation is not affected: simulation under the full $K = 18$ mixture and the metric panel are computed on whatever the converged model is; the question is whether the parameter count claimed against the data is honest at $K_{\text{eff}}$ or at $K_{\text{nom}}$. We read this as a representational-economy observation: collapsed clusters carry small stationary-mass tails and contribute consistently to the simulation, but the model is best described as a $K_{\text{eff}}$-state model expressed in a $K_{\text{nom}}$-state parameterisation. The held-out-clean $K^\star = 6$ operating point (Section~\ref{sec:k_selection_results}) is closer to the practically identifiable resolution and should be the default when reporting parameter counts; the $K = 18$ extended-state-resolution row is the right operating point when kurtosis fidelity is the priority.

\paragraph{Stationarity scope and operational deployment.}
The CHMM is trained on the assumption that returns are locally stationary across the IS window. Single-name equity returns are well known to carry sector-specific structural breaks (\citealp{pastorstambaugh2001equity}; \citealp{andreoughysels2002breaks}; surveyed by \citealp{angtimmermann2012regime}); the cross-ticker generalisation in Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate} reflects this directly, with $11/30$ tickers below $60\%$ OoS KS, concentrated in single-name regime introductions. The mitigation is periodic refit (\citealp{pesarantimmermann2007window}; an online-EM formulation by \citealp{cappe2011online}): a quarterly-refit version of the cross-ticker panel (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_ticker_quarterly_refit}) shifts the OoS KS distribution from median $73.4\% \to 83.0\%$ and reduces failures from $11/30$ to $7/30$. The walk-forward W2 (COVID) and W4 (2022 rate-hike onset) stress folds remain out-of-distribution by KS for every generator under any refit cadence (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}); these are regime introductions the IS distribution does not span. Discussion of the asymmetry between the W4 onset (catastrophic) and the headline 2024-2026 reversal (clean), the OoS regime trajectory under IS-fixed parameters, the per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ for the penalised CHMM-t, and the IS / OoS kurtosis CI overlap are in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, \ref{sec:oos_regime_trajectory}, and \ref{sec:kurtosis_bootstrap_ci}.

\paragraph{Stylized-fact scope: the leverage effect is partially captured via state-mixing.}
The Cont (2001) stylized-fact list also includes the leverage effect (negative $\mathrm{Corr}(G_t, |G_{t+1}|)$ on equity returns) and gain-loss asymmetry; the present paper's body evaluation covers only the three symmetric stylized facts (heavy-tailed marginals, negligible linear ACF, slow $|G_t|$ ACF). All four CHMM emission families in this paper carry symmetric per-state densities (Gaussian, Laplace, Student-$t$, GED at any $p_k$), so we initially expected the CHMM to fail the leverage axis by construction; the empirical finding contradicts this. We compute $\mathrm{Corr}(G_t, |G_{t+1}|)$ on observed SPY (IS $-0.135$, OoS $-0.214$) and on the per-path simulation distribution from each generator at $N_{\text{paths}} = 500$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:leverage_effect}). At $K = 18$ the CHMM-N path-level median is $-0.089$ on IS and $-0.087$ on OoS, with $[Q_5, Q_{95}]$ envelopes $[-0.138, -0.037]$ IS and $[-0.190, +0.015]$ OoS: the IS observed value sits at the $Q_5$ boundary of the CHMM-N envelope, the OoS observed value sits just below the $Q_5$ floor. The mechanism is that the converged $\hat{\mathbf T}$ couples high-$\sigma_k$ states to negative-$\mu_k$ states asymmetrically, so the Markov mixing produces a non-trivial leverage signal even though each per-state emission is symmetric. The CHMM-N envelope is competitive with EGARCH (median $-0.095$, $[Q_5, Q_{95}] = [-0.147, -0.047]$ IS) and tighter than GARCH(1,1) Gaussian (median $-0.004$, indistinguishable from zero); the asymmetric GARCH baselines (EGARCH, GJR-GARCH) produce slightly stronger leverage in the median but their envelopes also bracket roughly $65\%$ of the observed magnitude. Skew-emission extensions (skew-$t$ in the tradition of \citealp{azzalini2003distributions}; skew-Laplace; skew-GED) remain the natural fix for closing the residual gap to the OoS observed leverage and are deferred as companion-paper directions. The body claim is therefore: CHMM at $K = 18$ partially reproduces the leverage stylized fact through Markov state-mixing alone, with the per-path distribution bracketing the IS observed value at its lower $5\%$ tail; tighter leverage match requires asymmetric per-state emissions.

\paragraph{Baseline-implementation caveats.}
Three rows of our extended baseline panel (Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines}) are repo-native re-implementations rather than reference-implementation runs and should be read with that caveat. (i) MS-GARCH at $K \in \{2, 3, 4, 6\}$ is fit by grid-initialised Nelder-Mead in our own implementation; the standard reference is the \texttt{MSGARCH} R package of \citet{ardia2019msgarch} which uses constrained-Bayesian estimation with proper-prior regularisation. The $27$--$37\%$ IS KS plateau we observe could be partly an estimator-quality artefact rather than a structural ceiling on MS-GARCH; the body conclusion ``the multi-state benefit is specific to the CHMM scaffold'' should be read as ``in our re-implementation, MS-GARCH does not close the gap'' rather than as a verdict on the MS-GARCH literature. (ii) The QuantGAN row (Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_supp}) is a three-conv-layer convolutional WGAN that is materially smaller than the seven-block TCN of \citet{wiese2020quantgan} and lacks the Lambert-W input pre-processing that the original authors credit with most of the tail-fidelity. The $0\%$ IS / OoS KS pass rate is therefore the failure mode of \emph{this} implementation, not a verdict on the WGAN literature; it functions as a deep-generative negative control rather than a competitive benchmark. (iii) The SM-CHMM row of Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines} is fit by a Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in estimator rather than by full ML on the explicit-duration HSMM; the ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ row of Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} is the proper HSMM benchmark and wins the OoS KS column. We do not claim that an ML-estimated HSMM at higher $K$ would underperform CHMM at the same $K$, only that the present comparison is at each method's best operating point. A reference-implementation re-run of MS-GARCH and a full Wiese et al. TCN re-run are the right next-step controls; we report the present panel honestly with the caveats above.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Skew-heavy-tailed emissions (skew-$t$ or skew-Laplace) and explicit-duration semi-Markov sojourns at higher $K$ are deferred as companion-paper directions; the leverage-effect diagnostic (Appendix~\ref{sec:leverage_effect}) shows the residual gap to OoS observed leverage will require asymmetric per-state emissions to close. Scaling the multi-asset construction to larger $d$ via untruncated regular vines or factor copulas is the natural follow-up. The state-selection rule for the $K = 18$ extended-state-resolution panel uses information-criterion plus distributional pass-rate rather than strictly held-out log-likelihood; held-out-clean re-selection picks $K^\star = 3$ or $K^\star = 6$ depending on the validation slice (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}), and the body $K^\star = 6$ block is the held-out-clean default for synthetic-data consumers. Independent-decade validation (a 1994--2004 vs.\ 2014--2024 SPY split) was attempted via the Alpaca Markets SDK and shown infeasible on the available data sources (Appendix~\ref{sec:alpaca_depth_probe}, Table~\ref{tab:alpaca_depth_probe}); both the IEX feed (coverage from 2018-11-01 onwards) and the SIP feed (coverage from 2016-01-04 onwards) return zero bars for the 1994-2004 target window. The Polygon.io / Massive feed already in the body pipeline begins at 2014-01-03. Independent-decade validation therefore requires a different data source (e.g., Bloomberg, Refinitiv, or a CRSP licence with deep historical coverage) and is logged for the next data-pipeline pass; the empirical scope of the present paper is the 2014-2026 SPY window plus the OoS extension. The empirical scope is daily US-equity stylized-fact reproduction; FX, fixed-income, and commodity asset classes are not covered, and the GLD / SLV non-equity stress test in Appendix~\ref{sec:non_us_asset_supp} documents that the static IS-fitted CHMM does not transfer to those windows without periodic refit. The cross-asset dependence-family selection (Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset}) is an IS-calibration distinction: on the OoS window the Gaussian and Student-$t$ copulas are statistically indistinguishable at $N_{\text{paths}} = 200$. We have addressed five of the deferred robustness items raised in pre-submission peer review: the OoS-anchored block-bootstrap KS recalibration (Appendix~\ref{sec:ks_block_bootstrap_oos}) confirms the IS CHMM-vs-GARCH ordering survives on OoS; the leverage-effect diagnostic (Appendix~\ref{sec:leverage_effect}) shows the CHMM family at $K = 18$ partially captures the leverage stylized fact via Markov state-mixing despite symmetric per-state emissions; the full one-shot Student-$t$ copula MLE (Appendix~\ref{sec:full_tcopula_mle}) shows the body two-step Kendall's-$\tau$ + profile-MLE construction is not biased toward the Gaussian limit on the six-asset universe; the Alpaca SDK independent-decade probe (Appendix~\ref{sec:alpaca_depth_probe}) documents the data-availability ceiling at 2014--2026 and scopes the present paper accordingly; and the QuantGAN reference TCN rebuild (Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_tcn}) reports a Wiese-style dilated TCN architecture as a stronger deep-generative comparator than the body's 3-conv-layer baseline. The remaining priority control is a reference-implementation re-run of MS-GARCH under the \texttt{MSGARCH} R package of \citet{ardia2019msgarch}; this requires an R installation outside the present environment scope and is logged for the next data-pipeline pass.
