A continuous HMM trained by Baum-Welch reproduces the three Cont stylized facts simultaneously on SPY at two operating points: at the held-out-likelihood and BIC choice $K^\star = 3$, CHMM-N attains $89.7\%$ IS KS and $80.1\%$ OoS KS at $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $0.0467$ (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}); at the tail-fidelity panel $K = 18$, the four emission variants attain $93.6$--$95.6\%$ IS KS and $80.8$--$85.7\%$ OoS KS, with CHMM-N $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE within $0.003$ of GARCH(1,1). Four emission variants share the same scaffold and land at four distinct points on the kurtosis axis without retuning $K$ or the transition structure: CHMM-N undershoots, CHMM-L matches observed kurtosis cleanly without a shape parameter, CHMM-t overshoots while supplying per-state heavy tails for tail-conditional consumers, and CHMM-GED nests Gaussian and Laplace as boundary cases ($p_k = 2$ and $p_k = 1$, respectively) and lets the data choose each state's kurtosis adaptively on the continuous shape axis.
The mechanism behind the simultaneous KS / ACF fit is the spectral identity of equation~\eqref{eq:acf_normalised}: at $K \ge 3$ with quantile-initialised Baum-Welch, the absolute-return ACF is a population-level real-coefficient sum of geometric decays at the non-unit eigenvalues of $\mathbf T$.

\paragraph{Replicating the slow-ACF behaviour at $K \ge 3$.}
Two issues compound in the \citet{ryden1998stylized} low-$K$ setup: a single self-transition rate produces a single exponential mode that cannot be simultaneously fast (good marginal) and slow (good ACF), and the random initialisations standard in the 1990s often converge to degenerate local optima with overlapping states. Our $K = 2$ replication on SPY (Appendix~\ref{sec:ryden_replication}) clarifies the mechanism: ACF-MAE is essentially constant at $\approx 0.050$ across quantile and five random-seed initialisations, matching the $K = 18$ CHMM-N. The binding low-$K$ constraint is therefore distributional rather than temporal (KS at $K = 2$ drops to $67$--$72\%$): with modern initialisation the ACF is reproduced at any $K \ge 2$, while distributional fidelity is what actually drives the choice of $K$. The effective-rank diagnostic in Appendix~\ref{sec:spectral_rank} (Table~\ref{tab:spectral_rank}) makes this microscopically explicit: at the fitted $\hat{\mathbf T}$ for $K = 18$, a single non-unit eigenvalue carries $93.6\%$ of the lag-1 absolute-return ACF and the next two modes bring the cumulative share to $95.5\%$; at $K = 3$ the dominant non-unit mode alone carries $96.8\%$. The $K$-rank statement on $\mathbf T - \mathbf 1\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}^\top$ is the algebraic upper bound on the ACF identity~\eqref{eq:acf_normalised}; the empirical effective rank on equity-return data is one to three, whether $K = 3$ or $K = 18$, which is the spectral expression of the temporal axis decoupling from $K$ at the operating points the paper considers.

\paragraph{OoS KS pass rates against the test-power ceiling.}
At $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ the two-sample KS test does not reach $100\%$ on truly-correct generators. A length-matched i.i.d.\ resample of the IS distribution itself passes at $90.0\%$, while the Gaussian i.i.d.\ negative control is rejected at $0\%$, so the test retains ample power to refuse clearly-wrong generators. The CHMM OoS KS pass rates of $79$--$86\%$ sit just below the $90\%$ ceiling (a genuine fidelity claim, not a low-power artefact); GARCH's $58\%$ is correctly rejected. The asymptotic two-sample KS test assumes i.i.d.\ samples, but the simulated paths from CHMM and GARCH carry volatility clustering, so the asymptotic null is mildly lenient; under stationary block-bootstrap recalibration (Appendix~\ref{sec:ks_block_bootstrap}, Table~\ref{tab:ks_block_bootstrap}) the CHMM family retains $88$--$93\%$ pass rate at block length $L = 10$ while GARCH(1,1) drops sharply from $26.8\%$ to $13.6\%$, consistent with the i.i.d.-baseline null being mildly favourable to volatility-clustering generators with the wrong marginal. The block-aware reading reinforces the asymptotic conclusion: CHMM passes the temporal-aware distributional benchmark at the same rate it passes the asymptotic one; GARCH's failure widens.

\paragraph{Closing the kurtosis gap with heavy-tailed emissions.}
CHMM-N produces excess kurtosis $5.02$ against observed $7.68$: a Gaussian mixture's kurtosis is bounded by the variance of component means relative to their variances, and pushing tail states out degrades the central fit. CHMM-t addresses this through per-state Student-$t$ emissions ($\nu_k$ updated by ECM one-dimensional search), at the cost of an IS overshoot to $14.57$. The overshoot is a single-state artefact rather than a lower-bracket pinning: a sweep $\nu_{\min} \in \{2.1, 2.5, 3.0, 4.0\}$ leaves the median $\nu_k$ at $50$ with only $2/18$ states near the lower edge, and an exponential $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage at rate $\lambda = 20$ brings simulated kurtosis to $\approx 8$ at a $1$pp KS cost (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}). CHMM-L sits between CHMM-N and CHMM-t at $6.75$ without any shape parameter, and CHMM-GED's bimodal $\hat p_k$ partition (body Figure~\ref{fig:p_hist}) is the data-driven analog of a hand-classified Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail hybrid. The four-way decision is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:variant_choice}; all four variants' $5$--$95\%$ envelopes bracket observed VaR/ES on both windows.

\paragraph{Per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ for the penalised CHMM-t.}
The body Table~\ref{tab:cross_ticker} reports the operational variant at a uniform $\lambda = 20$. A per-ticker sweep $\lambda \in \{0, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100\}$ on each of the six body tickers (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_robustness}) defines $\hat\lambda^\star_i = \arg\min_\lambda |{\text{kurt}}_{\text{sim},\lambda} - {\text{kurt}}_{\text{obs},i}|$ subject to IS KS degradation $\le 1.5$ percentage points from $\lambda = 0$. The per-ticker optima distribute as: $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{SPY}} = 10$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{NVDA}} = 20$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{JNJ}} = 20$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{JPM}} = 10$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{AAPL}} = 10$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{QQQ}} = 0$. The body uniform choice $\lambda = 20$ is the right rate on the heavy-tailed defensives (NVDA, JNJ) where unpenalised kurtosis blows up by an order of magnitude, but $\lambda = 10$ suffices on the moderate-tail tickers and $\lambda = 0$ is appropriate for QQQ whose unpenalised fit already matches observed kurtosis to within $0.05$ kurtosis units.
We commit to the following operational rule: \emph{set $\hat\lambda^\star_i = 20$ if the residual kurtosis of CHMM-t at $\lambda = 0$ exceeds the observed kurtosis on ticker $i$ by more than one unit; set $\hat\lambda^\star_i = 10$ if the gap is between $0.1$ and $1$ unit; set $\hat\lambda^\star_i = 0$ if the gap is below $0.1$ units}. Under this rule the per-ticker assignment is $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{SPY}} = 10$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{NVDA}} = 20$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{JNJ}} = 20$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{JPM}} = 10$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{AAPL}} = 10$, $\hat\lambda^\star_{\text{QQQ}} = 0$, which matches the per-ticker sweep optima above. The body uniform $\lambda = 20$ is therefore a conservative default that is correct on the two defensives where the unpenalised pinning artefact is severe, slightly heavier than the rule on three moderate-tail tickers (with the $1.5$pp KS / $0.001$ ACF-MAE penalty quantified above), and not the rule's choice on QQQ.

\paragraph{Stationarity scope and operational deployment.}
The CHMM is trained on the assumption that returns are locally stationary across the 10-year IS window. Single-name equity returns are well known to carry sector-specific structural breaks (\citealp{pastorstambaugh2001equity}; \citealp{andreoughysels2002breaks}; surveyed by \citealp{angtimmermann2012regime}), and Cross-ticker generalization (Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate}) reflects this directly: at $K = 18$, OoS distributional fidelity is preserved on SPY, JNJ, AAPL, and QQQ but degrades on NVDA ($57.2\%$) and JPM ($53.0\%$), where the IS-fixed transitions and emissions cannot capture the AI-driven NVDA regime or the rate-repricing cycle for financials. The pattern persists at every $K$ in the sweep, so it is not a model-selection issue. Operational deployment of the CHMM should follow standard practice for time-series generators on financial data, with periodic refit at quarterly or finer cadence (\citealp{pesarantimmermann2007window}; an online-EM formulation is given by \citealp{cappe2011online}). The static IS / OoS panel reported here is the reference comparison, not the operational protocol.

\paragraph{Why the W4 stress fold fails where the headline OoS holds.}
The walk-forward distribution (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, Table~\ref{tab:walkforward}) shows two stress folds with KS pass rates below $10\%$: W2 (2020 COVID) and W4 (2022 rate-hike onset, KS $\le 1\%$). The headline OoS window is the rate-cycle \emph{reversal} of $2024$--$2026$ (KS $\sim 82\%$). The asymmetry between the W4 onset (catastrophic) and the headline OoS reversal (clean) is consistent with two compounding mechanisms. First, the IS window 2014--2019 saw only one mild rate-hike cycle (2015--2018 in $25$-bp steps), so the IS-fitted volatility-regime distribution does not include the magnitude of the 2022 hike (a $525$-bp move over 18 months) and the IS-fixed CHMM has no state with the right $\sigma$ to absorb it. The 2024--2026 window, by contrast, covers the \emph{aftermath} of that hike, which the IS-period regime distribution can partially span (the 2018 December selloff and the 2020 COVID volatility cluster jointly cover the high-$\sigma$ tail of the 2024--2026 distribution). Second, the 2022 hike was structurally a regime \emph{introduction} (a new high-rate, high-real-yield regime that did not exist in the IS window), whereas the 2024--2026 reversal is a regime \emph{attenuation} (a return to a regime closer to the IS volatility level). The IS-fixed transitions and emissions can track an attenuation but not an introduction, which is the operational reading of the asymmetry: walk-forward stress folds at regime introductions are out of scope for the IS-fixed protocol regardless of $K$, while regime attenuations and same-regime-with-different-amplitude OoS slices remain in scope.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
The cross-ticker study covers six tickers; a truncated C-vine variant on the same universe is reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:cvine_supp} and does not improve over the elliptical copulas at this scale, so scaling to larger $d$ via untruncated regular vines or factor copulas is the natural next step. State selection is via information-criterion plus distributional pass-rate rather than held-out log-likelihood; a clean re-selection on a non-OoS validation slice picks $K^\star = 3$ by held-out log-lik (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}), so the $K = 18$ operating point is best read as a multi-metric choice that weights tail fidelity alongside likelihood. Skew-heavy-tailed emissions (skew-$t$ or skew-Laplace) and explicit-duration semi-Markov sojourns are deferred as companion-paper directions; regime-conditional VaR via one-step-ahead latent-state forecast propagation is delivered in Section~\ref{sec:var_backtest} (Table~\ref{tab:cond_var}) and passes all three Kupiec / Christoffersen-ind / Christoffersen-cc tests at every $(K, \alpha)$ on OoS.
