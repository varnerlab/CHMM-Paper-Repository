A continuous HMM trained by Baum-Welch reproduces the three Cont stylized facts simultaneously on SPY at two held-out operating points: at $K^\star = 3$, CHMM-N attains $89.7\%$ IS / $80.1\%$ OoS KS at $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $0.0467$; at $K^\star = 6$ the four emission variants attain $88.3$--$92.6\%$ IS / $76.3$--$79.0\%$ OoS KS, with CHMM-N's simulated kurtosis $5.26$ matching observed OoS $5.29$ within $0.03$. Four emission variants share the same scaffold and land at four distinct kurtosis points without retuning $K$ or the transition structure. The mechanism behind the simultaneous KS / ACF fit is the spectral identity~\eqref{eq:acf_normalised}: at $K \ge 3$ with quantile-initialised Baum-Welch, the absolute-return ACF is a population-level real-coefficient sum of geometric decays at the non-unit eigenvalues of $\mathbf T$.

\paragraph{Replicating the slow-ACF behaviour at $K \ge 3$.}
Two issues compound in the \citet{ryden1998stylized} low-$K$ setup: a single self-transition rate produces a single exponential mode that cannot be simultaneously fast and slow, and the random initialisations standard in the 1990s often converge to degenerate local optima. Our $K = 2$ replication on SPY (Appendix~\ref{sec:ryden_replication}) clarifies the mechanism: ACF-MAE is essentially constant at $\approx 0.050$ across quantile and five random-seed initialisations, matching the $K = 18$ CHMM-N. The binding low-$K$ constraint is therefore distributional (KS at $K = 2$ drops to $67$--$72\%$): with modern initialisation the ACF is reproduced at any $K \ge 2$, while distributional fidelity drives the choice of $K$. The effective-rank diagnostic in Appendix~\ref{sec:spectral_rank} makes this microscopically explicit: at the fitted $\hat{\mathbf T}$ for $K = 18$, a single non-unit eigenvalue carries $93.6\%$ of the lag-1 absolute-return ACF, and at $K = 3$ the dominant non-unit mode alone carries $96.8\%$.

\paragraph{Closing the kurtosis gap with heavy-tailed emissions.}
CHMM-N produces excess kurtosis $5.02$ against observed $7.68$: a Gaussian mixture's kurtosis is bounded by the variance of component means relative to their variances. CHMM-t addresses this through per-state Student-$t$ emissions but at the cost of an aggregate-mean IS overshoot to $14.57$ from a single-state ECM lower-bracket pinning artefact (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}); an exponential $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage at rate $\lambda = 20$ brings the aggregate-mean simulated kurtosis to $\approx 8$ at a $1$pp KS cost (the per-path \emph{median} simulated IS kurtosis is centred near observed at every operating point even before the penalty; see Appendix~\ref{sec:kurtosis_ci_placement}). A shared-$\nu$ ablation (a single $\nu$ shared across all $K$ states under aggregate-$Q$ ECM, the standard one-parameter Student-$t$ HMM in the time-series literature) eliminates the aggregate-mean overshoot completely without any penalty: simulated IS kurtosis is $4.68$ at $K^\star = 3$, $9.38$ at $K^\star = 6$, and $6.25$ at $K = 18$, with the $K = 18$ row producing the cleanest IS / OoS heavy-tail match in the entire panel ($6.25$ IS / $5.00$ OoS against observed $7.68$ / $5.29$, Appendix~\ref{sec:chmm_t_shared_nu}, Table~\ref{tab:chmm_t_shared_nu}). The per-state $\nu_k$ design is therefore the binding constraint on the aggregate-mean overshoot; the body retains the per-state $\nu_k$ + $\lambda = 20$ construction for direct comparability with \citet{peel2000robust, liu1995ml} but the shared-$\nu$ row is the structurally-cleaner alternative for kurtosis-fidelity-priority consumers. The $\lambda = 20$ choice is validated on a strictly pre-2020 slice (estimation 2014-01-03 to 2018-06-29) by validation log-likelihood on 2018-07-02 to 2019-12-31, which yields $\lambda^\star = 20$ on the grid $\{0, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100, 200\}$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:lambda_cv_pre2020}). The penalty is validated at the SPY level but per-ticker (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, Table~\ref{tab:per_ticker_lambda}) the body uniform $\lambda = 20$ is over-shrunk on three of six tickers; the production recipe is the per-ticker validation rule of Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate}. The bracket-lift remark below documents the structural-cleanness alternative.

\begin{remark}[Bracket-lift ablation at $\nu_{\min} = 4$]
\label{rem:bracket_lift}
The body $\nu_{\min} = 2.1$ bracket lies below the kurtosis-finiteness threshold $\nu = 4$, so the lower-bracket pinning that produces the IS-kurtosis overshoot is partly an artefact of the bracket itself. A direct $\nu_{\min} = 4$ ablation (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, $\dagger\dagger$ row) brings simulated IS kurtosis from $14.35$ to $13.25$ ($\sim 1$ unit), still well above the bootstrap CI of observed ($7.68$ IS / $5.29$ OoS), with IS / OoS KS unchanged within sampling error ($95.5 / 85.0\%$ vs.\ $95.6 / 85.7\%$). The bracket lift alone is therefore not a substitute for the $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage; the kurtosis overshoot is a multi-state pinning effect that the penalty addresses directly and the bracket only partially mitigates. We report the penalised construction in the body for direct comparability with \citet{peel2000robust, liu1995ml} and the bracket-lift row alongside as the structurally-cleaner alternative.
\end{remark} CHMM-L sits between CHMM-N and CHMM-t at $6.75$ without any shape parameter; CHMM-GED's bimodal $\hat p_k$ partition (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_p_partition}) is the data-driven analog of a hand-classified Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail hybrid. The four-way variant decision is summarised in Table~\ref{tab:variant_choice}; bootstrap CIs on observed kurtosis (Appendix~\ref{sec:kurtosis_bootstrap_ci}) show that the IS-OoS difference of $\sim 2.4$ units is not statistically distinguishable at conventional levels, so the variant rankings should be read as point-comparisons inside a wide CI envelope.

\paragraph{Practical identifiability and the effective state count at $K = 18$.}
The $K = 18$ operating point is partly nominal: the standardized-distance single-linkage diagnostic of Appendix~\ref{sec:state_distinctness} collapses CHMM-N to $11/18$ effective states and CHMM-t to $12/18$, with $13/18$ CHMM-t states pinned at the upper $\nu$ bracket on SPY. Three implications follow. (i) The numerical full-rank check on $\hat{\mathbf T}$ in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_propositions}, which reports $\mathrm{rank}(\hat{\mathbf T} - \mathbf 1\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}^\top) = K - 1 = 17$ at tolerance $10^{-10}$, exhausts only algebraic rank; the practically identifiable rank is closer to $K_{\text{eff}} - 1 \approx 10$. (ii) The AIC / BIC parameter count at $K = 18$ over-credits the model; an information-criterion penalty computed against $K_{\text{eff}}$ rather than the nominal $K$ would re-rank $K^\star \in \{6, 12\}$ in the held-out panel (Section~\ref{sec:k_selection_results}, Table~\ref{tab:k_selection}) more consistently with each other. (iii) The headline-metrics interpretation is not affected: simulation under the full $K = 18$ mixture and the metric panel are computed on whatever the converged model is; the question is whether the parameter count claimed against the data is honest at $K_{\text{eff}}$ or at $K_{\text{nom}}$. We read this as a representational-economy observation: collapsed clusters carry small stationary-mass tails and contribute consistently to the simulation, but the model is best described as a $K_{\text{eff}}$-state model expressed in a $K_{\text{nom}}$-state parameterisation. A direct $K_{\text{nominal}} = 11$ rebuild (matching $K_{\text{eff}}$ at $K = 18$) confirms this reading: at $K = 11$ on SPY, CHMM-N attains IS KS $94.4\%$ / OoS KS $82.8\%$ / IS sim kurt $4.52$ / $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $0.0505$, matching or slightly exceeding the $K = 18$ nominal row ($94.0\%$ / $81.0\%$ / $5.04$ / $0.0514$) on every axis (Appendix~\ref{sec:k_eff_rebuild}, Table~\ref{tab:k_eff_rebuild}). The $K = 18$ over-parameterisation is therefore a parameter-counting artefact rather than an information-bearing extra resolution; $K_{\text{nominal}} = 11$ is the structurally cleaner expression of the same model. The $K^\star = 6$ sensitivity reference (Section~\ref{sec:k_selection_results}) is closer to the practically identifiable resolution and should be the default when reporting parameter counts; the $K = 18$ extended-state-resolution row is the right operating point when kurtosis fidelity is the priority, and reports against $K_{\text{nominal}} = 11$ ($K_{\text{eff}}$) for IC-penalised diagnostics.

\paragraph{Stationarity scope and operational deployment.}
The CHMM is trained on the assumption that returns are locally stationary across the IS window. Single-name equity returns are well known to carry sector-specific structural breaks (\citealp{pastorstambaugh2001equity}; \citealp{andreoughysels2002breaks}; surveyed by \citealp{angtimmermann2012regime}); the cross-ticker generalisation in Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate} reflects this directly, with $11/30$ tickers below $60\%$ OoS KS, concentrated in single-name regime introductions. The mitigation is periodic refit (\citealp{pesarantimmermann2007window}; an online-EM formulation by \citealp{cappe2011online}): a quarterly-refit version of the cross-ticker panel (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_ticker_quarterly_refit}) shifts the OoS KS distribution from median $73.4\% \to 83.0\%$ and reduces failures from $11/30$ to $7/30$. The walk-forward W2 (COVID) and W4 (2022 rate-hike onset) stress folds remain out-of-distribution by KS for every generator under any refit cadence (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}); these are regime introductions the IS distribution does not span. Discussion of the asymmetry between the W4 onset (catastrophic) and the headline 2024-2026 reversal (clean), the OoS regime trajectory under IS-fixed parameters, the per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ for the penalised CHMM-t, and the IS / OoS kurtosis CI overlap are in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, \ref{sec:oos_regime_trajectory}, and \ref{sec:kurtosis_bootstrap_ci}.

\paragraph{Stylized-fact scope: simulated leverage envelope brackets the IS observed value, not the OoS observed value.}
The Cont (2001) stylized-fact list also includes the leverage effect (negative $\mathrm{Corr}(G_t, |G_{t+1}|)$ on equity returns) and gain-loss asymmetry; the present paper's body evaluation covers only the three symmetric stylized facts (heavy-tailed marginals, negligible linear ACF, slow $|G_t|$ ACF). All four CHMM emission families in this paper carry symmetric per-state densities (Gaussian, Laplace, Student-$t$, GED at any $p_k$), so we initially expected the CHMM to fail the leverage axis by construction. We compute $\mathrm{Corr}(G_t, |G_{t+1}|)$ on observed SPY (IS $-0.135$, OoS $-0.214$) and on the per-path simulation distribution from each generator at $N_{\text{paths}} = 500$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:leverage_effect}). At $K = 18$ the CHMM-N path-level median is $-0.089$ on IS and $-0.087$ on OoS, with $[Q_5, Q_{95}]$ envelopes $[-0.138, -0.037]$ IS and $[-0.190, +0.015]$ OoS. Reading those quantiles as one-sided percentile p-values against the simulated null: \textbf{the IS observed value $-0.135$ sits just inside the simulated $Q_5$ envelope (one-sided $p \approx 0.05$, borderline non-rejection)}, and \textbf{the OoS observed value $-0.214$ sits below the simulated $Q_5$ floor of $-0.190$ (one-sided $p < 0.05$, rejection of the simulated null at the $5\%$ level)}. The substantive read is therefore: CHMM at $K = 18$ produces a simulated leverage signal that is borderline-non-rejected on IS at conventional levels and rejected on OoS, rather than ``partially captured'' on both windows. The mechanism behind the non-zero IS leverage is that the converged $\hat{\mathbf T}$ couples high-$\sigma_k$ states to negative-$\mu_k$ states asymmetrically, so the Markov mixing produces a non-trivial signed signal even though each per-state emission is symmetric; this mechanism explains why the IS gap is small while the OoS gap remains. The CHMM-N envelope is competitive with EGARCH (median $-0.095$, $[Q_5, Q_{95}] = [-0.147, -0.047]$ IS) and tighter than GARCH(1,1) Gaussian (median $-0.004$, indistinguishable from zero); the asymmetric GARCH baselines (EGARCH, GJR-GARCH) produce slightly stronger leverage in the median but their envelopes also fail to cover the OoS observed value at the $5\%$ level. Skew-emission extensions (skew-$t$ in the tradition of \citealp{azzalini2003distributions}; skew-Laplace; skew-GED) remain the natural fix for closing the OoS gap and are deferred as companion-paper directions. Closing the OoS gap requires asymmetric per-state emissions; this paper does not close it.

\paragraph{Baseline-implementation caveats.}
Two rows of our extended baseline panel (Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines}) are repo-native re-implementations rather than reference-implementation runs and should be read with that caveat. (i) The QuantGAN row (Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_supp}) is a three-conv-layer convolutional WGAN that is materially smaller than the seven-block TCN of \citet{wiese2020quantgan} and lacks the Lambert-W input pre-processing that the original authors credit with most of the tail-fidelity. The $0\%$ IS / OoS KS pass rate is the failure mode of \emph{this} in-house implementation; the body Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} also reports the seven-block TCN rebuild at the same $0\%$ KS, and the Lambert-W variant of Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_supp} likewise fails. We do not claim that a faithful reference-implementation re-run of \citet{wiese2020quantgan} would also fail; that is a deferred follow-up. The QuantGAN rows in this paper are in-house WGAN re-implementation results, not a verdict on the WGAN literature or the deep-generative class. (ii) The SM-CHMM row of Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines} is fit by a Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in estimator rather than by full ML on the explicit-duration HSMM; the ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ row of Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} is the proper HSMM benchmark and wins the OoS KS column. We do not claim that an ML-estimated HSMM at higher $K$ would underperform CHMM at the same $K$, only that the present comparison is at each method's best operating point. The MS-GARCH baseline is reported in two flavours: the in-house Nelder-Mead frequentist fit (Table~\ref{tab:extended_baselines} and the in-house rows of Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, $27$--$37\%$ IS KS plateau) and a reference Bayesian re-run via the \texttt{MSGARCH} R package of \citet{ardia2019msgarch} (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} ¶ rows and Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines}, $0$--$0.1\%$ IS / $5$--$6\%$ OoS KS at posterior-predictive simulation); both fall well short of the CHMM family on this dataset, so the body conclusion that ``the multi-state benefit is specific to the CHMM scaffold rather than to multi-state regime-switching per se'' is robust to estimator choice. A faithful Wiese et al.\ TCN re-run with Lambert-W pre-processing is the right next-step control on the deep-generative axis; we report the present panel honestly with the caveats above.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
Skew-heavy-tailed emissions (skew-$t$ or skew-Laplace) and explicit-duration semi-Markov sojourns at higher $K$ are deferred as companion-paper directions; the leverage-effect diagnostic (Appendix~\ref{sec:leverage_effect}) shows the residual gap to OoS observed leverage will require asymmetric per-state emissions to close. Scaling the multi-asset construction to larger $d$ via untruncated regular vines or factor copulas is the natural follow-up. The state-selection rule for the $K = 18$ extended-state-resolution panel uses information-criterion plus distributional pass-rate rather than strictly held-out log-likelihood; held-out re-selection picks $K^\star = 3$ or $K^\star = 6$ depending on the validation slice (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}), and the body $K^\star = 3$ block is the state-resolution-robust default for synthetic-data consumers. The cross-decade validation result reported in \S\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate} (\S\ref{sec:cross_decade_validation} for the full panel) is the same pattern at the decade scale: the IS axis is decade-robust within $\sim 5$pp on KS, while the calm 2004--2006 OoS slice collapses to $3$--$5\%$ pass rate, mirroring the W2 / W4 walk-forward stress folds rather than the headline 2024--2026 OoS slice. The empirical scope is daily US-equity stylized-fact reproduction; FX, fixed-income, and commodity asset classes are not covered, and the GLD / SLV non-equity stress test in Appendix~\ref{sec:non_us_asset_supp} documents that the static IS-fitted CHMM does not transfer to those windows without periodic refit. The cross-asset dependence-family selection (Section~\ref{sec:cross_asset}) is an IS-calibration distinction: on the OoS window the Gaussian and Student-$t$ copulas are statistically indistinguishable at $N_{\text{paths}} = 200$. We have addressed six of the deferred robustness items raised in pre-submission peer review: the OoS-anchored block-bootstrap KS recalibration (Appendix~\ref{sec:ks_block_bootstrap_oos}) confirms the IS CHMM-vs-GARCH ordering survives on OoS; the leverage-effect diagnostic (Appendix~\ref{sec:leverage_effect}) shows the CHMM family at $K = 18$ borderline-non-rejects the IS observed leverage at the $5\%$ level via Markov state-mixing despite symmetric per-state emissions, but rejects the OoS observed leverage at the same level (the OoS gap requires asymmetric per-state emissions to close); the full one-shot Student-$t$ copula MLE (Appendix~\ref{sec:full_tcopula_mle}) shows the body two-step Kendall's-$\tau$ + profile-MLE construction is not biased toward the Gaussian limit on the six-asset universe; the CRSP cross-decade validation (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_decade_validation}) extends the empirical scope to 1994-2006 via a day-pass WRDS query and shows that the IS axis of the stylized-fact reproduction is decade-robust (84-90\% IS KS at K $\in \{3, 18\}$ on the 1994-2004 slice) while the 2004-2006 OoS slice exhibits the same low-stress / low-kurtosis pattern as the W2 / W4 walk-forward stress folds; the QuantGAN reference TCN rebuild (Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_tcn}) reports a Wiese-style dilated TCN architecture as a stronger deep-generative comparator than the body's 3-conv-layer baseline; and the reference-implementation MS-GARCH re-run via the \texttt{MSGARCH} R package of \citet{ardia2019msgarch} (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} ¶ rows and Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines}) confirms the multi-state-benefit plateau under Bayesian estimation, with the IS KS pass-rate falling to $0$--$0.1\%$ under posterior-predictive simulation that integrates parameter uncertainty path-by-path.

\paragraph{Deferred follow-ups (closed and remaining).}
Two of the four follow-ups raised at pre-submission peer review are closed in this revision; the other two are deferred to companion work and acknowledged honestly so that the body claim of this paper is not inadvertently extended to scopes that were not exercised. \emph{(Closed) MS-GARCH regime-conditional VaR.} The regime-conditional VaR construction of Eq.~\eqref{eq:filter} is applied to the IS-fitted MS-GARCH(1,1)-Gaussian reference models of \citet{ardia2019msgarch} at $K \in \{2, 3, 4\}$ via the same Haas et al.\ (2004) state-filter pipeline (Appendix~\ref{sec:msgarch_conditional_var}, Table~\ref{tab:msgarch_cond_var}). At $\alpha = 0.05$ all three MS-GARCH operating points pass Christoffersen-cc and DQ cleanly; at $\alpha = 0.01$ MS-GARCH-2 / MS-GARCH-3 are borderline-rejected by Christoffersen-cc and rejected by DQ, while MS-GARCH-4 passes both at $\alpha = 0.01$ cleanly. The conditional-coverage value proposition is therefore not CHMM-specific (\S\ref{par:msgarch_cond_var}); the CHMM-vs-MS-GARCH choice is dominated by the marginal-distribution column of Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, not the conditional-coverage column. \emph{(Closed) Daily-refit / online-EM cond VaR on W2 / W4.} A daily-refit ($\equiv$ online-EM at the cadence limit) variant of the body conditional-VaR construction (Appendix~\ref{sec:walkforward_online_em}, Table~\ref{tab:walkforward_online_em}) is run on the W2 (COVID 2020) and W4 (2022 rate-hike onset) stress folds at $K^\star = 3$. Daily refit makes W2 strictly worse than the fold-IS-fixed baseline (Christoffersen-cc and DQ both reject at $p < 10^{-3}$ at $\alpha = 0.01$); the rolling-window train picks up rising COVID volatility through early March 2020 and tightens the VaR threshold, producing more breaches in the catastrophic days, not fewer. The body's "intrinsic regime-break" reading of W2 is therefore strengthened, not qualified, at the cadence boundary; a stochastic-recursion online-EM in the \citet{cappe2011online} sense cannot be more responsive than daily batch EM and is unlikely to close W2 by adaptive parameter tracking alone. \emph{(Remaining) Faithful reference-implementation QuantGAN re-run.} The body QuantGAN row (Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_supp} and Appendix~\ref{sec:quantgan_tcn}) is an in-house WGAN re-implementation that fails at $0\%$ IS / OoS KS in both the 3-conv and 7-block TCN variants, with and without Lambert-W input pre-processing; we do not claim that a re-run of the published Wiese et al.\ (2020) implementation would also fail. A faithful reference run requires the published PyTorch code with the original training schedule, hyperparameters, and Lambert-W preprocessing, and is the right next control on the deep-generative axis. \emph{(Remaining) Post-QuantGAN deep-generative baselines.} The deep-generative axis in this paper is exercised through QuantGAN only; time-series diffusion models on the \citet{rasul2021autoregressive} line, normalising-flow generators, and recent regime-switching deep models would extend the benchmark and are not run here. The deep-generative class is therefore represented in the body panel by one architecture from one 2020 paper; consumers who need a stronger deep-generative comparator should treat the body's deep-generative row as a placeholder pending these follow-ups.
