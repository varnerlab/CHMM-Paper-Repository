Synthetic generators of equity-return time series support stress testing, regulatory backtesting, scenario design, and the training of machine-learning models on data that cannot be released for privacy or licensing reasons~\cite{jordon2022synthetic, assefa2020generating, alswaidan2026hybrid}. A useful generator has to reproduce, at the same time, the standard stylized facts of daily returns: heavy-tailed marginals, negligible linear autocorrelation, and slow decay of the autocorrelation function (ACF) of absolute returns~\cite{mandelbrot1963variation, cont2001empirical}. Generalised autoregressive conditional heteroskedasticity (GARCH) models~\cite{engle1982autoregressive, bollerslev1986generalized} capture volatility clustering but not the multi-regime structure of the marginal; i.i.d.\ generators match the marginal but lose temporal persistence; deep generators~\cite{yoon2019timegan, wiese2020quantgan, rasul2021autoregressive} reproduce some stylized facts at the cost of interpretability and reproducibility. One negative result has shaped two decades of work on regime-switching models. \citet{ryden1998stylized} fitted Gaussian-emission hidden Markov models (HMMs) at low state counts and concluded that the geometric sojourn-time distribution of a standard Markov chain makes the ACF decay too fast to match the observed slow decay; \citet{bulla2006stylized} recovered the ACF fit by replacing the Markov chain with a hidden semi-Markov chain, at the cost of much greater complexity. The Ryd\'{e}n et al.\ failure combines two constraints: a temporal one (the absolute-return ACF must allow slow-decay modes) and a distributional one (the marginal mixture must carry enough components to match observed kurtosis). A standard closed-form identity for the absolute-return ACF, written as a sum over the non-unit eigenvalues of the transition matrix~\cite{hamilton1994time, krolzig1997markov, timmermann2000moments}, bounds the number of decay modes by the rank of the centred transition matrix. We ask whether that bound actually matters on equity-return data once enough latent states are allowed.

We answer this with a continuous hidden Markov model (CHMM): a hidden chain of market regimes in which each regime emits the day's return from its own probability distribution. The regime chain supplies the slow decay in the absolute-return ACF through its sojourn times, while the per-regime distributions supply the heavy-tailed marginal, so the two constraints that defeated the low-state Gaussian fit are met by different parts of the model. Because the Ryd\'{e}n failure is as much about the marginal as the chain, we do not commit to one shape for the per-regime distribution. We run the model with a Gaussian baseline and three heavy-tailed alternatives (Student-$t$, Laplace, and the generalised error distribution), all fitted by the same estimator so that the only thing changing across the four is the per-regime density. On the fitted model we then build a regime-conditional Value-at-Risk (VaR) that forecasts tail risk from the model's running estimate of which regime the day belongs to, and a multi-asset version that couples several single-asset CHMMs into one joint generator while leaving each asset's fitted distribution untouched.

In this study we put the model through four independent tests: a six-fold rolling-origin walk-forward on a decade of SPY, a sector-balanced 30-ticker panel across ten Global Industry Classification Standard (GICS) sectors, a Center for Research in Security Prices (CRSP) cross-decade transfer from a ten-year window to a later two-year slice, and a six-asset US-equity basket, which together guard against single-window over-fitting. With enough latent states and a heavy-tailed emission, the model reproduced all three symmetric Cont stylized facts and narrowed the kurtosis gap left by the Gaussian baseline. The rank bound from the ACF identity did not bind at the typical ticker, though it still mattered for a few names that introduced a new regime out of sample. The regime-conditional VaR passed the Christoffersen joint conditional-coverage test~\citep{christoffersen1998evaluating} cleanly on the main window and on most walk-forward folds, and the multi-asset version reproduced cross-asset correlations far better than a single-index alternative. We read this to mean that the Ryd\'{e}n low-state-count failure is a limit on the marginal distribution, not on the chain's temporal structure, once the fit is initialised well and the emissions can carry heavy tails. An i.i.d.\ bootstrap and a maximum-likelihood hidden semi-Markov model (HSMM) beat the CHMM on raw single-window fit, but neither delivers a regime-conditional risk forecast, a multi-asset coupling, or a privacy-preserving synthetic series, which are what the CHMM is for. The scope is daily US equities: a non-equity stress test on the GLD exchange-traded fund (ETF) broke down under static fitting, and we recommend periodic refit within that scope.
