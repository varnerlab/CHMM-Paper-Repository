Synthetic generators of equity-return time series support stress testing, regulatory backtesting, scenario design, and the training of downstream machine-learning models on data that cannot be released for privacy or licensing reasons \cite{jordon2022synthetic, assefa2020generating, alswaidan2026hybrid}. The bar for a useful generator is that it reproduce, simultaneously, the canonical stylized facts of daily returns: heavy-tailed marginals, negligible linear autocorrelation, and slow decay of the autocorrelation function (ACF) of absolute returns \cite{mandelbrot1963variation, cont2001empirical}. GARCH models \cite{engle1982autoregressive, bollerslev1986generalized} capture volatility clustering but not the multi-regime structure of the marginal; i.i.d.\ generators match the marginal but destroy temporal persistence; deep generators \cite{yoon2019timegan, wiese2020quantgan, rasul2021autoregressive} reproduce some stylized facts at the cost of interpretability and reproducibility.

An influential negative result has shaped two decades of work on the regime-switching route. \citet{ryden1998stylized} fitted Gaussian-emission HMMs at $K = 2$--$3$ states and concluded that the geometric sojourn-time distribution of a standard Markov chain produces exponential ACF decay too fast to match the observed slow decay; \citet{bulla2006stylized} restored ACF fidelity by replacing the Markov chain with a hidden semi-Markov chain at the cost of substantially increased complexity.

We identify the spectral root cause. The absolute-return autocovariance of a stationary continuous HMM admits a closed-form mixture-of-eigenvalues identity $\rho_{|G|}(\tau) = \sum_{k \ge 2} w_k \lambda_k^{\tau}$ over the non-unit eigenvalues of $\mathbf T$ (Section~\ref{sec:theory}); the empirical Ryd\'{e}n et al.\ result is then a $K$-rank statement on $\mathbf T$. At $K = 2$ the ACF is mono-exponential and matching slow decay forces $|\lambda_2| \to 1$, which collapses the marginal mixture and fails distributional Kolmogorov--Smirnov; at $K \ge 3$ the constraints decouple. The semi-Markov route injects the missing temporal flexibility through an explicit sojourn distribution; moderate-$K$ continuous HMM achieves the same end through the spectral richness of $\mathbf T$, inside the standard Baum-Welch scaffold.

We instantiate this on ten years of daily SPDR S\&P~500 ETF (SPY) data with a $2.5$-year held-out window, using a continuous HMM trained by Baum-Welch \cite{baum1970maximization, rabiner1986introduction} under quantile-based initialization, in three emission variants sharing identical forward-backward recursions: CHMM-N with Gaussian emissions, CHMM-t with per-state Student-t emissions whose degrees of freedom $\nu_k$ are updated inside the M-step by a golden-section search on the ECM $Q$-function in the tradition of \citet{peel2000robust} and \citet{liu1995ml}, and CHMM-L with Laplace emissions whose location is the weighted median and whose scale is the weighted mean absolute deviation, both in closed form. Against a benchmark panel covering the i.i.d.\ bootstrap (\citealp{politis1994stationary}), Gaussian and Laplace i.i.d., GARCH(1,1) (\citealp{bollerslev1986generalized}), heavy-tailed GARCH(1,1)-$t$ (\citealp{bollerslev1987conditionally}), and the regime-switching MS-GARCH foil of \citet{haas2004new}, the CHMM at moderate $K$ attains $93.8\%$ in-sample and $84.2\%$ out-of-sample Kolmogorov--Smirnov pass rates and an absolute-return ACF mean absolute error of $0.0507$, essentially matching GARCH's temporal fidelity without sacrificing distributional fit. The three emission variants land at three distinct points on the kurtosis axis: CHMM-N undershoots ($5.0$ simulated vs.\ $7.7$ observed), CHMM-t overshoots ($14.6$), and CHMM-L matches cleanly ($6.8$). The unconditional Value-at-Risk and Expected-Shortfall envelopes bracket observed historical values at both $1\%$ and $5\%$ on the in-sample and out-of-sample windows. No single benchmark dominates every metric; the i.i.d.\ bootstrap is the strongest distributional baseline, GARCH is the strongest unconditional volatility-clustering baseline, and the CHMM is the strongest joint-fit row at moderate state resolution. Multi-asset synthesis composes cleanly through a rank-based Student-t copula on top of the CHMM marginals (profile MLE selects $\nu^\star = 6$; six-asset off-diagonal correlation MAE $0.027$). Returns are assumed locally stationary across the ten-year in-sample window; substantial out-of-sample degradation on individual single names (NVDA, JPM) reflects the well-known stationarity-scope limit on financial time series \cite{pastorstambaugh2001equity, andreoughysels2002breaks, angtimmermann2012regime} and motivates periodic refit at deployment in the spirit of \citet{pesarantimmermann2007window}.

Our contributions are: (i) a closed-form mixture-of-eigenvalues identity for the absolute-return ACF that recasts the Ryd\'{e}n low-$K$ failure as a $K$-rank statement on $\mathbf T$; (ii) three emission families (Gaussian, Student-t, Laplace) under a unified ECM scaffold, where the M-step is the only architectural difference; (iii) a single-window IS / OoS empirical study on SPY plus a six-ticker generalisation panel and a six-asset Student-t copula extension; (iv) a companion Julia package \texttt{CHMM-Model.jl} that ships as a co-equal deliverable, with public API and reproducer entry point in Appendix~\ref{sec:supplementary} and full reproduction of every table and figure from a single seed root.
