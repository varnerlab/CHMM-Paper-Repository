\subsection{Descriptive Statistics and Stylized Facts}
\label{sec:descriptive}

All three Cont stylized facts are present in both windows (Figure~\ref{fig:stylized_facts} in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_stylized_facts}): heavy-tailed marginal (IS excess kurtosis $7.68$, Jarque--Bera $6{,}416.6$, $p < 0.001$), negligible linear autocorrelation in raw $G_t$ (Ljung--Box $\text{LB}_G \approx 5.5$ at lag $20$ vs.\ $\chi^2_{20}(0.05) = 31.4$), and persistent volatility clustering in $|G_t|$ ($\text{LB}_{|G|} = 2{,}959.3$, $p < 0.001$). The OoS window shares the same qualitative profile (excess kurtosis $5.29$). A stationary block bootstrap of \citet{politis1994stationary} at mean block length $L = 20$ gives $95\%$ CIs of $[2.17, 12.40]$ on the IS excess kurtosis and $[0.90, 8.26]$ on the OoS excess kurtosis (Appendix~\ref{sec:kurtosis_bootstrap_ci}, Table~\ref{tab:kurtosis_bootstrap}); the IS and OoS CIs overlap broadly and the IS-vs-OoS kurtosis difference of $\sim 2.4$ units lies within sampling error at this block length, so kurtosis-fidelity targets reported throughout this paper should be read against the joint $[0.90, 12.40]$ envelope rather than against the $7.68 / 5.29$ point estimates.

\subsection{State-Count Selection}
\label{sec:k_selection_results}

With $K - 1 = 2$ non-unit eigenvalues at $K^\star = 3$, the mixture-of-eigenvalues sum~\eqref{eq:acf_normalised} reproduces the slow absolute-return ACF that the \citet{ryden1998stylized} $K = 2$--$3$ Gaussian setup could not recover on the SPY 2014--2024 instance of that experiment (Appendix~\ref{sec:ryden_replication}). On the OoS sample-CRPS, the four CHMM variants are statistically indistinguishable on two-sided \citet{diebold1995comparing} tests at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:dm_bandwidth}); the family choice is therefore driven by the per-row kurtosis match (Table~\ref{tab:variant_choice}, \S\ref{sec:discussion}). The MS-GARCH benchmark plateaus at $34$--$36\%$ IS KS in our Nelder-Mead fit and at $0$--$0.1\%$ IS / $5$--$6\%$ OoS KS in the reference Bayesian re-run (Appendix~\ref{sec:extended_baselines}, Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} \textparagraph rows), so the multi-state benefit is specific to the CHMM scaffold rather than to multi-state regime-switching per se on this dataset under either estimator. A six-fold rolling-origin walk-forward (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, Table~\ref{tab:walkforward}; body summary: Table~\ref{tab:walkforward_body}) attains median OoS KS $62.1\%$ at $K^\star = 3$; the single-window OoS sits at the upper end of this distribution, with two stress folds (W2 COVID, W4 2022 rate-hike onset) below $10\%$ that every generator in the panel rejects. The walk-forward median, not the single-window OoS pair, is the operationally informative summary for production deployment: the headline single OoS window of $2024$--$2026$ is a relatively low-stress slice and the single-window numbers therefore overstate what a deployed CHMM achieves on average across rolling windows.

\paragraph{ML HSMM as a co-headline result.}
Replacing the geometric sojourn distribution with an explicit truncated Pareto and refitting through the explicit-duration forward-backward of \citet{yu2010hidden} gives ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ at $98.4\%$ IS / $\mathbf{91.0\%}$ OoS KS, the strongest single-window OoS KS row in the panel and beating every CHMM operating point. CHMM and ML HSMM are best read as complementary scaffolds on the same Markov backbone, with KS / ACF / kurtosis trade-offs that select between them by use case. The HSMM advantage on KS is offset by an absolute-return ACF-MAE that matches the i.i.d.\ baseline level ($0.0629$) rather than CHMM-N's $0.0460$, because the fitted Pareto sojourn concentrates probability mass on a single low-volatility state and the regime-driven slow-ACF mechanism that the CHMM exploits at $K \ge 3$ is muted (per-state $\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}$ concentration in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_hsmm_diagnostic}). Higher-$K$ HSMM variants (Pareto collapse at $K = 18$; discrete-Gamma-sojourn fix achieving $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $\mathbf{0.0462}$ on IS at $K = 18$) are reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_hsmm_diagnostic} and \ref{sec:hsmm_gamma_sojourn}. Readers selecting a synthetic-data generator on raw OoS KS alone should prefer ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$; CHMM at $K^\star = 3$ is the right choice when the structural use cases of \S\ref{sec:var_backtest}--\ref{sec:cross_asset} are in scope.

\paragraph{Bootstrap as a non-parametric synthetic-data benchmark.}
The stationary block bootstrap of \citet{politis1994stationary} attains $99.7\%$ IS / $92.1\%$ OoS KS in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, comparable to ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ ($91.0\%$ OoS) and above every CHMM operating point. The 1-day OoS KS column is therefore the wrong column to read for CHMM-vs-bootstrap differentiation. The use-case differentiation is not ``CHMM beats bootstrap on KS'' but rather ``CHMM offers parametric controls and structural use cases that the bootstrap cannot serve'': (i) the regime-conditional Value-at-Risk of \S\ref{sec:var_backtest}, which propagates the one-step-ahead state forecast through the predictive mixture, has no bootstrap analogue since the bootstrap is exchangeable and admits no notion of a forward state filter; (ii) the multi-asset copula composition of \S\ref{sec:cross_asset} requires per-asset parametric marginals and is incompatible with a non-parametric bootstrap that only re-emits IS samples; (iii) on the privacy / licensing axis, every bootstrap path is a permutation of literal IS observations, so the bootstrap is unusable wherever the IS sample is restricted. The body ordering is therefore: bootstrap for raw distributional fidelity; ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ when KS is the only objective; CHMM at $K^\star = 3$ when one of the three structural use cases above is in scope. A multi-day cumulative-return CRPS DM analysis (Appendix~\ref{sec:crps_dm_multiday}, \ref{sec:crps_dm_multiday_replication}) does not produce a robust CHMM advantage at the body operating point or across the six-asset universe; the substantive CHMM-vs-bootstrap differentiation is on the structural use cases, not on multi-day forecast horizons.

\paragraph{Block-aware OoS KS recalibration.}
The asymptotic two-sample KS critical value $1.36\sqrt{2/T_{\text{OoS}}} = 0.0804$ used in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} assumes i.i.d.\ samples; under autocorrelated daily returns the appropriate null is the stationary block-bootstrap KS distribution of \citet{politis1994stationary}. We report the block-aware OoS KS pass rate at mean block length $L = 20$ alongside the asymptotic value for the body operating point (Table~\ref{tab:ks_block_body}; full $L \in \{5, 10, 20\}$ panel, OoS-anchored critical value $0.0647$, and $K = 6$ / $K = 18$ sensitivity rows in Appendix~\ref{sec:ks_block_bootstrap}). The cross-generator ordering is preserved (the iid bootstrap remains the non-parametric ceiling, GARCH(1,1) the floor); the absolute level drops by roughly $20$--$30$pp across all rows on OoS, materially larger than the IS recalibration drop of $\sim 5$pp. Read together with Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, the OoS panel under a temporally-aware null moves CHMM-N from a ``passes most of the time'' generator to a ``passes about half the time'' generator at $L = 20$. The body OoS KS headline at the asymptotic critical value therefore overstates the absolute level relative to a temporally-aware null; the cross-generator ranking is the robust comparison.

\paragraph{Cross-decade validation.}
A CRSP-sourced cross-decade split (1994--2004 IS / 2004--2006 OoS; full panel in Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_decade_validation}) tests whether the body's stylized-fact reproduction transfers across non-overlapping decades. The IS axis is decade-robust: at $K^\star = 3$ CHMM-N attains $84$--$90\%$ IS KS pass rate against the $90$--$94\%$ obtained on the body 2014--2024 IS window (full $K \in \{3, 18\}$ panel in Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_decade_validation}). The 2004--2006 OoS pass rate, however, collapses to $3$--$5\%$ because the calm post-dot-com bull-market slice has observed excess kurtosis $0.06$ (essentially Gaussian) against the 1994--2004 IS kurtosis of $3.05$. The cross-decade pattern matches the W2 (COVID 2020) and W4 (2022 rate-hike) walk-forward stress folds (Table~\ref{tab:walkforward_body}, both with OoS KS $< 10\%$): the IS axis transfers cleanly across decades, but a static-IS-fitted CHMM cannot bracket OoS slices whose marginal moves outside the IS regime library. The operational implication parallels the cross-ticker panel above: the static-fit OoS pass rate is OoS-slice-specific and the deployment recipe is the periodic-refit construction (the cross-ticker quarterly-refit panel in Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_ticker_quarterly_refit} and the rolling-copula construction of \S\ref{sec:cross_asset}), not the single-window static fit.

\subsection{Cross-Asset Dependence (Pipeline~B): Student-t Copula}
\label{sec:cross_asset}

Pipeline~B reuses the per-asset CHMM-N marginals from \S\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate} and adds a rank-based Student-$t$ dependence layer (\S\ref{sec:cross_asset_methods}). The body cross-asset construction is scoped to a US-equity universe: profile MLE on six US-equity assets (SPY, NVDA, JNJ, JPM, AAPL, QQQ; $200$ paths) selects $\nu^\star = 6$ on the unit-spaced grid $\nu \in [4, 12]$ with Wilks $95\%$ profile-LL CI of $[6, 7]$ \emph{on the IS slice} (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_cross_asset}); a half-unit-grid refinement plus $B = 200$ parametric bootstrap CI confirms $[6, 7]$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:copula_halfunit}). A non-equity stress test on GLD and SLV ETFs over the same windows (Appendix~\ref{sec:non_us_asset_supp}) shows OoS KS collapses to $0\%$ on both ETFs under a static IS-fitted CHMM, confirming that the cross-asset claim does not extend to non-equity asset classes without periodic refit. Table~\ref{tab:cross_asset} reports per-asset KS and the off-diagonal correlation MAE: $0.027$ on IS for the Student-$t$ copula, against $0.030$ Gaussian, $0.068$ truncated C-vine, and $0.076$ SIM (full panel in Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_asset_supp}). \textbf{On OoS the dependence-family choice is empirically null at this $N_{\text{paths}}$}: off-diagonal MAE is $0.209$ for Student-$t$ and $0.202$ for Gaussian, a $0.007$ difference below the simulation-noise floor. The IS calibration distinction ($0.027$ vs.\ $0.030$, $0.003$ difference) is an IS-only finding and the dependence-family selection on OoS is not supported at this $N_{\text{paths}}$. The operational story on the OoS axis is the quarterly-rolling refit, which reduces the OoS off-diagonal MAE from $0.209$ to $\mathbf{0.185}$ on the six-asset universe (Appendix~\ref{sec:rolling_copula_supp}); the dependence-family choice is dominated by the refit-cadence choice on OoS. One additional caveat on the dependence-layer estimator: the body $\nu^\star$ is selected by profile MLE conditional on $\hat\Sigma$ obtained by Kendall's-$\tau$ inversion ($\rho_{ij} = \sin(\pi \tau_{ij}/2)$, exact under the Gaussian copula and approximate under the Student-$t$ copula); a full one-shot MLE that jointly maximises over $(\Sigma, \nu)$ on the same six-asset universe (Appendix~\ref{sec:full_tcopula_mle}) returns $\hat\nu_{\text{full}} = 6.40$ versus $\hat\nu_{\text{two-step}} = 6.00$, a $+5.55$ log-likelihood improvement, with all 15 off-diagonal correlation differences below $|\Delta \rho_{ij}| \le 0.025$. The two-step estimator is therefore not detectably biased toward the Gaussian limit on this universe; the body $\nu^\star = 6$ choice is robust to the estimator switch and remains inside the Wilks 95\% profile-LL CI of the full MLE.
