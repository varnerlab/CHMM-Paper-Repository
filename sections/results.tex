\subsection{Descriptive Statistics and Stylized Facts}
\label{sec:descriptive}

All three Cont stylized facts are present in both windows (Figure~\ref{fig:stylized_facts} in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_stylized_facts}): heavy-tailed marginal (IS excess kurtosis $7.68$, Jarque--Bera $6{,}416.6$, $p < 0.001$), negligible linear autocorrelation in raw $G_t$ (Ljung--Box $\text{LB}_G \approx 5.5$ at lag $20$ vs.\ $\chi^2_{20}(0.05) = 31.4$), and persistent volatility clustering in $|G_t|$ ($\text{LB}_{|G|} = 2{,}959.3$, $p < 0.001$). The OoS window shares the same qualitative profile (excess kurtosis $5.29$). A stationary block bootstrap of \citet{politis1994stationary} at mean block length $L = 20$ gives $95\%$ CIs of $[2.17, 12.40]$ on the IS excess kurtosis and $[0.90, 8.26]$ on the OoS excess kurtosis (Appendix~\ref{sec:kurtosis_bootstrap_ci}, Table~\ref{tab:kurtosis_bootstrap}); the IS and OoS CIs overlap broadly and the IS-vs-OoS kurtosis difference of $\sim 2.4$ units lies within sampling error at this block length, so kurtosis-fidelity targets reported throughout this paper should be read against the joint $[0.90, 12.40]$ envelope rather than against the $7.68 / 5.29$ point estimates.

\paragraph{$K_{\text{eff}}$-corrected information-criterion re-rank.}
The four ICs in Table~\ref{tab:k_selection} use the textbook parameter count $p_{\text{nom}} = K(K-1) + 2K$ in the penalty term. Reviewer feedback noted that the standardized-distance state-distinctness diagnostic of Appendix~\ref{sec:state_distinctness} reports $K_{\text{eff}} = 11/18$ effective states for CHMM-N at $K = 18$ on SPY IS, so $p_{\text{nom}}$ overstates the operationally distinct parameter count whenever multiple states collapse under the single-linkage merge. Sweeping the diagnostic across the entire $K$-grid on the pre-2020 estimation slice gives $K_{\text{eff}} = K_{\text{nom}}$ at $K \in \{3, 6, 9\}$, $K_{\text{eff}} = 9$ at $K \in \{12, 15, 21\}$, and $K_{\text{eff}} = 11$ at $K = 18$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:k_eff_corrected_ic}, Table~\ref{tab:k_eff_corrected_ic}); the sweep's apparent $K_{\text{nom}}$ blow-up at $K \ge 12$ is therefore largely a parameter-counting artefact. Re-evaluating the four ICs with $p_{\text{eff}} = K_{\text{eff}}(K_{\text{eff}} - 1) + 2 K_{\text{eff}}$ in place of $p_{\text{nom}}$ in the penalty term re-ranks the lighter-penalty criteria but not the heavier ones: AIC and HQC select $K^\star = 21$ under the corrected penalty (vs.\ $3$ under $p_{\text{nom}}$), while BIC and CAIC continue to select $K^\star = 3$ under both penalties. The held-out per-observation log-likelihood and held-out KS criteria do not use a parameter-count penalty and are unchanged ($K^\star = 6$ on the pre-2020 slice). The body headline $K^\star = 6$ therefore survives the correction; the substantive read is that the $K_{\text{nom}}$ IC sweep's monotonic preference for $K = 3$ across all four criteria is a parameter-counting artefact, and that the heavier-penalty BIC / CAIC pair (which scale with $\log n$) is the IC subset that remains coherent across the $K_{\text{nom}}$ vs.\ $K_{\text{eff}}$ choice.

\paragraph{ML HSMM as a co-headline result.}
Replacing the geometric sojourn distribution with an explicit truncated Pareto and refitting through the explicit-duration forward-backward of \citet{yu2010hidden} gives ML HSMM-N as a co-headline scaffold. The OoS KS column at three matched operating points: $K^\star = 3$ at $98.4\%$ IS / $\mathbf{91.0\%}$ OoS, $K = 6$ at $95.9\%$ IS / $\mathbf{89.8\%}$ OoS, and $K = 18$ at $0.8\%$ IS / $33.4\%$ OoS (collapse). At $K^\star = 3$ and $K = 6$, ML HSMM-N is the strongest single-window OoS KS row in the entire panel, beating every CHMM operating point; the HSMM-vs-CHMM trade-off is therefore real and we frame the headline as ``CHMM and ML HSMM are complementary scaffolds on the same Markov backbone, with KS / ACF / kurtosis trade-offs that select between them by use case'' rather than as ``CHMM is the panel headline''. The HSMM advantage on KS is offset by an absolute-return ACF-MAE that matches the i.i.d.\ baseline level ($0.0629$ at $K^\star = 3$, $0.0571$ at $K = 6$) rather than CHMM-N's $0.0467$ at $K^\star = 6$, because the fitted Pareto sojourn concentrates probability mass on a single low-volatility state and the regime-driven slow-ACF mechanism that the CHMM exploits at $K \ge 3$ is muted; the per-state $\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}$ concentration is documented in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_hsmm_diagnostic}. The Pareto-sojourn HSMM at $K = 18$ collapses to a near-degenerate optimum at $0.8\%$ IS / $33.4\%$ OoS KS; under the Yu (2010) explicit-duration EM at the same $T_{\text{IS}} = 2{,}516$, both KS pass rates collapse and the EM converges in $9$ iterations against $19$ at $K = 3$. Replacing the truncated Pareto sojourn with a discrete Gamma sojourn (Appendix~\ref{sec:hsmm_gamma_sojourn}) recovers a non-degenerate fit at $K = 18$ (IS KS $86.0\%$, OoS KS $80.2\%$, $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $\mathbf{0.0462}$ on IS), and at $K = 3$ trades $\sim 20$pp of KS pass rate ($98.4 \to 77.0\%$ IS, $91.0 \to 80.7\%$ OoS) for an $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE that drops from the i.i.d.\ baseline level $0.0629$ to $0.0528$ (Pareto-vs-Gamma comparison: Table~\ref{tab:hsmm_sojourn_compare}). The lighter-tailed Gamma sojourn distributes probability mass across sojourn durations more uniformly than the heavy-tailed Pareto, keeping the regime structure non-degenerate at high $K$ and producing the cleanest single-row $|G_t|$ ACF match in the entire HSMM panel. The complementary reading: ML HSMM under truncated Pareto sojourns wins on the marginal-distribution axis at $K \in \{3, 6\}$ (peak $91.0\%$ OoS KS); the Gamma-sojourn variant wins on the joint marginal-plus-volatility-clustering axis at $K = 18$ (the only HSMM operating point where $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE drops below the body CHMM-N $K = 18$ value of $0.0509$); CHMM at moderate $K$ remains the right choice when the structural use cases of \S\ref{sec:var_backtest}--\ref{sec:cross_asset} are in scope. Readers selecting a synthetic-data generator on raw OoS KS alone should prefer ML HSMM-N (Pareto) at $K^\star = 3$ or $K = 6$; readers prioritising $|G_t|$ ACF reproduction at high state resolution should prefer ML HSMM-N (Gamma) at $K = 18$. Full HSMM panel and per-$K$ EM-iteration counts in Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_hsmm_diagnostic}; sojourn-family comparison in Appendix~\ref{sec:hsmm_gamma_sojourn}.

\paragraph{Bootstrap as a non-parametric synthetic-data benchmark.}
The stationary block bootstrap of \citet{politis1994stationary} attains $99.7\%$ IS / $92.1\%$ OoS KS in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} on the headline single window, comparable to ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ ($91.0\%$ OoS) and above every CHMM operating point. We report this honestly: on raw OoS KS at this single window, the simplest non-parametric benchmark dominates the parametric panel. The use-case differentiation the paper actually delivers is therefore not ``CHMM beats bootstrap on KS'' but rather ``CHMM offers parametric controls and structural use cases that the bootstrap cannot serve''. Three differentiating use cases are exercised in this paper: (i) the regime-conditional Value-at-Risk of \S\ref{sec:var_backtest}, which propagates the one-step-ahead state forecast through the predictive mixture and passes Christoffersen-cc cleanly on $19/24$ walk-forward folds, has no bootstrap analogue at all, since the bootstrap is exchangeable and admits no notion of a forward state filter; (ii) the multi-asset copula composition of \S\ref{sec:cross_asset} requires per-asset parametric marginals and is incompatible with a non-parametric bootstrap that only re-emits IS samples; (iii) on the privacy / licensing axis raised in the introduction, every bootstrap path is a permutation of literal IS observations, so the bootstrap is unusable wherever the IS sample is restricted (proprietary feeds, regulatory carve-outs, training-data privacy). The body comparison ordering is therefore: bootstrap is the right choice for raw distributional fidelity on the IS series; ML HSMM-N at $K^\star \in \{3, 6\}$ is the right choice when KS is the only objective and parametric structure is not required; CHMM at moderate $K$ is the right choice when one of the three differentiating use cases above is in scope. We avoid framing CHMM as ``the headline KS row'' on this dataset because Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} does not support that framing; the headline is the use-case decision. There is one axis on which CHMM does dominate the bootstrap by a Diebold--Mariano test, however: at multi-day cumulative-return forecast horizons, where the regime-switching structure has time to express itself. On non-overlapping $20$-day cumulative-return blocks ($n = 28$ OoS blocks), CHMM-N attains lower per-block CRPS than the bootstrap with $\Delta\text{CRPS} = -0.180$, $\text{DM} = -2.99$, $p = 0.003$ (two-sided NW-HAC); at $h = 5$ ($n = 114$ blocks) CHMM-L is significant ($\Delta\text{CRPS} = -0.029$, $p = 0.042$) and CHMM-N is insignificant in the right direction ($\Delta\text{CRPS} = -0.020$, $p = 0.137$); at $h = 1$ neither side dominates ($p > 0.45$). Full panel: Appendix~\ref{sec:crps_dm_multiday}, Table~\ref{tab:crps_dm_multiday}. The bootstrap dominance on raw $1$-day OoS KS is therefore a horizon-specific result; on multi-day cumulative returns the regime-switching structure adds detectable information that the bootstrap's exchangeable construction cannot reproduce.

\paragraph{Block-aware OoS KS recalibration (headline reference).}
The asymptotic two-sample KS critical value $1.36\sqrt{2/T_{\text{OoS}}} = 0.0804$ used in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} assumes i.i.d.\ samples; under autocorrelated daily returns the appropriate null is the stationary block-bootstrap KS distribution of \citet{politis1994stationary}. We report the block-aware OoS KS pass rate at mean block length $L = 20$ alongside the asymptotic value for the body headline $K^\star = 3$ block, the $K^\star = 6$ sensitivity reference, and the $K = 18$ sensitivity reference (Table~\ref{tab:ks_block_body}; full $L \in \{5, 10, 20\}$ panel and the OoS-anchored block-bootstrap critical value $0.0647$ in Appendix~\ref{sec:ks_block_bootstrap}). Two takeaways: (i) the cross-generator ordering is preserved (CHMM-t penalised at $\lambda = 20$ at $K = 18$ remains the headline OoS row, the iid bootstrap remains the non-parametric ceiling, GARCH(1,1) the floor); (ii) the absolute level drops by roughly $20$--$30$ percentage points across all rows. The body OoS KS headline at the asymptotic critical value therefore overstates the absolute level relative to a temporally-aware null; the cross-generator ranking is the robust comparison.

\subsection{Cross-Ticker Generalization (Pipeline~A)}
\label{sec:cross_asset_univariate}

Table~\ref{tab:cross_ticker} reports the penalised CHMM-t aggregate IS and OoS KS distribution across a sector-balanced 30-ticker panel ($10$ GICS sectors $\times 3$ large-cap representatives) at three operating points (the body headline $K^\star = 3$, the sensitivity reference $K^\star = 6$, and the extended-state-resolution sensitivity reference $K = 18$), all at $\lambda = 20$. The three operating points are within $\sim 6$pp of each other on the headline distributional axis: OoS KS median is $69.1\%$ at $K^\star = 3$, $75.1\%$ at $K^\star = 6$, and $73.4\%$ at $K = 18$, with identical $11/30$ failure counts at all three operating points (and the same regime-introduction tickers driving each: LLY $8.7\%$ at $K^\star = 3$, UNH $16.5\%$, NEM $5.0\%$). They differ on the kurtosis-residual axis: per-ticker median residual rises sharply at low state resolution ($7.2$ at $K^\star = 3$, $10.3$ at $K^\star = 6$, $0.6$ at $K = 18$), reflecting that lower state-resolution cannot smooth the per-state $\nu_k$ shrinkage at uniform $\lambda$; the production recipe for kurtosis-fidelity consumers is therefore to deploy at $K = 18$, while KS-only consumers can pick any of the three at parity. At $K = 18$, the IS distribution is tight (median $99.5\%$, $[Q_1, Q_3] = [99.3, 99.6]\%$); the OoS distribution is wider with median $73.4\%$, mean $66.8 \pm 29.5\%$, and $11/30 = 36.7\%$ of tickers below $60\%$, concentrated in single-name regime introductions (LLY $7.6\%$, UNH $14.5\%$, NEM $5.6\%$). A one-way ANOVA on OoS KS by sector returns $F(9, 20) = 0.44$ at $p = 0.90$ with $\eta^2 = 0.16$ (the $n = 3$ per sector design is severely underpowered to detect sector effects of moderate size, so the result reads as ``failures are ticker-specific'' rather than as evidence against sector effects per se; Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_ticker_anova}). A quarterly-refit version of this panel at $K = 18$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_ticker_quarterly_refit}) shifts the OoS KS distribution from median $73.4\% \to \mathbf{83.0\%}$, mean $66.8\% \to 77.2\%$, and reduces the failure count from $11 / 30$ to $7 / 30$, with the largest gains on regime-introduction tickers (LLY $7.6\% \to 83.7\%$, HD $41.4\% \to 82.2\%$); the same quarterly-refit construction at the $K^\star = 6$ sensitivity reference is reported in the same appendix. A monthly-refit version (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_ticker_monthly_refit}) interpolates the trade-off further: median OoS KS $\mathbf{86.7\%}$, mean $78.8\%$, and $5/30$ failures, so each shorter refit window yields a roughly proportional further reduction in failure count without saturating.

\paragraph{Per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ tuning.}
The body uniform $\lambda = 20$ is the right rate on the heavy-tailed defensives (NVDA, JNJ) but is over-shrunk on the moderate-tail tickers: a per-ticker sweep $\lambda \in \{0, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100\}$ on the six body tickers under a $1.5$pp KS-degradation tolerance (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_misc}, Table~\ref{tab:per_ticker_lambda}) selects $\lambda^\star \in \{0, 10, 20\}$, with QQQ at $\lambda = 0$ (no penalty) and SPY/JPM/AAPL at $\lambda = 10$. The honest operational reading is that $\lambda = 20$ is a reasonable uniform default but per-ticker tuning is recommended whenever a ticker's residual kurtosis at $\lambda = 0$ is within $\sim 1$ unit of observed; the production recipe is to evaluate the kurtosis-residual / KS-degradation trade-off on a held-out validation slice and pick the smallest $\lambda$ that brings simulated kurtosis within $1.5$ units of observed.

\subsection{Cross-Asset Dependence (Pipeline~B): Student-t Copula}
\label{sec:cross_asset}

Pipeline~B reuses the per-asset CHMM-N marginals from \S\ref{sec:cross_asset_univariate} and adds a rank-based Student-$t$ dependence layer (\S\ref{sec:cross_asset_methods}). The body cross-asset construction is scoped to a US-equity universe: profile MLE on six US-equity assets (SPY, NVDA, JNJ, JPM, AAPL, QQQ; $200$ paths) selects $\nu^\star = 6$ on the unit-spaced grid $\nu \in [4, 12]$ with Wilks $95\%$ profile-LL CI of $[6, 7]$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:supp_cross_asset}); a half-unit-grid refinement plus $B = 200$ parametric bootstrap CI confirms $[6, 7]$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:copula_halfunit}), excluding the Gaussian limit \emph{on the IS slice}. A non-equity stress test on GLD and SLV ETFs over the same windows (Appendix~\ref{sec:non_us_asset_supp}) shows OoS KS collapses to $0\%$ on both ETFs under a static IS-fitted CHMM, confirming that the cross-asset claim does not extend to non-equity asset classes without periodic refit; we report this as a documented limitation rather than a deferred follow-up. Table~\ref{tab:cross_asset} reports per-asset KS and the off-diagonal correlation MAE: $0.027$ on IS for the Student-$t$ copula, against $0.030$ Gaussian, $0.068$ truncated C-vine, and $0.076$ SIM (full panel in Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_asset_supp}). On OoS the off-diagonal MAE rises to $0.209$ for Student-$t$ and $0.202$ for Gaussian; the $0.007$ difference is below the simulation-noise floor at $N_{\text{paths}} = 200$, so the dependence-family choice is an IS-calibration distinction rather than an OoS-deployment one. A quarterly-rolling refit on the six-asset universe reduces the OoS off-diagonal MAE to $0.185$ (Appendix~\ref{sec:rolling_copula_supp}). One additional caveat on the dependence-layer estimator: the body $\nu^\star$ is selected by profile MLE conditional on $\hat\Sigma$ obtained by Kendall's-$\tau$ inversion ($\rho_{ij} = \sin(\pi \tau_{ij}/2)$, exact under the Gaussian copula and approximate under the Student-$t$ copula); a full one-shot MLE that jointly maximises over $(\Sigma, \nu)$ on the same six-asset universe (Appendix~\ref{sec:full_tcopula_mle}) returns $\hat\nu_{\text{full}} = 6.40$ versus $\hat\nu_{\text{two-step}} = 6.00$, a $+5.55$ log-likelihood improvement, with all 15 off-diagonal correlation differences below $|\Delta \rho_{ij}| \le 0.025$. The two-step estimator is therefore not detectably biased toward the Gaussian limit on this universe; the body $\nu^\star = 6$ choice is robust to the estimator switch and remains inside the Wilks 95\% profile-LL CI of the full MLE.
