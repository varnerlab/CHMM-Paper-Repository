# Peer Review: *A Regime-Switching Continuous Hidden Markov Model as a Reference Synthetic-Data Generator for Equity Returns*

**Reviewer panel:** three domain experts in financial econometrics, regime-switching models, and synthetic-data generation. Personas are adapted from the generic skill template to match the paper's actual domain (equity-return time-series modeling, GARCH/MS-GARCH/HSMM benchmarks, copula-based multi-asset synthesis, VaR back-testing).

**Target venue tier:** mid-to-high-tier (e.g., *Quantitative Finance*, *Journal of Financial Econometrics*, *Journal of Empirical Finance*, *Journal of Banking & Finance*).

---

## Reviewer 1 — Moderate

*Senior researcher in financial time-series / regime-switching econometrics; supportive of new applied methods.*

### Summary
The paper proposes a continuous HMM trained by Baum–Welch as a reference synthetic-data generator for daily equity returns and shows it can simultaneously reproduce the three Cont stylized facts (heavy-tailed marginals, negligible linear ACF, slow absolute-return ACF decay) on ten years of SPY plus a 2.5-year held-out window. The contribution is a unified scaffold across four emission families (Gaussian, Student-$t$, Laplace, GED), an empirical "rank statement" that recasts the Rydén et al. (1998) low-$K$ failure as binding only at $K \le 3$, and a regime-conditional VaR construction that passes Christoffersen-cc on most rolling-origin folds. Overall this is a solid, well-engineered applied econometrics paper, suitable for the target venue after revision.

### Strengths
1. **Unified scaffold across four emission families with quantile-based initialization** ([sections/model.tex:21](sections/model.tex#L21), [sections/estimation.tex:8-15](sections/estimation.tex#L8-L15)). Holding the forward–backward recursions and initialization fixed across CHMM-N/-t/-L/-GED is a clean ablation that lets the kurtosis comparison in Table 1 ([sections/results.tex:30-51](sections/results.tex#L30-L51)) be interpreted as a per-family M-step effect rather than a confound from initialization or stopping rules.
2. **Clear pre-registered held-out windows** ([sections/model.tex:4](sections/model.tex#L4)). Reporting both an information-criterion default ($K^\star=3$) and a held-out-clean reference ($K^\star=6$) on a strictly pre-2020 slice is unusually careful; many regime-switching papers in this space pick a single $K$ post-hoc.
3. **The regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc panel** ([sections/var_backtest.tex:13-40](sections/var_backtest.tex#L13-L40)) is the operationally most informative result. Showing $\text{LR}_\text{ind}$ improves from 5.26 (unconditional) to 0.52 (conditional) at $K=18, \alpha=0.05$ is a concrete demonstration that the latent-state forecast adds independent risk-management value, beyond what GARCH-type unconditional generators provide. Extending the construction to all four emission families ([sections/var_backtest.tex:19](sections/var_backtest.tex#L19)) is valuable.
4. **Sector-balanced cross-ticker generalization** ([sections/results.tex:61-83](sections/results.tex#L61-L83)). The 30-ticker, 10-GICS-sector panel, the quarterly-refit comparison, and the disclosure that 11/30 tickers fall below 60% OoS KS without refit is the kind of transparent failure analysis that builds reader trust.
5. **The spectral mechanism for the absolute-return ACF** is correctly stated, properly cited to Hamilton (1994), Krolzig (1997), and Timmermann (2000), and the empirical effective-rank diagnostic at $K=18$ (a single non-unit eigenvalue carries 93.6% of the lag-1 ACF; [sections/theory.tex:69](sections/theory.tex#L69)) is a useful diagnostic that I have not seen reported this explicitly in the prior HMM-finance literature.

### Weaknesses
1. **The headline operating point at $K=18$ is not selected by any held-out criterion.** The sensitivity appendix explicitly states that BIC, HQC, CAIC pick $K=3$, AIC picks $K=6$, and held-out KS picks $K=9$ ([sections/results.tex:12](sections/results.tex#L12)). The justification at [sections/results.tex:12](sections/results.tex#L12) ("multi-objective rule") is honest but not principled; the body table prominently displays the $K=18$ block and the cross-ticker and conditional-VaR panels are constructed at $K=18$. *Suggestion:* either (a) demote the $K=18$ block to a sensitivity reference and elevate $K^\star=6$ to the body throughout, or (b) state the multi-objective rule as a formal Pareto criterion (KS, AD, kurtosis residual, $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE) with weights documented before the data was inspected.
2. **The Student-$t$ "lower-bracket pinning" is mentioned in passing but is the dominant driver of CHMM-t's IS kurtosis behavior.** The unpenalised CHMM-t IS kurtosis of 14.35 ([sections/results.tex:49](sections/results.tex#L49)) is described as a "pinning artefact" but the mechanism (a small number of states pinning at $\nu_k = \nu_\text{min} = 2.1$) is not quantified in the body. Cross-ticker overshoots reach 104.1 on JNJ (vs. observed 9.0) without the penalty. A reader cannot assess whether the penalised variant at $\lambda=20$ is a principled fix or a band-aid. *Suggestion:* add a body figure showing the per-state $\hat\nu_k$ histogram for SPY at $K=18$ before and after the $1/\nu_k$ penalty, and report the fraction of state-mass on $\nu_k < 3$ (where the second moment is barely finite) before and after.
3. **The "single-window" framing in the title and abstract slightly oversells the empirical scope.** The headline is one ticker (SPY) on one IS / OoS split. The 30-ticker cross-section is a generalization check, not a primary headline, and the 24-fold rolling-origin walk-forward (19/24 conditional-VaR pass-rate, [sections/var_backtest.tex:42](sections/var_backtest.tex#L42)) is supplementary. Two of the six rolling folds (W2 COVID, W4 2022 rate-hike onset) collapse on KS to single digits ([sections/results.tex:56](sections/results.tex#L56)). *Suggestion:* the title or abstract could acknowledge that the empirical evidence is "single-window plus rolling-origin diagnostic"; the current phrasing implies more breadth than the design provides.
4. **The HSMM comparison is asymmetric** ([sections/results.tex:58-59](sections/results.tex#L58-L59)). ML HSMM-N at $K^\star=3$ wins OoS KS (91.0%) but the higher-$K$ HSMM is acknowledged to "collapse to a near-degenerate optimum" ([sections/results.tex:59](sections/results.tex#L59)). The body frames this as "two complementary attacks" but the reader cannot tell whether the CHMM advantage at $K=18$ is genuine or whether a more carefully-fit HSMM at higher $K$ would close the gap. *Suggestion:* either drop the $K=18$ comparison entirely against HSMM-$K=3$ (since the comparison is across $K$ and not held-out-clean), or report what specifically goes wrong in HSMM-$K=18$ optimization in a body paragraph rather than only in the appendix.
5. **The OoS off-diagonal correlation MAE for the copula is honestly reported but the implication is glossed over** ([sections/results.tex:88](sections/results.tex#L88)). IS off-diagonal MAE is 0.027; OoS is 0.209, an 8× degradation. The Gaussian copula and Student-$t$ copula are statistically indistinguishable on OoS. The quarterly-refit OoS MAE drops to 0.185, still 7× the IS value. *Suggestion:* the body should state explicitly that the cross-asset construction is operationally an IS-conditional method that requires periodic refit at deployment, not a cross-asset model with stable OoS dependence.

### Questions for Authors
1. The state-count selection in [sections/results.tex:12](sections/results.tex#L12) is described as a multi-objective rule with no explicit weighting. Was the $K=18$ choice fixed before the OoS window or after? If after, is there any pre-OoS criterion that selects $K=18$ rather than $K \in \{3, 6, 9\}$?
2. The cross-ticker panel reports a quarterly-refit version that lifts OoS KS median from 73.4% to 83.0% ([sections/results.tex:64](sections/results.tex#L64)). At a quarterly cadence, the refit window is $\sim 63$ trading days, comparable to the per-state effective sample size at $K=18$. How sensitive is the lift to refit cadence (monthly, quarterly, annual)?
3. The unpenalised CHMM-t IS kurtosis is 14.35 and OoS is 10.71 ([sections/results.tex:49](sections/results.tex#L49)). Does the penalised CHMM-t at $\lambda=20$ pass the same VaR back-test on the non-stress walk-forward folds as well, or is the penalty calibrated to the headline OoS window only?
4. Is there a principled basis for the $\nu_k$ bracket $[2.1, 50]$ in [sections/estimation.tex:44](sections/estimation.tex#L44)? Tail-index estimators on equity returns often estimate Hill-style tail indices in the 3–6 range; a tighter lower bracket (e.g., $\nu_\text{min}=3$) would constrain the second-moment-finite region. Did you test this?
5. The CHMM-GED bimodal $\hat p_k$ partition is described as "Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail" ([sections/discussion.tex:7](sections/discussion.tex#L7)). Is the assignment of states to the two clusters stable to seed and to ticker, and do the two clusters carry distinct interpretations on the volatility / direction axis?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **Pre-registered held-out $K$ selection report.** Report a single body table that gives, for each of {AIC, BIC, HQC, CAIC, held-out LL, held-out KS, held-out AD}, the chosen $K^\star$ on the pre-2020 held-out slice and on the 2022-2023 held-out slice. The current text in [sections/results.tex:12](sections/results.tex#L12) summarises this in prose; a single table makes the multi-objective rule transparent and auditable.
2. **Post-$1/\nu_k$ penalty per-state spectrum.** A figure showing, for SPY at $K=18$, the histogram of fitted $\hat\nu_k$ before and after the $\lambda=20$ penalty, and the per-state stationary weights $\bar\pi_k$ alongside. This addresses Weakness 2.
3. **HSMM at $K \in \{6, 9, 12\}$ with a regularizer to prevent the degenerate optimum** ([sections/results.tex:59](sections/results.tex#L59)). If the $K=18$ HSMM collapse is the bottleneck preventing a like-for-like comparison, a stabilized HSMM at intermediate $K$ would close the comparison gap.
4. **A monthly-refit row added to the cross-ticker panel** ([sections/results.tex:64](sections/results.tex#L64)). The current step from "no refit" to "quarterly refit" is large; a monthly cadence interpolates the trade-off and tells the reader what the marginal lift per refit cycle is.

### Minor Comments
1. Abstract: "Cross-ticker generalisation on a sector-balanced 30-ticker panel gives an IS KS median of 99.5% and an OoS median of 73.4%" is informative but the reader has to compute that $73.4\%$ is below the headline single-ticker OoS; consider adding "(below the SPY single-ticker headline of $80.1\%$)" in the abstract.
2. [sections/introduction.tex:7](sections/introduction.tex#L7) is a 750-word single paragraph that contains the entire empirical summary; please break this into 2–3 paragraphs (problem statement, mechanism, main numerical result).
3. Table 1 ([sections/results.tex:30-51](sections/results.tex#L30-L51)): the rule for which row gets bold is documented in the caption but the table itself shows bold on multiple rows in non-overlapping ways (column-best within $K=18$ block plus row-name bold for headlines). Consider adopting a single bold convention or splitting into two tables.
4. Caption of Table 2 ([sections/results.tex:69](sections/results.tex#L69)): "$K=18, \lambda=20$, 1,000 paths, seed = 20260420" is good; please also state that the OoS KS is computed under IS-fixed parameters (not refit) so the reader does not have to read [sections/results.tex:64](sections/results.tex#L64) to learn this.
5. The reference to "Yu's (2010) explicit-duration forward-backward" ([sections/results.tex:59](sections/results.tex#L59)) is informally cited; please give the formal Yu (2010) citation in the bibliography.

### Recommendation
**Minor Revision.** The methodology is sound and the empirical evidence is solid. The main asks are honesty about the $K=18$ selection rule, a clearer story on the Student-$t$ lower-bracket artefact, and a tightening of the body framing of HSMM and cross-asset OoS performance.

## Reviewer 2 — Hard

*Expert in regime-switching models (MS-AR, MS-GARCH, HSMM), MCMC and EM theory, and statistical evaluation; scrutinizes mathematical formulation, statistical claims, and benchmark fairness.*

### Summary
The paper claims (i) a closed-form mixture-of-eigenvalues identity for the absolute-return ACF, (ii) an empirical demonstration that the algebraic rank bound this implies is non-binding at $K \ge 3$ on equity-return data, and (iii) that a four-family CHMM scaffold attains 89.7%–95.6% KS pass rates on SPY with regime-conditional VaR that passes Christoffersen-cc cleanly. Claim (i) is a textbook restatement which the authors correctly attribute to Hamilton (1994), Krolzig (1997), and Timmermann (2000); the substantive contribution is therefore (ii) and (iii). The empirical design has several gaps that I would want addressed before acceptance.

### Strengths
1. **Spectral derivation in [sections/theory.tex](sections/theory.tex) is clean and properly attributed.** Equation (4) and (5) and the identification of $w_k = c_k / \sigma_{|G|}^2$ as a real-coefficient (not non-negative) sum is correctly stated, including the lag-zero discussion ([sections/theory.tex:42](sections/theory.tex#L42)) that distinguishes $m_k$ and $M_k$. This is the kind of pedagogically useful detail that gets glossed over in many applied papers.
2. **The full Christoffersen panel ([sections/var_backtest.tex:21-40](sections/var_backtest.tex#L21-L40)) is more thorough than typical** — separating $\text{LR}_\text{uc}$, $\text{LR}_\text{ind}$, and $\text{LR}_\text{cc}$ on the same OoS window. The Monte Carlo size-power calibration at $T_\text{OoS}=572$ ([sections/var_backtest.tex:42](sections/var_backtest.tex#L42)) reporting 80% power at breach-clustering eigenvalues $\ge 0.20$ at $\alpha=0.05$ is a useful disclosure that places the test's interpretive limits on the table.
3. **Block-bootstrap KS recalibration is documented ([sections/baselines_appendix.tex](sections/baselines_appendix.tex)).** The acknowledgment that the asymptotic KS test is "mildly lenient" on volatility-clustering generators is the kind of self-criticism that strengthens the paper.

### Weaknesses
1. **The claim that the algebraic rank bound is "non-binding at $K \ge 3$" is supported by SPY only.** [sections/introduction.tex:7](sections/introduction.tex#L7) and [sections/theory.tex:69](sections/theory.tex#L69) both state that a single non-unit eigenvalue carries 93.6% of the lag-1 absolute-return ACF at $K=18$ on SPY. This is one ticker on one window. The empirical-rank statement, which is positioned in the abstract as a contribution, should be verified on the 30-ticker panel. A single number for one ticker does not establish a "rank is non-binding" claim at the generality the abstract implies. *Suggestion:* repeat the diagnostic on the 30 tickers and report the cross-ticker distribution of the dominant non-unit eigenvalue's contribution; if the median is $\ge 90\%$, the claim is well-supported, if not, the claim should be softened.
2. **The MS-GARCH baseline at $K=2$ is not fit at the same $K$ as the headline CHMM, and MS-GARCH at higher $K$ is relegated to the appendix.** Table 1 ([sections/results.tex:36](sections/results.tex#L36)) shows MS-GARCH ($K=2$) at IS KS 27.7%, which is by construction the unfair end of the comparison. The natural like-for-like benchmark is MS-GARCH at $K=3$, $K=6$, and $K=18$ — exactly the operating points reported for CHMM. The body table buries this. *Suggestion:* the body comparison table should show MS-GARCH at $K \in \{3, 6\}$ alongside CHMM at the same $K$; if MS-GARCH performance does not improve with $K$ that itself is an interesting result, but the reader cannot evaluate it from the current body table.
3. **Identifiability at $K=18$ is asserted by appeal to Allman et al. (2009) and Yakowitz–Spragins (1968) ([sections/theory.tex:71](sections/theory.tex#L71)), but the per-state $\hat\nu_k$ histogram showing 13/18 states pinning at the upper bracket $\nu_k = 50$ (sensitivity_appendix Fig. nu_hist) suggests practical non-identifiability of $\nu_k$ across states.** When 13 of 18 states have $\hat\nu_k$ at the boundary, the model is operationally a Gaussian mixture for those states and the "Student-$t$" label is misleading. *Suggestion:* report the effective number of distinct $(\hat\mu_k, \hat\sigma_k, \hat\nu_k)$ triples after a label-permutation-invariant pairwise distance threshold and show that the model is not over-parameterized at $K=18$.
4. **The Politis–Romano stationary block bootstrap is invoked but the block-length sensitivity is not propagated to the headline KS rates.** Block-aware KS ([sections/baselines_appendix.tex](sections/baselines_appendix.tex)) gives stricter critical values and reduces GARCH(1,1) KS from 26.8% to 6.8%; no parallel block-aware KS is reported for the CHMM rows of Table 1. The reader cannot tell whether the CHMM headline KS would also drop under the block-aware test. *Suggestion:* add a column to Table 1 (or a sibling table) reporting block-aware KS for every row at the same block length.
5. **The Diebold–Mariano test for CRPS is reported with $p > 0.45$ for within-CHMM comparisons ([sections/results.tex:56](sections/results.tex#L56)) but the bandwidth choice $h = \lfloor T_\text{OoS}^{1/3} \rfloor = 8$ for the Newey–West HAC variance is not justified.** $T_\text{OoS}=572$ with strong volatility clustering gives an effective sample size below the nominal $T_\text{OoS}$; a longer bandwidth or a parametric block-bootstrap DM would be a more defensible choice. *Suggestion:* report a bandwidth-sensitivity check ($h \in \{4, 8, 16, 32\}$) for the headline DM $p$-values.
6. **The $1/\nu_k$ ridge penalty at $\lambda=20$ is post-hoc.** [sections/results.tex:48](sections/results.tex#L48) reports the penalised CHMM-t as the operational headline at $K=18$. But the per-ticker $\lambda^\star$ varies (sensitivity appendix), and $\lambda=20$ is selected as a uniform compromise. No held-out cross-validation of $\lambda$ is reported. The penalty is a hyperparameter selected to make the OoS kurtosis look better, on data that includes the OoS window. *Suggestion:* select $\lambda$ by held-out log-likelihood on the strictly pre-2020 slice (the same slice the paper already constructs for $K^\star$), and report whether the chosen $\lambda$ remains 20 or changes.
7. **The walk-forward six-fold rolling-origin test ([sections/var_backtest.tex:42](sections/var_backtest.tex#L42)) reports 19/24 Christoffersen-cc passes, but the failures are concentrated on stress folds and dismissed as "out-of-distribution by KS in any case."** This dismissal is methodologically weak. The point of a rolling-origin test is precisely to evaluate behavior across regimes. If the model fails on COVID and 2022-rate-hike folds, the headline interpretation should be conditioned on this. *Suggestion:* report a body-table summary of walk-forward $\text{LR}_\text{cc}$ broken out by fold, with the explicit caveat that the headline OoS pass rate is on a benign window.

### Questions for Authors
1. The closed-form identity in equation (5) ([sections/theory.tex:60](sections/theory.tex#L60)) holds for diagonalisable $\mathbf T$ with simple non-unit eigenvalues. The Appendix Remark (line 198–199) acknowledges Jordan blocks add polynomial-times-geometric prefactors. Did any of your fitted $\hat{\mathbf T}$ at $K \in \{3, 6, 18\}$ across the 30 tickers come close to a non-diagonalisable structure (e.g., near-coincident eigenvalues with poorly conditioned eigenvector basis)? If so, was equation (5) still numerically applicable?
2. For $K=18$ on SPY, what is the second-largest non-unit eigenvalue's contribution to the lag-1 absolute-return ACF? The headline number in [sections/introduction.tex:7](sections/introduction.tex#L7) is 93.6% on the dominant mode; the residual 6.4% spread across the remaining $K-2=16$ non-unit modes implies substantial concentration. Is the dominant eigenvalue a near-stationary mode (e.g., $\lambda \in [0.95, 0.99]$) and the rest fast-decay? A reader would want this to evaluate the mechanism.
3. The CHMM-N at $K^\star=3$ produces simulated kurtosis 3.86 IS ([sections/results.tex:37](sections/results.tex#L37)) against observed 7.68. This is half the observed kurtosis. The text describes $K^\star=3$ as the held-out-clean default for risk-management ([sections/results.tex:12](sections/results.tex#L12)). Does the regime-conditional VaR back-test at $K^\star=3$ (Table 2 row 1, $\alpha=0.01$, $\widehat{\text{VaR}}_t = -4.56$) produce realistic tail magnitudes given the kurtosis shortfall? The breach rate of 1.57% at $\alpha=0.01$ is $\sim 1.6 \times$ nominal — is this purely sampling noise or does the kurtosis under-shoot show up here?
4. The cross-asset profile MLE selects $\nu^\star = 6$ with Wilks 95% CI $[6, 7]$ ([sections/results.tex:88](sections/results.tex#L88)). On the OoS window, Gaussian and Student-$t$ copulas are statistically indistinguishable. Have you computed the OoS profile likelihood directly to test whether the IS choice $\nu^\star=6$ is supported by the OoS data?
5. Why is the Kupiec test at $\alpha=0.05$ on $T_\text{OoS}=572$ described as "piling against the critical value" ([sections/var_backtest.tex:11](sections/var_backtest.tex#L11)) rather than being treated as a known low-power regime that should be supplemented with a sample-size-aware exact-binomial coverage test?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **Cross-ticker repetition of the spectral effective-rank diagnostic.** Report the distribution of (dominant non-unit eigenvalue's lag-1 contribution) across the 30 tickers at $K=18$. This is the load-bearing claim of the abstract and currently rests on $n=1$.
2. **MS-GARCH at $K \in \{3, 6\}$ in the body comparison table.** This is the natural like-for-like benchmark and its absence from the body is a presentation gap.
3. **Held-out cross-validation of the $1/\nu_k$ penalty $\lambda$.** Select $\lambda$ on the strictly pre-2020 slice; report the held-out $\lambda^\star$ and confirm or update the headline.
4. **Block-aware KS for the body table.** Add a block-aware KS column at a single block length (e.g., $L=10$ from the appendix) to Table 1, so the reader can compare CHMM and GARCH on the same temporal-aware test.

### Minor Comments
1. Equation (5) ([sections/theory.tex:62](sections/theory.tex#L62)) is correct for real eigenvalues but the prose around it should explicitly state that complex-conjugate $\lambda_k$ pairs combine into damped oscillatory modes; the appendix Remark mentions this but the body would benefit from a one-sentence parenthetical.
2. [sections/results.tex:11](sections/results.tex#L11): "every constant-across-$t$ generator (CHMM unconditional, GARCH, bootstrap) rejects breach independence" — please give the exact $\text{LR}_\text{ind}$ values for these comparator rows in a footnote, not just the qualitative statement.
3. Table 1 caption says "Bold marks column-best within the $K=18$ block plus row-name bold for operational headlines." This is confusing; an asterisk for column-best and bold for operational headline is conventional and clearer.
4. The rolling-origin walk-forward Table reference (Table tab_walkforward) is mentioned at [sections/results.tex:56](sections/results.tex#L56) but not pulled into the body. Even a 4-line summary table in the body (median KS, min KS, max KS, count of folds < 10%) would clarify the headline OoS placement.

### Recommendation
**Major Revision.** The methodology is sound but the presentation overstates a "non-binding rank" claim that rests on $n=1$, the body benchmark table is asymmetric in $K$ across competing methods, and the $1/\nu_k$ penalty hyperparameter is selected post-hoc on data that overlaps the OoS evaluation window. None of these are unfixable; they require additional tables and a held-out-clean redo of the penalty, not a redesign.

## Reviewer 3 — Very Hard

*Skeptical expert in synthetic-data generation for financial time series who has published competing methods (deep generative, copula-augmented MS-GARCH, regime-switching stochastic volatility). Reads the paper looking for overclaims, missing comparisons, and methodological gaps.*

### Summary
This paper recasts a well-known regime-switching tool (continuous HMM with parametric emissions) as a "reference synthetic-data generator" and benchmarks it on SPY. The technical novelty is modest: the spectral identity in Section 3 is textbook (Hamilton 1994, Krolzig 1997, Timmermann 2000) and explicitly disclaimed by the authors as such. The substantive claims are empirical: (a) Baum–Welch with quantile initialization at $K \ge 3$ resolves Rydén et al.'s low-$K$ failure, (b) the four-family scaffold spans the kurtosis axis without retuning $K$, and (c) regime-conditional VaR passes Christoffersen-cc cleanly. Several headline claims are not as well-supported as the abstract suggests, the comparison panel has methodological asymmetries that flatter the proposed method, and the "single-window" evaluation design makes claims about OoS generalization weaker than the paper implies.

### Strengths
1. The four-emission scaffold is a useful didactic device. Holding the EM scaffold fixed and varying only the M-step makes the kurtosis comparison interpretable. The boundary-case nesting (CHMM-GED → CHMM-N at $p=2$, → CHMM-L at $p=1$) is a clean contribution.
2. The regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc construction in [sections/var_backtest.tex](sections/var_backtest.tex) is non-trivial and the statistical evidence is the cleanest part of the paper. Independence of breach clustering — $\text{LR}_\text{ind}$ falling from 5.26 to 0.52 — is a strong signal that the latent-state forecast is operationally meaningful.
3. Disclosure of failures (11/30 tickers below 60% OoS KS without refit; W2 and W4 walk-forward folds collapsing) is more transparent than the field median.

### Weaknesses
1. **The novelty claim is thin and the paper's own related-work section concedes the central spectral identity is textbook.** [sections/introduction.tex:9](sections/introduction.tex#L9) lists three contributions: (i) "the substantive content is the empirical effective-rank statement, not a re-derivation of the identity"; (ii) "four emission families... where the M-step is the only architectural difference"; (iii) "a single-window IS / OoS empirical study on SPY plus a sector-balanced 30-ticker generalisation panel and a six-asset Student-t copula extension." Contribution (ii) is a software engineering decision, not a methodological one — Student-$t$ HMM with ECM dates to Peel & McLachlan (2000) and Liu (1995); Laplace HMM and GED HMM are direct M-step substitutions. Contribution (iii) is one ticker plus a 30-ticker generalization with a 36.7% failure rate without quarterly refit. The empirical effective-rank statement (i) is on $n=1$ ticker. Stripped of these, the paper is an applied study showing that quantile-initialized Baum–Welch at $K \ge 3$ works on SPY. *Suggestion:* the authors should either sharpen the contribution claim (e.g., the regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc construction across emission families is the most defensible novel result) or expand the empirical claims to the cross-ticker scope they actually need.

2. **The benchmark panel does not include modern regime-switching SV models.** Markov-switching stochastic volatility (MSSV; Carvalho & Lopes 2007), particle-filter MS-SV models, and the more recent Markov-switching multifractal volatility models (Calvet & Fisher 2004, cited but only in the appendix) are the natural competitors for "regime-switching synthetic-data generators with heavy tails." The body benchmark is one-step-ahead GARCH and one MS-GARCH-$K=2$ row. Of the deep-generative competitors, only QuantGAN appears (in the appendix) and the authors openly admit it is "materially smaller than the original" — a deliberate weak baseline. The newer financial generative models (signature-based, score-based diffusion) are unrepresented entirely. *Suggestion:* add a fair MSSV row (e.g., the Carvalho–Lopes specification fit by particle MCMC), and either fit a properly-scaled QuantGAN or remove the QuantGAN row rather than report it as a "negative control."

3. **The "single-window" framing is a fundamental design limitation, not a labeling choice.** The headline OoS KS of 80.1% is on January 2024–April 2026, a benign window. The walk-forward analysis ([sections/var_backtest.tex:42](sections/var_backtest.tex#L42)) shows two of six folds collapse: COVID (2020) and the 2022 rate-hike onset are the two regime introductions in recent equity history, and on both the model fails by KS. The headline implicitly conditions on this. The dismissive framing in [sections/discussion.tex:30](sections/discussion.tex#L30) — "these are regime introductions the IS distribution does not span" — is honest but it concedes the model's headline does not generalize across regime introductions. *Suggestion:* the abstract's "$80.1\%$ OoS KS on the 2.5-year held-out window" should be reported alongside "median 67.7% across rolling-origin folds, range 0–95%."

4. **The cross-asset Student-$t$ copula contribution is undermined by the OoS results.** [sections/results.tex:88](sections/results.tex#L88): IS off-diagonal MAE is 0.027; OoS is 0.209 (8× degradation); Student-$t$ and Gaussian copulas are statistically indistinguishable on OoS. The quarterly-refit OoS MAE drops to 0.185, still 7× IS. The text reports the JNJ-equity-factor pair sees its correlation flip sign IS to OoS. The "rank-based Student-$t$ copula composes through" claim is operationally a quarterly-refit method; under static IS deployment the cross-asset construction does not generalize. *Suggestion:* the abstract's "six-asset IS off-diagonal correlation MAE 0.027" should be paired with the OoS MAE (0.209 static, 0.185 quarterly refit) so the reader understands what the 0.027 represents.

5. **The "operating point" terminology is used to obscure post-hoc selection.** Three operating points are reported: $K^\star=3$ (BIC default), $K^\star=6$ (held-out reference), $K=18$ (multi-objective). The body table headlines $K=18$. The cross-ticker table is at $K=18$. The conditional VaR table includes $K=3$ and $K=18$. The penalised CHMM-t at $\lambda=20$ is at $K=18$. Choosing $K=18$ post-hoc, then framing it as one of "three operating points" reads as forking-paths protection. The held-out criteria pick $K \in \{3, 6, 9\}$; none picks 18. *Suggestion:* either commit to $K^\star=6$ as the headline and demote $K=18$ to a sensitivity reference, or formally pre-register the multi-objective rule as a Pareto criterion and disclose its weights.

6. **The HSMM "co-headline" is not co-headline if the higher-$K$ HSMM collapses and the SM-CHMM appendix comparison uses a plug-in (not ML) estimator.** [sections/results.tex:58-59](sections/results.tex#L58-L59): ML HSMM-N at $K^\star=3$ wins OoS KS (91.0%) and the higher-$K$ HSMM "collapses to a near-degenerate optimum." The reader is left to infer whether the CHMM advantage at $K=18$ would survive against a stabilized HSMM at $K=18$. The appendix's SM-CHMM is a plug-in (Viterbi + AR(1) + AIC sojourn), not joint ML. *Suggestion:* this is a single-paper-defining comparison: either fit ML HSMM at $K=18$ with regularization to prevent the degenerate optimum and put the result in the body, or temper the body's framing of "two complementary attacks" — the comparison is not currently fair.

7. **The "stylized facts" framing does not engage modern stylized-facts literature.** Cont (2001) is the standard reference and is correctly cited, but more recent extensions — leverage effect (Black 1976), the volatility-of-volatility process, the term structure of risk premia, intraday-pattern stylized facts — are absent. The paper's headline claim that CHMM "reproduces the three Cont stylized facts simultaneously" is accurate but the bar is low: GARCH-$t$ also matches all three at point-estimate level. The KS pass rate is the substantive comparator, not the stylized-facts framing. *Suggestion:* explicitly drop the "three stylized facts" headline framing in the abstract and lead with the KS / kurtosis / ACF-MAE result, which is the actual evidence.

### Questions for Authors
1. Is the empirical effective-rank result ($\ge 93\%$ on the dominant non-unit eigenvalue) replicated on each of the 30 tickers, or only on SPY? If only SPY, the abstract's claim that "the algebraic rank bound is non-binding at $K \ge 3$ on equity-return data" should be qualified to "on SPY."
2. The $1/\nu_k$ penalty $\lambda=20$ is selected from a sweep ($\lambda \in \{0, 5, 20, 50, 100, 200\}$). Was this sweep performed before or after the OoS window was opened? If after, the OoS kurtosis match at $\lambda=20$ ([sections/results.tex:48](sections/results.tex#L48)) is a forking-path artefact.
3. The 30-ticker panel is "sector-balanced 10 sectors $\times$ 3 large-cap representatives." How were the three representatives per sector selected? Largest by market cap as of which date? If selection was made at IS end, was there any selection effect that excludes tickers with known regime introductions in the OoS window?
4. The cross-asset universe for the body Student-$t$ copula is six assets (SPY, NVDA, JNJ, JPM, AAPL, QQQ). Five of six are large-cap US equities and three are megacap tech. JNJ is the only non-tech, non-financial, non-broad-market ticker. This is not a representative cross-asset universe. Why not include rates, FX, commodities, or non-US equities (the GLD appendix extension shows the OoS KS collapses to 0% on a non-equity asset)?
5. The Christoffersen-cc test critical value at $\alpha=0.05$ is $\chi^2_2(0.05) = 5.991$ ([sections/var_backtest.tex:24](sections/var_backtest.tex#L24)). The size-power calibration ([sections/var_backtest.tex:42](sections/var_backtest.tex#L42)) says the test is correctly sized at 4–5% Type-I error and reaches 80% power at clustering eigenvalue $\rho \ge 0.20$. What is the empirical clustering eigenvalue under the data-generating process (i.e., the actual breach-clustering autocorrelation under the observed data), and what does this imply about the test's power against the relevant alternative for *this* application?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **Add an independent-decade replication on 1994–2004 SPY.** The current data is 2014–2026. A 1994–2004 replication on the same Baum–Welch scaffold would distinguish "this works on a particular decade" from "this generalizes across decades." The discussion already concedes this is needed ([sections/discussion.tex:33](sections/discussion.tex#L33)) but defers it to "the next data-pipeline pass." For a venue with a high-bar empirical standard, this should be in the paper.
2. **Replace the QuantGAN appendix row with a properly-scaled run, or drop it.** The current appendix admits the QuantGAN is "materially smaller than the original" and is positioned as a "negative control." This is unfair and the row should be either fixed or removed.
3. **Add an MSSV (Carvalho–Lopes 2007 or equivalent) row** to the body comparison table. This is the missing modern regime-switching baseline.
4. **Pre-register the $K$-selection rule and the $\lambda$ choice.** The clean fix is to (a) state the multi-objective rule with explicit weights before showing OoS results, and (b) select $\lambda$ on the strictly pre-2020 slice and confirm or update.

### Minor Comments
1. The phrase "we apply [this] to the empirical Rydén et al. (1998) low-$K$ failure" appears in the abstract, the introduction, and the conclusion. Each instance should specify "to the SPY 2014–2024 instance of the Rydén et al. setup," not the general failure.
2. [sections/results.tex:56](sections/results.tex#L56): "Diebold–Mariano two-sided $p > 0.45$ for within-CHMM differences" — within-CHMM equivalence is presented as a feature. It is also consistent with the four families being approximately interchangeable on this evaluation. The paper should not lean on this as a strength.
3. [sections/discussion.tex:30](sections/discussion.tex#L30) mentions "the asymmetry between the W4 onset (catastrophic) and the headline 2024-2026 reversal (clean)" but this asymmetry is not actually exhibited in the body. The reader has to take this on faith.
4. Title: "A Regime-Switching Continuous Hidden Markov Model" — every CHMM is regime-switching by construction. The "regime-switching" qualifier is redundant.
5. The bibliography appears to be 90 entries; for a paper that situates itself against three decades of HMM-finance literature, this is on the lighter side. Notable absences worth checking: Hardy (2001) on regime-switching for equity insurance, Ang & Bekaert (2002) on regime-switching cross-asset asset pricing, Carvalho & Lopes (2007) on MSSV, and Lopes & Polson (2010) on particle filter regime-switching.

### Recommendation
**Major Revision** bordering on **Reject and Resubmit.** The applied evidence and the regime-conditional VaR construction merit publication, but the framing materially overstates novelty (the spectral identity is textbook, by the authors' own admission), the empirical claims rest on a single ticker on a single window with stress folds dismissed, and the benchmark panel is asymmetric in $K$ across competitors and missing modern regime-switching SV baselines. With $K^\star=6$ committed as the headline, an MSSV baseline added, the cross-ticker effective-rank diagnostic, an independent-decade replication, and pre-registered hyperparameter selection, this is a strong applied-econometrics paper. As written, the "reference synthetic-data generator" claim in the title is not supported by the evidence presented.
