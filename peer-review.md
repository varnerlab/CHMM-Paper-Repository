## Reviewer 1 (Moderate) — Senior Researcher, Regime-Switching Time-Series Models

### Summary
The revised manuscript proposes a continuous Hidden Markov Model (CHMM) as a reference synthetic-data generator for daily equity returns, trained by Baum-Welch / ECM under quantile-based initialization, with four interchangeable emission families (Gaussian, Student-t, Laplace, GED) sharing identical forward-backward recursions. The principal recommended operating point is now the held-out-selected $K^\star = 3$, with a $K = 18$ tail-fidelity reference panel for synthetic-data consumers; a regime-conditional VaR back-test under one-step-ahead latent-state forecast propagation passes Kupiec / Christoffersen-ind / Christoffersen-cc cleanly at every $(K, \alpha)$ on OoS. The empirical study is comprehensive for a single-asset benchmark, the spectral identity for the absolute-return ACF is correctly stated and now correctly attributed to Hamilton (1994) / Krolzig (1997) / Timmermann (2000), and the contribution is incremental but useful. Suitable after a second round of revisions.

### Strengths
1. The unified ECM scaffold across four emission families (Section 3, Algorithm A.1) is a clean architectural choice. Reporting CHMM-N, CHMM-t, CHMM-L, and CHMM-GED on identical forward-backward recursions and quantile-based initialization isolates the emission family as the only architectural lever, which makes the kurtosis-axis comparison in Section 4 (CHMM-N undershoots, CHMM-t overshoots, CHMM-L matches, CHMM-GED interpolates with a bimodal Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail $\hat p_k$ partition) genuinely interpretable. The CHMM-GED variant with bracketed per-state $p_k$ is the most original element of the paper.
2. The headline reframing addresses the v2 selection-on-the-test-set concern. Section 3.2 now cleanly reports two reference operating points ($K^\star = 3$ as the held-out-likelihood / BIC choice; $K = 18$ as the tail-fidelity reference panel), Table 1 leads with the held-out-selected row, and the abstract's first KS pass-rate quote is the $K^\star = 3$ value. This is the right framing on the authors' own held-out evidence.
3. The regime-conditional VaR result delivered in Section 4.4 (Table 4, equation defining $\widehat{\text{VaR}}_t(\alpha) = F_t^{-1}(\alpha)$ with $F_t = \sum_k \Pr(s_t = k|\mathcal F_{t-1})\,\Phi(\cdot;\mu_k,\sigma_k)$) is the substantive risk-management contribution that v2 flagged as missing. The improvement from $\text{LR}_{\text{ind}} = 5.26$ unconditional to $\text{LR}_{\text{ind}} = 0.52$ conditional at $(K = 18, \alpha = 0.05)$ on OoS is a clean demonstration of the regime-switching value proposition. Reusing the IS-fixed parameters rather than retuning is the right operational choice.
4. The effective-spectral-rank diagnostic in Appendix C.4 (Table C.3) is the right complement to Section 2.3's rank statement. Showing that a single non-unit eigenvalue ($|\lambda| = 0.929$) carries $93.6\%$ of the lag-1 absolute-return ACF at $K = 18$ and that the dominant mode at $K = 3$ alone carries $96.8\%$ is an unambiguous demonstration that the temporal axis decouples from $K$ on equity-return data, and it makes the body framing of the spectral mechanism honest: the rank constraint on $\mathbf T - \mathbf 1\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}^\top$ is a non-binding upper bound at $K \ge 3$, and the substantive contribution is the empirical calibration, not the identity.
5. The robustness layer is thorough: ten-seed Monte Carlo (Table B.2), KS test-power calibration with the $90\%$ "nearly correct" ceiling (Appendix B.1), block-bootstrap KS recalibration at three block lengths (B.2), the multi-emission $K$-sweep across all four families (Table A.2), and the Ryd\'{e}n $K = 2$ replication under quantile vs five random seeds (Table A.7). The seed-to-seed standard deviations (1.2--1.8% on IS KS) are small relative to cross-generator gaps in Table 1.

### Weaknesses
1. **The semi-Markov foil is still fit by a Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in estimator** (Appendix B.6, "SM-CHMM" paragraph). The body Section 1 paragraph 3 and Section 5 paragraph 1 continue to argue that "moderate-$K$ continuous HMM achieves the same end through the spectral richness of $\mathbf T$ and the additional mixture components" as the explicit-duration HSMM route, and the only empirical support for this claim is the SM-CHMM rows in Table B.5 ($79$--$80\%$ IS KS vs CHMM $93$--$94\%$). The authors themselves caveat in Appendix B.6 ("Caveat on the SM-CHMM estimator") that this is not maximum likelihood for the underlying HSMM and a full ML HSMM may close part of the gap, with the ML HSMM "left as a companion-paper extension." The caveat is appropriate; the body framing that survives the caveat is not. Recommendation: either (a) implement an ML HSMM at $K \in \{3, 18\}$ with explicit Pareto sojourn (the AIC-selected sojourn family for $17$ of $18$ states in Appendix B.6, so the implementation reuses what the plug-in produced), or (b) remove the body's "achieves the same end" framing entirely and limit the SM-CHMM panel to a faithfulness check on the plug-in estimator. Without an ML HSMM, the principal narrative of the introduction does not survive scrutiny.
2. **The OoS evaluation is a single 2024--2026 window for the headline univariate panel.** The OoS observed kurtosis (5.29) is materially lower than IS (7.68), and the period covers an AI-driven NVDA rally and a rate-cycle reversal (cross-ticker results show the JPM and NVDA OoS KS cliffs). The CHMM-N $K^\star = 3$ vs $K = 18$ ranking and the four-emission ordering are reported on this single window. A walk-forward / rolling-origin design (e.g., 5--8 windows of 250 trading days each, each preceded by a 5-year training window) would let the reader assess how much of the $K^\star = 3$ vs $K = 18$ headline ordering is window-specific. Recommendation: add a rolling-origin Table 1 in the appendix reporting headline metrics aggregated across 5--8 windows. This is a moderate computational cost given the existing single-seed-root reproducer.
3. **The cross-ticker generalization panel is still six tickers** (SPY, NVDA, JNJ, JPM, AAPL, QQQ), which is too small for a "generalization" framing. Section 4.2 reports two of six tickers below 60% OoS KS (NVDA $53.8$, JPM $53.8$); the body characterises this as a stationarity-scope artefact, but the same scope concern applies to the SPY headline OoS window. The 30-50 ticker sector-balanced panel asked for in v2 is not delivered. Recommendation: either extend the universe to at least 30 tickers (sector-balanced from the S&P 500) and report the aggregate OoS KS pass-rate distribution (median, IQR), or rename Section 4.2 to "Spot-check on five additional tickers" and remove the generalisation framing from the introduction and conclusion.
4. **The IS / OoS asymmetry of the Pipeline-B off-diagonal MAE remains under-emphasised.** The abstract reports the IS off-diagonal MAE of $0.027$ at $\nu^\star = 6$ and notes that the Gaussian and Student-t copulas are statistically indistinguishable on OoS, but does not report the OoS off-diagonal MAE itself ($0.209$, an order of magnitude larger). The conclusion does report both values, and the rolling-quarterly-refit improvement to $0.185$ (Appendix C.10, Table C.6) is an honest piece of work, but a reader reading only the abstract would miss the IS-to-OoS degradation. Recommendation: report both IS and OoS off-diagonal MAE in the abstract, with the rolling-refit $0.185$ reported alongside the static-IS $0.209$ as the operational variant.
5. **The CHMM-GED $\hat p_k$ partition deserves more visibility.** The bimodal Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail partition (13 bulk states with $\hat p_k$ near the upper bracket, 4 tail states with $\hat p_k \in [0.86, 1.24]$) is the most original empirical finding in the paper, replicates across 10 seeds and across all six SPY-member tickers, and is currently buried in Appendix C.5 "$\hat p_k$ diagnostics" with a single sentence in the discussion. Recommendation: promote a one-figure summary of the $\hat p_k$ partition (per-state $\hat p_k$ histogram, ordered by stationary mass) into the body, ideally inside Section 4.1. This is the structural finding that distinguishes the paper from a "yet another HMM emission family swap."
6. **The unconditional Kupiec column in Table 4 is barely informative at this $T_{\text{OoS}}$.** Footnote in Table 3 (the body VaR/ES table) acknowledges the integer-breach grid effect: at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ and $\alpha = 0.05$, $k = 19$ breaches gives $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}} = 3.828$, just below the asymptotic $\chi^2_1$ critical value of $3.841$, so most CHMM rows cluster at $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}} \in \{3.03, 3.83\}$. The unconditional pass / fail verdict is correspondingly coarse. The regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc is the more informative diagnostic, and it should lead the body discussion of VaR rather than the unconditional Kupiec.

### Questions for Authors
1. **Effective rank diagnostic vs $K$ choice.** Appendix C.4 shows that at the fitted $\hat{\mathbf T}$ for $K = 18$ a single non-unit eigenvalue carries $93.6\%$ of the lag-1 ACF, and that at $K = 3$ the dominant mode carries $96.8\%$. If the effective rank is one to three at every operating point in the sweep, why not parameterize $\mathbf T$ directly with low effective rank (e.g., a rank-3 sketch of the deflated matrix plus the stationary projector) and reduce parameter count from $K^2 + 2K - 1 = 359$ at $K = 18$ to $\approx 60$? This is the natural follow-up implied by your own diagnostic.
2. **Pre-2020 held-out re-selection picks $K^\star = 6$** (Appendix C.6, "no rate-hike confound" paragraph). Have you considered making $K = 6$ the second reference operating point, either replacing or supplementing the $K = 18$ tail-fidelity panel? Held-out KS pass rate at $K = 6$ on the strictly pre-COVID validation slice is $96.8\%$ (against $84.6\%$ at $K = 3$); both held-out criteria that avoid the rate-hike confound select $K^\star = 6$.
3. **CHMM-GED structural partition robustness.** The 13 / 4 bulk / tail $\hat p_k$ split at $K = 18$ is reported as replicating across 10 seeds and 6 tickers. Does the partition replicate qualitatively at $K = 12$ or $K = 9$ (where Table A.2 shows CHMM-GED at the highest IS KS in the panel, $97.6\%$ at $K = 12$)? If so, the bulk / tail partition is an emission-family property rather than a $K$-resolution artefact, and that argument deserves a sentence.
4. **Conditional VaR at $\alpha = 0.01$ at $K^\star = 3$.** Table 4 reports $\text{LR}_{\text{cc}} = 3.98$ at $K^\star = 3$ and $K = 18$ at $\alpha = 0.01$, very close to the $\chi^2_2$ critical value of $5.991$ but the $\text{LR}_{\text{ind}} = 2.36$ component is roughly half the budget. Does the conditional construction at the $1\%$ tail get easier or harder under the penalised CHMM-t at $\lambda = 20$ (which is closer-to-observed on per-state heavy tails)? A one-row extension would let you confirm whether the conditional-VaR construction is robust to the choice of emission family.
5. **Profile-LL $95\%$ CI for $\hat\nu$.** The 10-point grid $\{2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 15, 20, 30\}$ selects $\nu^\star = 6$, with $\nu = 5$ at $-14.1$ profile-LL units and $\nu = 8$ at $-9.1$ (Appendix C.8, Figure C.4). A finer grid in $[4, 12]$ and a Wilks confidence interval would tighten the optimum, particularly given the OoS Gaussian-vs-Student-t indistinguishability. Have you computed this?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **ML HSMM at $K \in \{3, 18\}$ with explicit Pareto sojourn** (Appendix B.6 already AIC-selects Pareto for 17 of 18 states under the plug-in). Report Table 1 metrics and Christoffersen-cc on the OoS window for both $K$ values. Required to substantiate the body framing that CHMM at moderate $K$ "achieves the same end" as the semi-Markov route at lower complexity.
2. **Rolling-origin walk-forward Table 1.** Replace the single $2024 \to 2026$ OoS window with 5--8 walk-forward windows of 250 trading days each, each preceded by a 5-year training window. Report Table 1 metrics (IS / OoS KS, ACF-MAE, kurtosis, CRPS) aggregated across windows (median + IQR). This is the most fragile element of the empirical study.
3. **CHMM-GED bulk / tail partition figure.** Promote the per-state $\hat p_k$ histogram from Appendix C.5 into the body Section 4.1, ordered by stationary mass with the bulk / tail boundary marked. This is the most distinctive empirical finding in the paper and currently does not appear in the body.
4. **Penalised CHMM-t at $\lambda = 20$ in the conditional-VaR panel.** The body conditional-VaR Table 4 reports CHMM-N only. A one-row extension reporting the penalised CHMM-t conditional-VaR results (using the per-state $t_\nu(\mu_k, \sigma_k)$ predictive mixture rather than the Gaussian mixture) would let the reader see whether the conditional-VaR construction is sensitive to emission family. The forward-filter implementation is straightforward.

### Minor Comments
1. The abstract is dense; consider leading with the empirical headline (KS pass rates and ACF-MAE) and putting the spectral mechanism in the third sentence. The spectral attribution to Hamilton / Krolzig / Timmermann is now correctly stated, but the abstract still front-loads the spectral framing.
2. Figure 1 (pipeline schematic) is helpful; the bracket labels "marginals" and "dependence" overlap visually with the box labels on some renderings. Consider raising the brackets or thinning the box outlines.
3. Table 1 row "CHMM-N ($K^\star = 3$)" sits between MS-GARCH and "CHMM-N ($K = 18$)"; readers will scan it as a CHMM row by visual proximity. Consider a subtle horizontal rule or a "reference operating point" sub-header to distinguish the held-out-selected row from the tail-fidelity panel.
4. Section 4.3 paragraph "The headline KS pass rates are stable...": ten-seed standard deviations are reported as $\pm 1.79$ etc.; the text reads as if these are standard errors of the headline pass rate. They are seed-level standard deviations of the per-seed pass rate (and at $N_{\text{paths}} = 250$ per seed, an upper bound on the std at $N_{\text{paths}} = 1{,}000$). Make this distinction explicit in the prose.
5. References to "Pipeline A" and "Pipeline B" recur dozens of times; consider a one-sentence reminder at the top of Section 4 ("Pipeline A: per-asset CHMM, single-asset metrics. Pipeline B: per-asset CHMM marginals + Student-t copula on ranks.").

### Recommendation
**Minor Revision.** The revisions since v2 substantially address the headline-operating-point and conditional-VaR concerns. The remaining items (ML HSMM, walk-forward OoS, larger cross-ticker panel, $\hat p_k$ promoted to body) are tractable, and addressing them would convert this to a clear Accept. The empirical study is now more thorough than typical for the venue, the spectral attribution is honest, and the CHMM-GED contribution is genuinely novel.

## Reviewer 2 (Hard) — Constraint-Based Time-Series Modeling and Risk Backtesting

### Summary
The authors fit a continuous Hidden Markov Model with $K$ regimes to daily SPY excess log returns under quantile-based initialization, with four interchangeable emission families, and benchmark against i.i.d., GARCH-family, MS-GARCH, semi-Markov, and QuantGAN baselines. The headline claims in this revision are (i) joint reproduction of three Cont stylized facts at $K^\star = 3$ as the held-out-selected default and at $K = 18$ as the tail-fidelity reference, (ii) a closed-form spectral identity for the absolute-return ACF (now correctly attributed as folklore), with the empirical demonstration that the rank constraint is non-binding at $K \ge 3$ supported by an explicit effective-rank diagnostic, (iii) clean Pipeline-A and Pipeline-B reproduction of marginals and rank-based copula dependence, and (iv) a regime-conditional VaR back-test that passes Kupiec / Christoffersen-ind / Christoffersen-cc cleanly at every $(K, \alpha)$ on OoS. The mathematics is correct, the simulation infrastructure is reproducible, and the comparisons are reasonable. My remaining concerns are (a) the unaddressed ML HSMM foil, (b) the single-window OoS evaluation, (c) the integer-breach coarseness of the unconditional Kupiec column at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$, and (d) some loose ends in the dependence-layer story.

### Strengths
1. The unified Algorithm A.1 is clean and the ECM monotonicity argument (Proposition A.1) is correctly formulated under bracket compactness; the per-state $\nu_k$ and $p_k$ search over a compact bracket is a standard MM-style argument that does not require new theory but is correctly invoked. Good practice.
2. The block-bootstrap KS recalibration (Appendix B.2, Table B.1) is the right way to address the i.i.d. assumption embedded in the asymptotic two-sample test. The authors compute critical values at three block lengths (asymp $0.0383$ vs $0.0306 / 0.0338 / 0.0350$ at $L = 5, 10, 20$, the block-aware critical values being smaller than the asymptotic one because they account for within-window dependence in the IS series itself), and confirm GARCH(1,1)'s drop from $26.8\% \to 6.8 / 13.6 / 17.4\%$ under block-aware testing while CHMM moves only modestly (CHMM-GED drops from $95.6\%$ to $83.2 / 90.2 / 93.2\%$). This is the kind of calibration most regime-switching papers in the literature skip.
3. The numerical full-rank check on $\hat{\mathbf T}$ at $K = 18$ across the four families (Table A.1) is a genuinely useful identifiability diagnostic. CHMM-t at condition $1{,}620$ is borderline, but the authors flag this and connect it to per-state $\nu_k$ ECM lower-bracket pinning. The CHMM-GED at condition $63.5$ is the most well-conditioned, which is consistent with the bulk / tail partition spreading $\hat p_k$ across the bracket rather than concentrating at the lower edge.
4. The CRPS column with Diebold-Mariano $p$-values (Table 1) is the right proper-scoring-rule complement to KS. CHMM-N at $K = 18$ ties the four nearest competitors at $|d| < 0.002$ and significantly beats the Gaussian negative control ($p \approx 0.002$). This rules out the "KS is a bad metric" objection.
5. The new effective-spectral-rank diagnostic (Appendix C.4, Table C.3) is exactly the right complement to the rank statement of Section 2.3. The empirical reading ($93.6\%$ of lag-1 ACF carried by a single mode at $K = 18$, $96.8\%$ at $K = 3$) is now stated honestly: the rank constraint is non-binding at $K \ge 3$, the bound is tight at $K = 2$, and the substantive contribution is the empirical calibration.
6. The regime-conditional VaR back-test (Section 4.4 / Table 4) is the right delivery of the missing piece from v2. The construction $\widehat{\text{VaR}}_t(\alpha) = F_t^{-1}(\alpha)$ with $F_t = \sum_k \Pr(s_t = k|\mathcal F_{t-1})\,\Phi(\cdot;\mu_k,\sigma_k)$ is the textbook one-step-ahead predictive density under regime-switching with Gaussian emissions, and reusing the IS-fixed parameters rather than retuning is the right operational choice.

### Weaknesses
1. **The ML HSMM foil at matched $K$ is still missing.** Appendix B.6 explicitly caveats that the SM-CHMM rows in Table B.5 are fit by a Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in estimator that is not ML for the underlying HSMM, and notes that "an ML HSMM at $K = 18$ on this universe is left as a companion-paper extension." The body Section 1 paragraph 3 and Section 5 paragraph 1 nevertheless argue that "moderate-$K$ continuous HMM achieves the same end through the spectral richness of $\mathbf T$ and the additional mixture components" as the semi-Markov route, and this claim is supported only by the plug-in rows that the appendix itself caveats. This is a logical gap. Given that Appendix B.6 reports AIC-selected Pareto sojourns for 17 of 18 states, an explicit-duration ML HSMM at $K \in \{3, 18\}$ with Pareto sojourn under the Yu (2010) forward-backward over (state, duration) pairs is a few hundred lines of code on top of the existing CHMM Julia package. Required: either (a) deliver the ML HSMM in this paper, or (b) remove the "achieves the same end" framing from the body (intro paragraph 3, Section 5 paragraph 1, conclusion paragraph 2 still carries it implicitly). The current version cannot have it both ways.
2. **The OoS evaluation is a single 2024--2026 window.** The headline univariate panel, the cross-ticker generalisation table, the Pipeline-B copula evaluation, and the regime-conditional VaR back-test all use the same $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$-day window. The OoS observed kurtosis (5.29) is unusually low against IS (7.68); the period spans the AI-driven NVDA rally and the rate-cycle reversal; the JPM / NVDA OoS KS cliffs document at least one source of regime instability inside the window. The conditional-VaR pass at every $(K, \alpha)$ on OoS may be partly an artefact of an unusually moderate OoS realisation, and there is no rolling-origin design to rule this out. Required: report Table 1 (or at minimum the headline KS, ACF-MAE, and kurtosis columns) aggregated across 5--8 walk-forward windows of 250 trading days. The single-window design is the most fragile element of the otherwise-careful empirical work.
3. **The integer-breach grid effect on Kupiec at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ should be foregrounded, not footnoted.** The body Table 3 footnote acknowledges that $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}}$ takes integer-breach values and clusters at $\{3.03, 3.83\}$ (e.g., 19 breaches gives $3.828$, just under the $3.841$ critical value). Most CHMM rows in Table 3 cluster at exactly these two values, which means the unconditional Kupiec column is barely informative at this sample size. The conditional Christoffersen-cc in Table 4 is the more informative diagnostic and should lead the body discussion of VaR. Required: rewrite Section 4.4 to lead with Table 4 (regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc) and put the unconditional Table 3 in supporting position. Alternatively, supplement Kupiec with a confidence-interval-based test (e.g., a binomial-exact $p$-value on the breach count) that does not have the integer-grid problem.
4. **The cross-ticker table reports the penalised CHMM-t at $\lambda = 20$ as the operational variant, but the rate sweep is reported only on SPY** (Appendix C.6). The kurtosis blow-ups across tickers (NVDA $40.8$, JNJ $104.1$, JPM $17.6$ unpenalised) are clearly the reason for choosing the penalty. Does the same $\lambda = 20$ generalise across the panel, or does each ticker need its own $\lambda$? If the latter, the operational story is more complex than the body Table 2 suggests, and the table should report a per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ column. Required: report the per-ticker penalised-CHMM-t kurtosis sensitivity at $\lambda \in \{0, 5, 20, 50, 100\}$ either in Table 2 or in a new appendix table.
5. **The Pipeline-B Student-t copula degrees-of-freedom selection** uses a 10-point grid $\{2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 15, 20, 30\}$. The reported optimum is $\nu^\star = 6$ with neighbours at $\nu = 5$ ($-14.10$ profile-LL units) and $\nu = 8$ ($-9.10$). A finer grid in $\nu \in [4, 12]$ and a Wilks confidence interval would give a tighter sense of the optimum, particularly given the OoS Gaussian-vs-Student-t indistinguishability ($0.202$ vs. $0.209$ off-diag MAE) and the rolling-window refit's $\nu^\star \in \{5, 6, 10, 15\}$ sequence (Appendix C.10, Table C.6). Required: report a profile-LL based 95% Wilks CI for $\hat\nu$ on the IS sample. If the CI is wide (the rolling-refit $\nu^\star$ wandering across $\{5, 6, 10, 15\}$ suggests it is), the $\nu^\star = 6$ headline should be reported with the CI rather than as a point estimate.
6. **The CHMM-GED bulk / tail partition is the strongest novel finding and is buried in the appendix.** Section 4 introduces CHMM-GED as a fourth emission family but does not show the $\hat p_k$ histogram in the body; Section 5 discusses the partition in one sentence; the visual evidence is in Appendix C.5 only. This is upside-down: among the four emission families, only CHMM-GED produces a structurally-informative output (the data-chosen Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail partition), and that finding deserves body-level visibility. Required: promote a one-figure $\hat p_k$ summary into Section 4.1 and a one-paragraph discussion of what the partition implies for state interpretation.
7. **Inconsistency between Table 1 and Table A.2 on CHMM-GED at $K = 18$.** Table 1 (body) reports CHMM-GED at $K = 18$ with IS KS $95.2\%$, kurtosis $5.15$, ACF-MAE $|G_t|$ $0.0548$. Table A.2 (multi-emission $K$-sweep) reports CHMM-GED at $K = 18$ with IS KS $96.3\%$, kurtosis $5.05$, ACF-MAE $|G_t|$ $0.0546$. The discrepancy is small (within multi-seed std) but is not explained, and similar small discrepancies appear for the other CHMM rows. The cause is presumably a different sub-seed / path count, but the reader cannot tell. Required: a single sentence in the Table A.2 caption noting that the multi-emission sweep is run at the same path count and seed root as Table 1 but at a different sub-seed offset (or whatever the actual cause is).

### Questions for Authors
1. **Effective rank vs $K$ choice.** Appendix C.4 shows that the effective spectral rank is one to three at every $K \in \{3, 18\}$. If the rank constraint is non-binding, what is the empirical justification for $K = 18$ over, say, $K = 6$? The body argument is that the marginal-mixture binding constraint relaxes at moderate $K$, but Section 4.1 also reports IS KS rising from $89.7\%$ ($K = 3$) to $94.7\%$ ($K = 18$), which is a 5-percentage-point gain at the cost of $25.2$ nats of held-out log-likelihood and a $4\times$ increase in parameter count. Is the 5pp KS gain worth it for synthetic-data consumers? A discussion paragraph weighing this tradeoff explicitly would help; the current text reports both numbers but does not commit to a verdict.
2. **Why does the conditional-VaR independence improvement land at $\text{LR}_{\text{ind}} = 0.52$ at $K = 18$ but $\text{LR}_{\text{ind}} = 2.36$ at $K^\star = 3$ (both at $\alpha = 0.05$)?** The improvement is large but $K^\star = 3$ doesn't quite reach the $K = 18$ tightness. Is this because $K = 3$'s state forecast is too coarse to track within-window volatility shifts, or because the predictive mixture has too few components to track the marginal in clustered windows? A diagnostic plot of the realised state-forecast entropy $H(\Pr(s_t|\mathcal F_{t-1}))$ over the OoS window would localise the cause.
3. **Profile-LL CI for $\hat\nu^\star$.** The rolling-window refit (Appendix C.10) reports $\nu^\star$ wandering across $\{5, 6, 10, 15\}$ across nine quarters. Is this stationary noise or a genuine non-stationarity in the tail-coupling? A formal test (Quintos-Fan-Phillips structural-break statistic on the rolling $\hat\nu$ series) would make the case either way. As reported, the rolling-window result is suggestive but not conclusive.
4. **MS-GARCH at $K = 4$ and $K = 6$ in the body.** The appendix MS-GARCH extension (Appendix B.5) shows IS KS rising from $27.7\%$ ($K = 2$) to $37.6\%$ ($K = 4$) and $34.5\%$ ($K = 6$), with log-likelihoods saturating across the sweep. This is a clean demonstration that the unimodal-innovation constraint, not the regime count, is the MS-GARCH binding constraint, which is the body's argument and the right argument. Why is this not in the body Table 1? It is the most direct rebuttal to "compare to MS-GARCH at matched $K$" reviewer pushback.
5. **CHMM-N $K^\star = 3$ kurtosis (3.86 IS) vs $K = 18$ kurtosis (5.04 IS).** The held-out-selected operating point is further from observed kurtosis (7.68) than the tail-fidelity panel. ## Reviewer 3 (Very Hard) — Skeptical Methodologist (Published Competing Methods in Regime-Switching and Heavy-Tailed Time-Series)

### Summary
The paper claims that a continuous HMM at the held-out-selected $K^\star = 3$ (with a $K = 18$ tail-fidelity reference panel) reproduces the three Cont stylized facts simultaneously, packages this as a synthetic-data generator with four interchangeable emission families and a copula dependence layer, and adds a regime-conditional VaR back-test that passes Christoffersen-cc on the OoS window. This revision substantially closes several v2 gaps. The remaining concerns are (i) the body framing that CHMM at moderate $K$ "achieves the same end as semi-Markov" continues to rest on a Viterbi plug-in HSMM the appendix itself disowns, (ii) the spectral-mechanism story remains the principal framing of the introduction despite the effective-rank diagnostic showing the binding constraint is the marginal mixture, and (iii) the cross-ticker generalization claim still rests on six tickers, two of which fail OoS. The paper has moved from "selection-on-the-test-set adjacent" to a defensible held-out story; it has not yet moved past "the moderate-$K$ CHMM and a hidden semi-Markov model are different things, and the paper has only fit one of them properly."

### Strengths
1. The reproducibility infrastructure (single seed root, per-experiment sub-seed map, companion Julia package, single-script rebuild) remains among the best in the regime-switching literature. The CHMM-Model.jl package (Appendix A.1) and the explicit additive sub-seed rule are the correct way to do this.
2. The honest reporting of the held-out $K^\star = 3$ (Section 3.2) and the pre-2020 held-out $K^\star = 6$ (Appendix C.6) is appreciated; the revision now leads with the held-out-selected operating point, which most papers in this literature would not have done.
3. The KS test power calibration (Appendix B.1) anchoring the OoS KS pass rates against the 90% "nearly correct" ceiling is the right move and lets me read the 80--86% CHMM OoS pass rates as a serious fidelity claim rather than a low-power artefact.
4. The CHMM-GED variant with bracketed per-state $p_k$ remains the most original element of the paper. The bimodal Gaussian-bulk / Laplace-tail partition that replicates across seeds and tickers is genuinely interesting, even if the body presentation underplays it.
5. The regime-conditional VaR back-test (Section 4.4 / Table 4) is the right delivery of the missing piece from v2. Pass at every $(K, \alpha)$ on OoS is a clean result, and reusing the IS-fixed parameters rather than retuning is the right operational construction.

### Weaknesses
1. **The body framing that "moderate-$K$ continuous HMM achieves the same end as the semi-Markov route" survives only because the only semi-Markov foil in the panel is a Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in, which Appendix B.6 explicitly notes is not ML for the underlying HSMM.** This is the same issue v2 raised. The fact that the appendix now caveats it more carefully than before does not fix the issue: the body intro paragraph 3, Section 5 paragraph 1, and (implicitly) the conclusion all rely on the SM-CHMM rows being a fair foil for the comparison. The authors have a companion-paper code base (Appendix B.6 cites `alswaidan2026smchmm`), and Appendix B.6 already AIC-selects Pareto sojourns for 17 of 18 states under the plug-in. An ML HSMM at $K = 18$ with Pareto sojourn is the right next step for this paper, not the next paper. The comparison with Bulla 2006 is at $K \in \{2, 3\}$ on monthly returns and does not transfer. Required: either deliver the ML HSMM or remove the body framing that contrasts CHMM with the semi-Markov route on capacity grounds. The current version cannot have it both ways.
2. **The introduction continues to lead with the spectral-mechanism story, which the new effective-rank diagnostic shows is operationally non-binding.** Appendix C.4 (Table C.3) reports that a single non-unit eigenvalue carries $93.6\%$ of the lag-1 ACF at $K = 18$ and a single mode carries $96.8\%$ at $K = 3$. This is the appropriate reading: the rank constraint $\le K - 1$ on $\mathbf T - \mathbf 1\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}^\top$ is non-binding at $K \ge 3$, the effective rank is one to three irrespective of $K$, and the substantive lever is the marginal-mixture component count. The body discussion (Section 5, "Replicating the slow-ACF behaviour at $K \ge 3$" paragraph) now states this honestly. But the introduction (paragraph 3) still leads with the spectral identity as the central mechanism, and the abstract still reports the ACF-MAE-vs-GARCH gap as if the spectral story is the main contribution. Required: rewrite the introduction third paragraph to lead with the marginal-mixture / two-axis story (rank constraint non-binding at $K \ge 3$, marginal-mixture component count is the binding axis at moderate $K$) and demote the spectral identity to a supporting role. The empirical effective-rank diagnostic in Appendix C.4 is the right framing; the introduction should match it.
3. **The cross-ticker generalisation is still six tickers, two of which fail OoS.** Section 4.2 reports NVDA OoS KS $53.8\%$ and JPM OoS KS $53.8\%$ on a six-ticker panel. The body characterises this as a stationarity-scope artefact; that may be true, but on six tickers with two failures the panel cannot support a "generalisation" framing. The 30+ ticker panel asked for in v2 is not delivered. The answer "we acknowledge the JPM and NVDA OoS cliffs as stationarity-scope limitations rather than model-selection issues" is a self-protective framing, not a generalisation result. Required: extend the universe to at least 30 tickers (sector-balanced) and report the aggregate generalisation statistics, or remove the generalisation framing from the introduction and conclusion.
4. **The Pipeline-B IS-OoS off-diagonal MAE asymmetry is reported honestly in the conclusion but selectively in the abstract.** The abstract reports the IS off-diagonal MAE of $0.027$ at $\nu^\star = 6$ but does not report the OoS off-diagonal MAE of $0.209$. The conclusion does report both. This is a less egregious version of v2's reporting concern, but it is still selective: a reader of the abstract alone takes away $0.027$ as the cross-asset fidelity claim. The rolling-quarterly refit improvement to $0.185$ (Appendix C.10) is honest; the abstract should incorporate it. Required: report both IS and OoS off-diagonal MAE in the abstract, with the rolling-refit number alongside the static-IS number.
5. **The "tail-fidelity reference panel" framing of $K = 18$ is doing too much work.** The body promotes $K^\star = 3$ to the headline (good), but $K = 18$ is then justified on the basis of (a) IS KS plateau, (b) OoS distributional pass rate, and (c) CAIC, with the body acknowledging in Section 3.2 that "this multi-objective rule uses the same 2024-2026 window later employed for VaR evaluation, and is therefore not a strictly held-out criterion." The pre-2020 held-out re-selection (Appendix C.6) selects $K^\star = 6$, not $K = 18$. So the body has two operating points: one is held-out-clean ($K^\star = 3$), one is selected on a multi-objective rule that includes the OoS window ($K = 18$); $K^\star = 6$ from the strictly pre-COVID slice is reported but not foregrounded. This is incrementally better than v2 (which led with $K = 18$), but the multi-objective-with-OoS-overlap criterion still produces the operating point that the abstract reports the strongest tail-fidelity numbers from. Required: replace $K = 18$ with $K^\star = 6$ as the second reference operating point, since $K^\star = 6$ is selected by held-out criteria on the strictly pre-2020 validation slice, has substantially fewer parameters, and Section 4.1 already shows ACF-MAE flat across the sweep. The body would then lead with two held-out-clean operating points ($K^\star = 3$ for risk consumers; $K^\star = 6$ for synthetic-data consumers) rather than one held-out-clean and one multi-objective-with-leakage.
6. **The conditional-VaR construction uses the Gaussian predictive mixture irrespective of which CHMM emission family was fit on IS** (Section 4.4, equation defining $F_t$ explicitly uses $\Phi(\cdot; \mu_k, \sigma_k)$). Why is the conditional-VaR result reported only for CHMM-N, when the body argues that CHMM-t / CHMM-L / CHMM-GED carry per-state heavy tails that matter for tail-conditional consumers? The natural extension is the per-state $t_{\nu_k}$ predictive mixture for CHMM-t, the Laplace mixture for CHMM-L, and the GED mixture for CHMM-GED. Without it, the body can either claim that the regime-conditional construction is the substantive risk-management contribution (in which case it should be reported across all four emission families), or it can claim that the kurtosis-axis comparison across families is the substantive contribution (in which case the conditional-VaR result is a secondary diagnostic for CHMM-N only). Currently it reads as both, which is an overclaim by half. Required: extend Table 4 to all four emission families, or position the conditional-VaR result as a secondary diagnostic for the recommended CHMM-N risk consumer default.
7. **The unconditional Kupiec integer-breach-grid effect at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ neutralises the unconditional column.** The body Table 3 reports four CHMM rows with $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}}$ at $\{3.03, 3.83\}$, both clustered at the integer-breach values just below the $\chi^2_1$ critical value of $3.841$. Most of the CHMM rows are not actually distinguishable from each other on this metric at this sample size; the appearance of pass / fail discrimination is a coarse-grid artefact. The footnote in the table caption acknowledges this but does not draw the obvious conclusion: at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$, Kupiec is uninformative, and the conditional Christoffersen-cc (Table 4) is the diagnostic that should lead the body discussion. Required: restructure Section 4.4 to lead with Table 4 (regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc) and put the unconditional Kupiec / VaR-envelope discussion in supporting position.

### Questions for Authors
1. The body Section 5 paragraph 1 attributes the simultaneous KS / ACF fit to "the spectral identity of equation~(5)... at $K \ge 3$ with quantile-initialised Baum-Welch." But Appendix C.4 shows that the effective rank is one to three at every $K$ in the sweep, and Section 5 itself notes that "the binding low-$K$ constraint is therefore distributional rather than temporal." So the spectral identity is the mechanism for the easy axis (temporal), and the marginal mixture is the mechanism for the binding axis (distributional). Why is the spectral identity foregrounded as the central mechanism in the introduction and Section 5 paragraph 1, when by the authors' own diagnostic the binding mechanism is the marginal-mixture component count?
2. The body claim that "moderate-$K$ continuous HMM achieves the same end through the spectral richness of $\mathbf T$ and the additional mixture components" as the semi-Markov route depends on the SM-CHMM rows in Table B.5. Appendix B.6 explicitly caveats that those rows are not ML for the HSMM. What is your prior on what an ML HSMM at $K = 18$ with Pareto sojourn would attain on IS / OoS KS? If your prior is that the ML HSMM would close most of the gap, the body framing should be revised; if your prior is that it would not, an explicit one-paragraph argument supporting the prior would be substantive content.
3. The pre-2020 held-out re-selection picks $K^\star = 6$ (Appendix C.6, "no rate-hike confound" paragraph). Why is $K = 18$ retained as the second reference operating point rather than $K^\star = 6$? $K^\star = 6$ has $\sim 47$ parameters versus $K = 18$'s $\sim 359$, is selected by held-out criteria on a strictly pre-2020 slice, and Section 4.1 shows ACF-MAE flat across the sweep. The current second operating point is justified by a multi-objective rule that the authors themselves note overlaps the OoS window.
4. The cross-asset off-diagonal MAE jumps from $0.027$ IS to $0.209$ OoS, partially recovered to $0.185$ by quarterly rolling refit (Appendix C.10). The per-pair table (C.4) attributes most of the gap to JNJ pairs. Have you tested the Pipeline-B fit on the same six-asset universe with JNJ removed? If the OoS off-diagonal MAE drops materially on the five-asset panel, the "stationarity-scope" framing is correct; if it does not, the issue is more general than the JNJ regime shift.
5. Why does the body Table 3 row "GARCH" not include the GARCH-$t$ (which is the strongest GARCH variant on KS in the extended panel B.6) and the MS-GARCH (which is the regime-switching foil)? The body VaR table compares CHMM against bootstrap, GARCH-Gaussian, and CHMM only; the natural risk-management benchmark is GARCH-$t$ / MS-GARCH, which would let the reader see whether the CHMM VaR / ES envelope is genuinely tighter than the regime-aware GARCH benchmarks at matched parameter count.

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **ML HSMM at $K \in \{3, 18\}$ with Pareto sojourn** (the central blocking issue). As Reviewers 1 and 2 also requested. Required for the introduction's framing to survive.
2. **Replace $K = 18$ with $K^\star = 6$ as the second reference operating point.** Both held-out criteria on the strictly pre-2020 slice select $K^\star = 6$; the multi-objective $K = 18$ rule overlaps the OoS window. The body would then lead with two held-out-clean operating points ($K^\star = 3$ and $K^\star = 6$).
3. **Conditional-VaR panel across all four emission families** (CHMM-N, CHMM-t penalised at $\lambda = 20$, CHMM-L, CHMM-GED) at $K = 3$ and $K = 18$. The Gaussian predictive mixture is a special case; the per-state Student-t and GED predictive mixtures are the natural construction for the heavy-tail emission variants and are a few lines of code on top of the existing forward filter.
4. **Aggregate cross-ticker panel.** Replace the 6-ticker spot-check with a 30-50 ticker sector-balanced panel and report aggregate OoS KS pass-rate distribution (median, IQR), aggregate ACF-MAE, and aggregate kurtosis residual. Include the percentage of tickers below the 60% OoS KS threshold, since the current 6/4 split (4 of 6 above 80%, 2 of 6 below 60%) cannot support a generalisation claim.

### Minor Comments
1. The paper still describes $K = 18$ as "moderate-$K$" or "moderate state resolution" in several places (introduction paragraph 5, Section 5 paragraph 1). Compared to the regime-switching literature where $K \in \{2, 3\}$ is standard and $K \in \{4, 6\}$ is on the high end, $K = 18$ is large, not moderate. The "moderate" framing is editorial. Now that $K^\star = 3$ is the headline, $K = 18$ as "high state resolution" would be both more accurate and more consistent with the body's own framing.
2. The companion paper `alswaidan2026smchmm` is cited but is in preparation. Some venues prohibit citation to unpublished work; check the target's policy and consider a preprint.

### Recommendation
**Major Revision.** The contribution is real (CHMM-GED is genuinely interesting, the unified scaffold is clean, the regime-conditional VaR result is a substantive risk-management addition, the empirical infrastructure is reproducible, and the spectral attribution is now honest), but the principal narrative still rests on a Viterbi-plug-in HSMM foil that the appendix itself disowns, the second reference operating point ($K = 18$) is selected by a multi-objective rule that overlaps the OoS window when $K^\star = 6$ would be held-out-clean, and the cross-ticker "generalisation" framing is supported by a six-ticker panel with two failures. With (a) ML HSMM delivered, (b) $K^\star = 6$ replacing $K = 18$ as the second reference, (c) the introduction reframed to lead with the marginal-mixture-binding story, and (d) a 30+ ticker generalisation panel, this becomes a strong fit for the target venue. The revision is substantially better than v2; it is not yet ready as-is.
