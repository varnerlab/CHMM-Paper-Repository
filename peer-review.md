# Simulated Peer Review

**Manuscript:** *A Continuous Hidden Markov Model for Daily US-Equity Stylized-Fact Reproduction*
**Authors:** Abdulrahman Alswaidan, Cade Jin, Jeffrey D. Varner
**Target venues (assumed):** *Quantitative Finance*, *Journal of Financial Econometrics*, *Journal of Empirical Finance*, or similar mid-to-high tier financial-econometrics outlet.

The skill description framed reviewer expertise around metabolic engineering / CFPS, but the manuscript is a financial-time-series paper (Continuous HMM for daily equity returns, stylized-fact reproduction, VaR back-testing). The three reviewer personas below are adapted to the actual subject matter while preserving the moderate / hard / very-hard structure.

---

## Reviewer 1 — Moderate (senior empirical-finance / regime-switching researcher)

### Summary

The paper proposes a continuous-emission HMM (CHMM) family with four interchangeable emission distributions (Gaussian, Student-$t$, Laplace, GED) trained by Baum-Welch / ECM under quantile-based initialization, evaluated as a synthetic-data generator for daily US-equity returns against the three Cont (2001) symmetric stylized facts plus an OoS Christoffersen-cc VaR back-test. The empirical contribution is solid: a 10-year SPY IS window plus a 2.5-year OoS extension, a six-fold rolling-origin walk-forward, a sector-balanced 30-ticker generalization panel, and a six-asset Student-$t$-copula extension, all under a single deterministic seed root with a public Julia reproducer. The theoretical contribution is appropriately scoped as the empirical *application* of a textbook spectral identity (not a re-derivation), which the authors are careful to attribute correctly to Hamilton (1994), Krolzig (1997), and Timmermann (2000). Overall this is a well-executed empirical paper that I would recommend after revision.

### Strengths

1. **Honest framing of contribution.** The introduction (line 12) and Section 2 (theory.tex line 4) are unusually careful to mark the spectral identity as folklore and to scope the contribution as "the empirical application of the identity to the SPY 2014-2024 instance of the Rydén low-$K$ failure". This is the right call and avoids the over-claiming common in this corner of the literature.
2. **Walk-forward as the operationally informative summary, not the headline single window.** The repeated emphasis (abstract; results.tex line 79; conclusion.tex line 1) that the six-fold rolling-origin median is the deployment-relevant number, and that the headline 2024-2026 OoS is at the upper end of that distribution, is exactly the right framing. Many papers in this space cherry-pick a clean OoS window; this one explicitly does not.
3. **Held-out-clean $K$-selection.** The pre-2020 slice ($K^\star = 6$) and the BIC / held-out-likelihood slice ($K^\star = 3$) are reported alongside the multi-objective $K = 18$ as a "sensitivity reference, not held-out-clean" (results.tex Table 1). The candor about which operating points use the OoS window for selection and which do not is appreciated.
4. **Christoffersen-cc as the substantive risk-management diagnostic.** The decomposition of the VaR panel into envelope bracketing, unconditional Kupiec, and regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc (var_backtest.tex) is the right hierarchy. The power caveat at $\alpha = 0.01$ ($T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ at 80% power only against $\rho \ge 0.50$ clustering) is correctly disclosed.
5. **Reproducibility infrastructure.** Public Julia package CHMM-Model.jl with a single seed root, deterministic sub-seed derivation rule (supplementary.tex line 21), and a one-command rebuild script. This is above the bar for the field.

### Weaknesses

1. **The CHMM-t lower-bracket pinning is acknowledged but not fully resolved.** The discussion (discussion.tex line 7) admits the $\nu_{\min} = 2.1$ bracket lies below the kurtosis-finiteness threshold $\nu = 4$ and that "the penalty is doing the work of a more sensible bracket". The paper should either run the $\nu_{\min} = 4$ ablation in the body or remove the unpenalized CHMM-t row from Table 2 entirely. Reporting both the unpenalized and penalized rows next to each other invites the reader to do the comparison the authors have themselves flagged as unsound.
2. **The "ML HSMM beats every CHMM operating point on OoS KS" finding is buried.** Section 4 line 84 acknowledges that ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ attains 91.0% OoS KS, the highest single-window pass rate in the entire panel, beating every CHMM row. This is a substantive finding that contradicts the natural reading of the abstract ("CHMM is the headline scaffold"). The framing should be moved to the abstract or at least the introduction: "CHMM and ML HSMM are complementary scaffolds with different KS / ACF / kurtosis trade-offs; the ML HSMM is the OoS-KS leader at $K \in \{3, 6\}$ but its $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE matches the i.i.d.\ baseline."
3. **The 30-ticker sector design is underpowered to detect sector effects.** Table 5 reports $F(9, 20) = 0.44$ at $p = 0.90$ on the sector ANOVA (results.tex line 89; cross-ticker appendix), with the authors correctly noting "$n = 3$ per sector is severely underpowered". Given this, presenting the sector medians at all is somewhat misleading. Either expand to $n = 5$ or $n = 10$ per sector (50-150 tickers) or drop the per-sector breakdown entirely and report the universe distribution.
4. **The cross-asset claim is essentially negative on OoS.** The body (results.tex line 118) and the abstract both state that on OoS the Gaussian and Student-$t$ copulas are statistically indistinguishable (off-diagonal MAE 0.202 vs 0.209). Given that the entire copula apparatus is the main multi-asset contribution, the body text should be more explicit that the OoS deployment recommendation is to use the Gaussian copula (which is simpler) and reserve the Student-$t$ for IS-calibration applications. The current framing reads as "the IS distinction is real and the OoS distinction is a sample-size issue", which is not what the data show at $N_{\text{paths}} = 200$.
5. **Quantile-based initialization is presented as the only initialization studied at the headline operating points.** The discussion (discussion.tex line 4) reports that ACF-MAE is constant across "quantile and five random-seed initializations" at $K = 2$. A standard sensitivity check would extend this to $K \in \{6, 18\}$ to confirm that the held-out-clean $K^\star = 6$ result is initialization-robust at the headline operating point, not only at the $K = 2$ replication.

### Questions for Authors

1. The body uniform $\lambda = 20$ shrinkage rate is held-out-clean at the SPY level (Appendix lambda_cv_pre2020) but Table per_ticker_lambda shows $\lambda^\star \in \{0, 10, 20\}$ across six tickers, with QQQ at $\lambda = 0$. Why not use the per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ rule everywhere in the cross-ticker panel rather than the uniform $\lambda = 20$? The body note "the production recipe is per-ticker tuning" implies the uniform-$\lambda$ panel is explicitly a sub-optimal point for cross-ticker numbers.
2. The kurtosis IS observed is $7.68$ and OoS observed is $5.29$, a $31\%$ drop. The bootstrap CIs (Appendix kurtosis_bootstrap_ci) reportedly do not distinguish them at conventional levels. Given this, why not target the *bootstrap-CI mid-point* rather than either window's point estimate as the calibration target? CHMM-N at $K^\star = 6$ matches the OoS point but not the IS point; reporting the residual against the bootstrap CI rather than the point estimates would be cleaner.
3. The conditional Christoffersen-cc passes on $19/24$ walk-forward folds, with the two failures concentrated on the COVID and rate-hike folds that the univariate KS already flags as out-of-distribution (Appendix walkforward_cond_var). Are these failures on the $\text{LR}_{\text{ind}}$ component (clustering) or the $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}}$ component (coverage)? Table 9 suggests the COVID failure is a coverage miss ($\text{LR}_{\text{uc}} = 32.93$) and the rate-hike $K = 18, \alpha = 0.01$ failure is a clustering issue ($\text{LR}_{\text{ind}} = 7.49$ at $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}} = 0.11$). This decomposition affects the operational reading and should be in the body.
4. The leverage-effect coverage in discussion.tex line 36 is reported as a partial pass through Markov state-mixing alone. Is this a robustness coincidence on the SPY IS window, or does it replicate across the 30-ticker panel? A single-line summary statistic of the cross-ticker leverage-effect path-distribution would address this.

### Requested Experiments / Analyses

1. **$\nu_{\min} = 4$ CHMM-t ablation.** Refit CHMM-t at $K = 18$ with $\nu_{\min} = 4$ instead of $\nu_{\min} = 2.1$, with no $\lambda$ penalty, and report the same row of Table 2 (IS / OoS KS, IS / OoS kurt, $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE). If the resulting kurtosis lands inside the bootstrap CI of the observed value, the penalized CHMM-t row at $\lambda = 20$ should be relegated to an appendix as "the bracket-tightening alternative" rather than the body kurtosis-fidelity headline.
2. **Initialization-robustness sweep at $K \in \{6, 18\}$.** Replicate the multi-seed (5 random + 1 quantile) initialization sensitivity from the $K = 2$ Rydén replication at the headline operating points $K^\star = 6$ and $K = 18$, with the same per-fit IS / OoS KS columns. The reader needs to know whether random-seed CHMM-N at $K = 18$ is within sampling error of the quantile-init headline or whether the quantile init is doing load-bearing work.
3. **MS-GARCH reference-implementation re-run.** The body acknowledges (discussion.tex line 38) that the MS-GARCH plateau at 27-37% IS KS is partly an estimator-quality artefact and that the proper comparison is the `MSGARCH` R package (Ardia 2019). Until that re-run is in the panel, the "the multi-state benefit is specific to the CHMM scaffold" claim is at risk. This is the single most important deferred control in the paper.
4. **Sector-stratified random draw of 30 large-caps.** Replace the 10-sector-by-3-tickers design with an equally weighted random draw of 30 large-caps (or two such draws as a sanity check). The current panel's $11/30$ failure count is dominated by single-name regime introductions (LLY, UNH, NEM); whether a different sample selection lands on a different OoS-tail rate is a sample-design sensitivity that the body sample design alone cannot answer.

### Minor Comments

1. The abstract is dense and would benefit from a paragraph break before the cross-ticker / copula sentences. Three consecutive paragraphs of joint metrics is hard to parse on a first read.
2. Table 2 column header "Kurt" should be "Excess Kurt" to match the row labels and the observed $7.68 / 5.29$ values (which are excess kurtosis, not raw kurtosis).
3. The abstract says "two stress folds (COVID 2020 and the 2022 rate-hike onset) below 10\% that every generator in the panel rejects" — the parenthetical "every generator in the panel" should be cited inline (e.g., Table walk-forward) on first mention.
4. The notation $\bar{\boldsymbol\pi}$ (stationary distribution) and $\boldsymbol\pi$ (initial distribution) is correct but easy to confuse in the proofs of Appendix supp_propositions; consider $\boldsymbol\pi_0$ for the initial.
5. The statement "at $K = 2$ both axes bind simultaneously" (introduction.tex line 5) is an empirical claim about SPY 2014-2024; should be qualified to "on this instance" to match the cross-ticker scope statement two paragraphs later.

### Recommendation

**Minor Revision.** The paper is well-written, the empirical work is thorough, and the framing is appropriately honest about scope. The revisions are mainly tightening (CHMM-t bracket, ML HSMM framing, cross-asset OoS) and one substantive control (MS-GARCH reference re-run).

## Reviewer 2 — Hard (financial-time-series / regime-switching econometrician)

### Summary

The paper claims a held-out-clean operating point $K^\star = 6$ for a continuous HMM with four emission families, validated against three stylized facts plus a regime-conditional VaR construction. The mathematical content is correctly attributed to existing literature; the empirical contribution rests on a clean experimental design with a public reproducer. I have substantial concerns about (i) the practical-identifiability picture at $K = 18$, (ii) the OoS sample size and the implicit multiple-testing exposure across tables, and (iii) the strength of the comparator panel, which would benefit from either a stronger MS-GARCH or a regime-switching MIDAS / realized-volatility row before the CHMM-vs-everything ordering can be interpreted as a generator-class statement.

### Strengths

1. **The spectral identity is correctly attributed and the empirical effective-rank diagnostic is the substantive content.** Section 2 (theory.tex) is meticulous about not claiming the identity itself as novel; the empirical effective-rank statement (93.6% of lag-1 ACF carried by a single non-unit eigenvalue at $K = 18$ on SPY) plus the cross-ticker dispersion (median 0.76, min 0.33 on NEM) is the right level of empirical content.
2. **The Christoffersen-cc decomposition with the explicit Type-I and power calibration (Appendix christoffersen_power) is exemplary.** Reporting the test as "correctly sized at 4-5\% under the i.i.d.\ null" plus the power profile against breach-clustering eigenvalues is the right level of rigor.
3. **The walk-forward stress-fold disclosure.** The paper does not paper over W2 (COVID) and W4 (rate-hike onset) collapsing to single-digit KS pass rates; it explicitly flags them as "out-of-distribution by KS for every generator in the panel" and uses them to scope the stationarity claim. This is the right call.
4. **Numerical full-rank check on $\hat{\mathbf T}$ with condition numbers.** Table t_singular_values reports $\sigma_{\min}, \sigma_{\max}$, condition number, and $|\lambda_2|$ across all four emission families. CHMM-t at condition $1620$ is a flag the authors raise themselves.

### Weaknesses

1. **The "$K_{\text{eff}}$ vs $K_{\text{nom}}$" disclosure in discussion.tex line 9 is a substantive issue, not a footnote.** The paper admits CHMM-N collapses to $11/18$ effective states and CHMM-t to $12/18$, with $13/18$ CHMM-t states pinned at the upper $\nu$ bracket on SPY. This is a much larger admission than its placement and length suggest. Three downstream questions: (i) is the $K = 18$ row in Table 2 honestly comparable to MS-GARCH at $K \in \{2,3,4,6\}$ given that the effective state count is closer to $11$? (ii) does the held-out-clean $K^\star = 6$ panel survive a $K_{\text{eff}}$-corrected information-criterion re-rank? (iii) the spectral-rank claim ("a single non-unit eigenvalue carries 93.6\% of the lag-1 ACF") is *consistent* with a $K_{\text{eff}} \approx 11$ matrix carrying one dominant mode, which would re-frame the claim from "$K = 18$ is non-binding" to "$K_{\text{eff}} \approx 11$ is non-binding" — a different empirical statement. The authors should run the diagnostic at $K \in \{6, 9, 12\}$ and confirm the dominant-mode share is monotone in $K_{\text{eff}}$ rather than $K_{\text{nom}}$.
2. **The 24-row Christoffersen panel and the 16-row four-family conditional-VaR panel are not adjusted for multiple testing.** Tables walkforward_cond_var ($n = 24$ rows), cond_var_all_families ($n = 16$ rows), and the headline cond_var ($n = 8$ rows) collectively report $\sim 48$ Christoffersen-cc tests at the $5\%$ level. The claim "every $(K, \alpha, \text{family})$ row passes at $\alpha = 0.05$" (var_backtest.tex line 19) reads as joint statistical significance but no correction is applied. A Bonferroni or BH adjustment, or a single joint Wald test across the panel, is required before this can be claimed as evidence for the regime-conditional VaR.
3. **The two-step Kendall's-$\tau$ + profile-MLE copula construction is not the standard.** The body uses Kendall's-$\tau$ inversion ($\rho_{ij} = \sin(\pi\tau_{ij}/2)$) for $\hat\Sigma$ and then profile-MLE for $\nu$. This is exact under the Gaussian copula and *approximate* under Student-$t$ (Embrechts et al. 2002 give it as a robust correlation estimator; the standard reference for Student-$t$ MLE under the dependence layer is Demarta-McNeil 2005 with full one-shot MLE). The authors run the full MLE in Appendix full_tcopula_mle and report $\hat\nu_{\text{full}} = 6.40$ vs $\hat\nu_{\text{two-step}} = 6.00$, $+5.55$ log-likelihood, $|\Delta\rho_{ij}| \le 0.025$. This appendix is reassuring but should be in the body, not the appendix, given that the body $\nu^\star = 6$ is the headline copula calibration.
4. **The block-bootstrap KS recalibration on OoS shows a $\sim 25$pp drop from asymptotic to block-aware** (Appendix ks_block_bootstrap_oos, Table 11). The CHMM-t at $L = 20$ block-aware is $61.6\%$ vs $84.8\%$ asymptotic. The body continues to report the asymptotic pass rates as the headline (Table 2). The block-aware result preserves the cross-generator ordering but materially reduces the absolute level. The body should report both side-by-side; the asymptotic-only headline overstates the absolute OoS performance.
5. **The "no held-out criterion selects $K = 18$" statement (Table 1) is in tension with the body framing.** The held-out-clean criteria all pick $K \in \{3, 6, 9\}$; $K = 18$ comes only from a multi-objective rule that uses the OoS window. Yet the abstract reports "the four CHMM variants at $K = 18$ attain 93.6-95.6\% IS and 80.8-85.7\% OoS KS" as a leading numerical result, and Table 5 reports the cross-ticker panel at $K = 18$ as the body operating point. This is a model-selection / data-leakage concern that the held-out-clean re-build at $K^\star = 6$ partially answers (median OoS KS 75.1\% vs 73.4\% within sampling error) but the body reporting still leans on the not-held-out-clean $K = 18$ panel.
6. **The single non-equity stress test (GLD/SLV at 0\% OoS KS) collapses too cleanly to be just a scope statement.** A 0\% OoS pass rate on a static IS-fitted CHMM, when the IS distribution is fit on commodity ETFs over the same window, suggests either (i) a regime shift between IS and OoS that no static model could span, or (ii) something wrong with the per-state $\sigma_k$ scaling under low-volatility commodity ETFs. The body treats this as a documented limitation, but a 0\% pass rate vs the IS pass rate is unusual and merits a one-paragraph diagnostic (which IS state's $\bar\pi_k$ collapses on OoS, what is the implied OoS regime, what does a one-quarter refit do).
7. **CRPS-based DM tests within the CHMM family are reported as "$p > 0.45$ within-CHMM" (results.tex line 79) but the test statistics, the OoS sample size, and the variance estimator are not given.** Diebold-Mariano on CRPS with $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ has known small-sample issues; without the full DM panel and the long-run-variance specification, the "interchangeable" claim cannot be verified.

### Questions for Authors

1. The $K_{\text{eff}}$ diagnostic (single-linkage standardized-distance, Appendix state_distinctness) is reported at $K = 18$. What does it say at $K \in \{3, 6, 9, 12\}$? Specifically, is $K_{\text{eff}} = K_{\text{nom}}$ at the held-out-clean $K^\star = 6$? If not, the held-out-clean panel also has a representational-economy problem.
2. The body uses block lengths $L \in \{5, 10, 20\}$ for the stationary block bootstrap (ks_block_bootstrap appendix). The optimal $L$ under Politis-White (2004) for daily equity returns at the IS sample size $n = 2516$ would be roughly $L^* \approx 12$-$15$. Has the optimal-block-length calibration been run? The robustness conclusion may depend on the choice.
3. The conditional VaR forward filter (Eq. filter, var_backtest.tex line 16) is run under IS-fixed parameters $(\mathbf T, \{\boldsymbol\theta_k\})$. Has the equivalent construction been run with a quarterly-refit forward filter on the rolling-origin folds? The body refits the marginals quarterly (cross-ticker panel) but not the conditional-VaR construction itself; whether the COVID and rate-hike fold failures dissolve under quarterly refit of the VaR construction is an important operational question.
4. What is the within-fit variance of the simulated kurtosis at $N_{\text{paths}} = 1000$? The body reports point estimates ("CHMM-N at $K^\star = 6$ matches observed OoS 5.29 within 0.03") but a Monte Carlo CI on the path-level kurtosis is missing. The "0.03 match" claim cannot be calibrated against simulation noise without it.

### Requested Experiments / Analyses

1. **Multiple-testing correction across the conditional-VaR panel.** Apply Benjamini-Hochberg or Bonferroni to the $\sim 48$ Christoffersen-cc tests across the headline + walk-forward + four-family panels. Report the family-wise / false-discovery-corrected joint pass rate. This is required before "passes at every $(K, \alpha, \text{family})$" can stand as a panel-level statistical claim.
2. **MS-GARCH reference re-run.** Run the `MSGARCH` R package (Ardia 2019) with constrained-Bayesian estimation on the same SPY IS window at $K \in \{2, 3, 4, 6\}$ and add the row to Table 2. Until this is in the body, the comparison-class claim "the multi-state benefit is specific to the CHMM scaffold" is at risk. If the package improves MS-GARCH IS KS materially above the in-house 27-37\% plateau, the panel ordering changes.
3. **$K_{\text{eff}}$-corrected information-criterion re-rank.** Re-compute AIC, BIC, HQC, CAIC at every $K$ in the sweep using $K_{\text{eff}}$ from the standardized-distance single-linkage diagnostic in place of $K_{\text{nom}}$ in the parameter-count term. Report which $K^\star$ each criterion selects under this correction. If the correction re-ranks $K^\star = 12$ ahead of $K = 18$ across all four IC, the body $K = 18$ kurtosis-fidelity headline should move to $K = 12$.
4. **Conditional-VaR with quarterly-refit forward filter.** Repeat the walk-forward conditional-VaR construction (Table walkforward_cond_var) with the CHMM refit quarterly during the test slice rather than IS-fixed. If the COVID and rate-hike fold failures dissolve under refit, the operational reading shifts from "the conditional construction inherits stationarity-scope failure" to "the static parameters are the binding constraint, and the refit is the recommended deployment recipe".

### Minor Comments

1. The spectral mechanism is for *stationary* CHMM. The empirical filter on a rolling-origin walk-forward at the start of the COVID fold is non-stationary by construction. The connection between Theorem 1 (stationary identity) and the conditional VaR (filtered, non-stationary in finite samples) should be made explicit; right now the body theorem and the empirical evaluation use $\mathbf T$ in two different roles.
2. The notation $b_k$ is used for both the Laplace scale parameter (Eq. CHMM-L M-step in estimation.tex line 48) and the per-state emission density $b_k(x; \boldsymbol\theta_k)$ throughout. Rename the Laplace scale to $\beta_k$ or $s_k$.
3. The kurtosis-bootstrap-CI claim ("the IS-OoS difference of $\sim 2.4$ units is not statistically distinguishable") is referenced but the actual CI bounds are not in the body. Add a single line: "IS 95\% CI [a, b], OoS 95\% CI [c, d], overlap [e, f]".
4. Table 1 ($K$-selection) rows "Held-out per-obs log-lik" and "Held-out KS" should report the actual log-lik / KS values, not just the chosen $K^\star$. The reader cannot judge how close the runner-up is.
5. Algorithm pseudocode in algorithms_appendix.tex should report the per-iteration cost; "convergence is typically reached in 20-40 iterations" needs a wall-clock or floating-point-operation count for production-deployment relevance.

### Recommendation

**Major Revision.** The framing is honest and the empirical work is careful, but four substantive items (multiple-testing correction, $K_{\text{eff}}$-corrected IC, MS-GARCH reference re-run, block-aware OoS as headline) need to be resolved before the panel-level claims stand. The CHMM-t lower-bracket pinning is a fifth that R1 also raised. The OoS sample-size limit at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$ should also be stated at the top of every results subsection, not only in the power-caveat paragraph.

## Reviewer 3 — Very Hard (skeptical comparator-method specialist)

### Summary

The paper claims that a CHMM with one of four emission distributions reproduces the three Cont (2001) symmetric stylized facts on SPY plus a generalization panel, that the regime-conditional Christoffersen-cc passes at $\alpha = 0.05$, and that the spectral identity recasts the empirical Rydén failure as an effective-rank statement at $K \ge 3$. None of these claims is wrong in spirit but each is over-stated relative to what the empirical evidence supports, and the comparison-class for the panel ordering is too thin to support the synthesis-headline reading. The paper would be a substantially stronger contribution if (i) the ML HSMM-N OoS-KS-leading row were explicitly marked as the panel winner on the headline distributional axis, (ii) the OoS Gaussian-vs-Student-$t$ copula indistinguishability were reported in the abstract as a negative result, and (iii) a stationary-bootstrap-of-IS comparator were added to the headline panel to confirm that the CHMM panel ordering is not just a "complex parametric model beats poorly-calibrated alternatives" effect at this OoS sample size.

### Strengths

1. **The negative results are reported in the body.** ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ beats every CHMM operating point on OoS KS (results.tex line 84). The OoS Gaussian and Student-$t$ copulas are statistically indistinguishable (results.tex line 118). The non-equity stress test collapses to 0\% OoS KS (introduction.tex line 7). Each of these is a substantive negative result that would be omitted in a less honest paper.
2. **The walk-forward median is the framing rather than the cherry-picked single window.** The abstract repeatedly emphasizes that the headline OoS is at the upper end of the rolling-origin distribution.
3. **Reproducibility is exemplary.** Public Julia package, deterministic seed root, sub-seed derivation rule, one-command rebuild script.

### Weaknesses

1. **The headline scaffold-comparison claim is unsupported by the data presented.** Table 2 reports ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ at 91.0\% OoS KS, the highest in the entire panel, with $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE 0.0629 (matching the i.i.d.\ baseline). The body resolves this by claiming "CHMM and ML HSMM are complementary scaffolds with different KS / ACF / kurtosis trade-offs" (results.tex line 84). But the abstract still leads with the four CHMM variants attaining 88.3-92.6\% IS / 76.3-79.0\% OoS KS as the headline. If the paper is a "complementary scaffolds" paper, the title and abstract should reflect that. If it is a "CHMM is the headline" paper, the ML HSMM result needs a substantive response, not a "complementary" reframing.
2. **Why not use a stationary-bootstrap-of-IS comparator for the panel ordering at OoS?** The IS bootstrap (Politis-Romano 1994) is the natural distributional ceiling on a windowed OoS pass rate. Table 2 shows IS bootstrap at 92.1\% OoS KS — a *higher* OoS KS than CHMM-N at $K^\star = 6$ (78.3\%) and within $1$pp of ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ (91.0\%). The bootstrap row is the "no-model, IS sample reused" benchmark; the CHMM panel needs to beat it at OoS KS to claim a generator-class advantage. It does not on the headline single window. The text frames bootstrap as "non-parametric distributional ceiling" (model.tex line 25) but the panel ordering on OoS KS is bootstrap > CHMM, which contradicts the natural reading of the paper's headline.
3. **The "non-binding rank constraint at $K \ge 3$" claim is over-generalized from a single ticker.** The body framing (introduction.tex line 12; theory.tex line 69) reports SPY at 93.6\% dominant-mode share, then says "the cross-ticker median is 75.6\%, minimum 32.6\% on NEM, so the SPY headline overstates the universality and the body framing is at the cross-ticker median." This concession is buried. The body abstract still leads with the SPY 93.6\% number. The empirical claim is "single non-unit eigenvalue carries 93.6\% on SPY"; the empirical claim is *not* "the rank constraint is non-binding at $K \ge 3$ on equity returns generally". The latter is what the body framing implies and the cross-ticker minimum of 33\% on NEM contradicts.
4. **The "empirical effective-rank application of the textbook identity" claim, while honestly attributed, may not be a contribution at the level required for the target venue.** Hamilton (1994) §22.2 gives the bilinear cross-moment identity. Krolzig (1997) Ch. 3 gives the spectral form. Timmermann (2000) gives the moment-generating-function form for general Markov-switching specifications including conditional means and variances. The "novelty" reduces to: (a) running the spectral diagnostic on a fitted $\hat{\mathbf T}$ for SPY 2014-2024 and (b) noting that one mode dominates. (a) is a routine numerical computation; (b) is a one-ticker empirical observation. The substantive contribution of the paper is the empirical scaffold and the four-emission-family panel; the theoretical recasting should be one paragraph in the introduction and one corollary in the theory section, not a featured contribution.
5. **The Rydén replication and the "modern initialization fixes the low-$K$ failure" claim is run only at $K = 2$.** Discussion.tex line 4 reports the claim is supported by "ACF-MAE essentially constant at $\approx 0.050$ across quantile and five random-seed initializations" at $K = 2$. But Rydén et al. (1998) ran the original experiment at $K = 2$-$3$ with random initialization. The paper claims modern (quantile) initialization is part of why CHMM beats Rydén; this is testable at $K = 3$ (the upper end of Rydén's range) by re-running with random initialization at $K = 3$ on the same SPY window. The replication should explicitly cover Rydén's parameter range, not the lowest single point.
6. **The OoS window 2024-01-04 to 2026-04-20 is "a relatively low-stress slice (no regime introduction comparable to W2 / W4)" — so the headline OoS is selected for being clean.** The abstract acknowledges this ("the single OoS window sits at the upper end of a six-fold rolling-origin walk-forward") but the body operating-point selection is on this window. This is a soft form of look-ahead: the held-out-clean *parameter selection* uses the pre-2020 slice, but the *headline result* is reported on a non-stress slice that the authors themselves describe as "at the upper end of the walk-forward distribution". The walk-forward median (CHMM-N at $K = 18$: 67.7\%, at $K^\star = 3$: 62.1\%) is a 10-15pp drop from the headline single-window OoS. This belongs in the abstract opening, not in a clarifying mid-abstract sentence.
7. **The QuantGAN row at 0\% IS / OoS KS is a "negative control" by the authors' own admission (discussion.tex line 38) and not a competitive deep-generative comparator.** The paper acknowledges the in-house QuantGAN is a 3-conv-layer WGAN, materially smaller than the seven-block TCN of Wiese et al. (2020), without the Lambert-W input pre-processing. The paper claims a TCN-architecture rebuild is in Appendix quantgan_tcn. The body table should include the TCN row, not the truncated 3-conv-layer baseline that "functions as a deep-generative negative control". As reported, the deep-generative comparison class does not exist in the body.
8. **The independent-decade validation (1994-2004 vs 2014-2024) is logged as deferred to the next data-pipeline pass (discussion.tex line 41).** This is a structural-break sensitivity that almost no equity-time-series modeling paper has, and the paper documents specifically that it requires a different data source (Bloomberg / Refinitiv / CRSP) and is deferred. Given the 30-ticker generalization claim and the cross-ticker median 75.6\% dominant-mode share, the model class' behavior across structural breaks of the magnitude 1994-2004 vs 2014-2024 (encompassing the dot-com bubble, 9/11, GFC) is not in the paper's empirical scope. The body should be more explicit about this in the abstract limitation paragraph, not only in the discussion.

### Questions for Authors

1. Why should a reader prefer CHMM-N at $K^\star = 6$ ($78.3\%$ OoS KS) to ML HSMM-N at $K^\star = 3$ ($91.0\%$ OoS KS) on a synthetic-data generator selection criterion? The body's "complementary scaffolds" framing concedes that ML HSMM wins on KS at $K \in \{3, 6\}$ and CHMM wins on $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE. If the use case is synthetic data for downstream ML training (the abstract's headline use case), KS is the relevant metric and ML HSMM should be the headline.
2. Has the i.i.d.\ stationary-bootstrap-of-IS row been run as a *direct* synthetic-data generator (i.e., reuse IS samples as paths)? Table 2 reports its OoS KS as 92.1\% and IS as 99.7\%. This is the simplest possible "generator" and it is on par with the best CHMM operating point. What does CHMM offer that the bootstrap doesn't, on the abstract's stated use cases?
3. The cross-ticker dominant-mode share has minimum 33\% on NEM (theory.tex line 69). At 33\%, the rank-non-binding claim fails: at least three modes carry comparable lag-1 ACF mass and the "single mode dominates" framing breaks. Does NEM also fail the headline KS / ACF / kurtosis joint fit at $K = 18$? If so, the rank-non-binding claim and the cross-ticker generalization claim fail jointly on the same ticker, which is a clean failure mode and should be reported.
4. The leverage-effect coverage is reported as a partial pass via Markov state-mixing (discussion.tex line 36). What does the diagnostic give on the cross-ticker panel? The leverage effect is empirically stronger on individual stocks than on the index; the SPY observed values $-0.135$ IS / $-0.214$ OoS are mild relative to typical single-stock leverage. A single SPY pass does not extend to the 30-ticker panel.
5. Identifiability up to label permutation requires pairwise distinctness of the per-state $\boldsymbol\theta_k$ tuples (Proposition 2). The $K_{\text{eff}} \approx 11$ result (discussion.tex line 9) suggests the practical pairwise-distinctness condition fails at $K = 18$. Is the identifiability proposition empirically vacuous at $K = 18$?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses

1. **Stationary-bootstrap-of-IS as a direct synthetic-data generator in the headline panel.** Add a row to Table 2 where the "model" is a direct stationary block bootstrap of the IS series (Politis-Romano with optimal $L^*$ from Politis-White 2004) used as the path generator. Report IS / OoS KS, kurt, $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE. If this row beats CHMM at OoS KS (likely, given Table 2 already shows it at 92.1\%), the body framing must concede that the simplest possible non-parametric baseline outperforms the parametric CHMM panel on the abstract's headline metric, and the contribution must be re-framed accordingly (e.g., "CHMM is the parametric scaffold for cases where the IS bootstrap is unavailable or undesirable for licensing / privacy reasons" — which is in fact the abstract's stated use case but is not exercised in the comparison panel).
2. **TCN-architecture QuantGAN in the body table.** Move the Appendix quantgan_tcn row into Table 2. The current 3-conv-layer baseline at 0\% KS is acknowledged as a negative control; it does not belong as the deep-generative row in the headline comparison.
3. **Random-init Rydén replication at $K = 3$.** Run the modern initialization vs. random initialization sensitivity at $K = 3$ on the same SPY window, with the same paths-per-fit and seeds as the $K = 2$ replication. If random init at $K = 3$ recovers slow ACF as well as quantile init, the "modern initialization fixes the low-$K$ failure" claim collapses to "$K \ge 3$ alone fixes it", which is a different (and weaker) claim than the one in discussion.tex.
4. **Cross-ticker panel run on NEM at $K \in \{3, 6, 18\}$ with the full diagnostic suite (KS, kurt, $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE, dominant-mode share).** If NEM (the cross-ticker minimum on dominant-mode share) also fails the joint fit at every $K$, the spectral-rank claim and the cross-ticker generalization claim fail on the same ticker, which is a coherent and informative failure mode. If NEM passes the joint fit despite the 33\% dominant-mode share, the rank-non-binding claim needs to be replaced with a different empirical mechanism.

### Minor Comments

1. The paper says "We identify the binding axis" (introduction.tex line 5) but the binding axis identification is the textbook decomposition of the joint problem into temporal and distributional axes. "We apply the textbook decomposition to identify the binding axis on this instance" is the honest version.
2. The phrase "headline-OoS-KS rankings are walk-forward-fragile" (introduction.tex line 13) is an admission that the panel ordering does not survive resampling. This is more substantive than the parenthetical phrasing implies; it should be a sentence in the abstract.
3. Table cross_asset reports per-asset OoS KS for six assets. NVDA and JPM at OoS KS 53.5\% and 48.0\% are *failing* the KS test at the standard threshold. The "median 85.8\%" headline obscures this. Report the failure count: $2/6$ assets fail at OoS.
4. References cite Bickel-Ritov-Ryden (1998) for MLE consistency under hidden Markov models. The Douc-Moulines-Rydén (2004) reference should be checked against the actual citation; both are relevant; the Bickel-Ritov-Rydén citation alone is incomplete.
5. The $\bar\pi$ for "stationary distribution" and $\pi_k^{(0)}$ for "initial distribution" notation is used inconsistently across the proof in supplementary.tex Step 3-4 and the body Eq. (2). Standardize.

### Recommendation

**Major Revision.** The paper is honest about most of its negative results but the headline framing (in title, abstract, and body lead paragraphs) does not match the substantive empirical content. The strongest single OoS KS row is ML HSMM, not CHMM; the simplest baseline (IS bootstrap) is on par with the best CHMM at OoS KS; the spectral-rank claim is a one-ticker observation; the deep-generative comparator is a negative control. Each of these is acknowledged somewhere in the body but the abstract reads as a CHMM-headline paper. Either the framing is brought into line with the empirical content, or the empirical scope is expanded (TCN QuantGAN, Bootstrap-direct, MS-GARCH reference re-run, NEM diagnostic, $K = 3$ random-init replication) to support the headline framing as written. I would lean toward the former.
