## Reviewer 1 (Moderate) — senior researcher in regime-switching time-series models

### Summary
The paper proposes a Continuous Hidden Markov Model (CHMM) trained by Baum-Welch / ECM with quantile-based initialisation as a synthetic-data generator for daily US-equity returns, instantiated in four interchangeable per-state emission families (Gaussian, Student-$t$, Laplace, GED) sharing a single forward-backward scaffold. The core empirical claim is that the three symmetric Cont (2001) stylized facts (heavy tails, negligible linear ACF, slow $|G_t|$ ACF) are reproduced jointly at $K \ge 3$, with the slow-ACF mechanism explained by the textbook bilinear ACF identity over the non-unit eigenvalues of $\mathbf{T}$, applied empirically to the SPY 2014-2024 instance of the Rydén (1998) low-$K$ failure. A regime-conditional Value-at-Risk that propagates the one-step-ahead state forecast through the predictive mixture passes Christoffersen-cc cleanly at $\alpha = 0.05$ on the headline OoS window and on $19/24$ walk-forward folds.

Overall this is a careful empirical study that does the right things on most axes (rolling-origin walk-forward CV, multi-seed Monte Carlo, block-bootstrap KS recalibration, multiple-testing correction, reference-implementation MS-GARCH re-run) and is unusually transparent about limitations (i.i.d. bootstrap dominates raw 1-day OoS KS; ML HSMM beats every CHMM on OoS KS; cross-decade and non-equity transfer collapse). The substantive contribution is the unified scaffold + comprehensive panel rather than a new statistical result.

### Strengths
1. The unified-scaffold framing in [model.tex](sections/model.tex) §2.2 and Algorithm 1 in [algorithms_appendix.tex](sections/algorithms_appendix.tex) is a clean exposition: one EM engine, one quantile-based initialisation, four interchangeable M-steps, with branch points marked explicitly in the pseudocode. This is genuinely useful as a methodological skeleton even though the individual emission families are not new.
2. The empirical evaluation is thorough on the right axes: 6-fold rolling-origin walk-forward (Table~\ref{tab:walkforward_body}), sector-balanced 30-ticker panel with $n=6$ per-sector expansion that adequately powers the ANOVA (Table~\ref{tab:cross_ticker} and Appendix~\ref{sec:sector_panel_n6}), block-bootstrap KS recalibration on both windows ([baselines_appendix.tex](sections/baselines_appendix.tex) Tables~\ref{tab:ks_block_bootstrap}, \ref{tab:ks_block_bootstrap_oos}), and the Engle-Manganelli DQ test as a higher-power complement to Christoffersen-cc.
3. The honesty about the K=18 / K_eff disconnect ([discussion.tex](sections/discussion.tex), "Practical identifiability and the effective state count") is welcome: the K_nominal=11 rebuild that confirms the K=18 row is over-parameterised on the same dataset is exactly the kind of self-check most papers omit.
4. The conditional-VaR construction (Eq.~\ref{eq:filter} in [var_backtest.tex](sections/var_backtest.tex)) is clean and the contrast against constant-across-$t$ generators (which all reject Christoffersen-ind) makes the regime-switching value proposition concrete.
5. Code and data availability is adequate: companion Julia package `CHMM-Model.jl`, Polygon/Alpaca attribution, deterministic seed root.

### Weaknesses
1. **Abstract is overlong and dense.** The abstract runs to roughly 600 words across two paragraphs and contains so many qualifications, sensitivity-reference asides, and walk-forward / multiple-testing caveats that the core contribution is hard to extract on first read. Recommendation: cut to ~250 words. State the four-family scaffold, the headline $K^\star=3$ KS / kurtosis numbers, the conditional-VaR result with the $\alpha=0.01$ DQ caveat, and the cross-asset and cross-ticker scope. Push the K_eff discussion, walk-forward $|z|$ machinery, and the bilinear-ACF identity wording entirely into the body.
2. **The "complementary scaffolds" framing for HSMM vs CHMM understates the HSMM dominance on the headline distributional metric.** ML HSMM-N at $K^\star=3$ attains $91.0\%$ OoS KS, beating every CHMM operating point in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}. The body's "complementary" framing relies on the Pareto-sojourn HSMM matching the i.i.d. baseline on $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE; but the Gamma-sojourn HSMM at $K=18$ ([results.tex](sections/results.tex), "ML HSMM as a co-headline result") attains ACF-MAE $0.0462$ on IS, the lowest in the entire panel. The fix: report ML HSMM more even-handedly. State explicitly that on raw OoS KS, HSMM dominates CHMM, and that the CHMM advantage rests on (i) the structural use cases (regime-conditional VaR, copula composition) and (ii) the parametric privacy axis. The current framing reads as if the trade-off is symmetric; it is not.
3. **The bilinear ACF identity contribution is candid about being folklore but the body still leans heavily on it as a contribution.** §1 (Introduction) and §2.3 (Theory) cite Hamilton (1994) §22.2, Krolzig (1997) Ch. 3, and Timmermann (2000) for the identity itself, then describe the contribution as "empirical effective-rank application to the SPY 2014-2024 instance." Tightening: re-frame the spectral material as a **diagnostic** (an effective-rank diagnostic on $\hat{\mathbf{T}} - \mathbf{1}\bar{\boldsymbol{\pi}}^\top$) used to interpret the Rydén failure on this instance, and de-emphasise it as a contribution. The cross-ticker minimum of $32.6\%$ dominant-mode share on NEM (introduction) makes clear the "rank non-binding at $K \ge 3$" statement is at the median, not universal.
4. **The QuantGAN row in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} is methodologically uninformative.** Both in-house re-implementations (3-conv and 7-block TCN, with and without Lambert-W) fail at $0\%$ IS / $0\%$ OoS KS. The discussion explicitly says "We do not claim that the published reference implementation of \citet{wiese2020quantgan} would also fail; that is a deferred follow-up." Including a row that is admitted to be a re-implementation failure in the headline panel weakens the paper's scientific credibility on the deep-generative axis. Either move the QuantGAN row to the appendix and replace with "Deep generative: see Appendix~X" or run the published reference implementation before submission.
5. **The penalised CHMM-t at $\lambda=20$ is over-shrunk on three of six body tickers** ([results.tex](sections/results.tex), "Per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$ tuning"), with QQQ at $\lambda=0$ and SPY/JPM/AAPL at $\lambda=10$. The body uses uniform $\lambda=20$ as the headline penalty. Either commit to per-ticker validated $\lambda^\star$ in the body table (and report the cross-ticker panel under that recipe), or move the penalised CHMM-t out of the headline and use the shared-$\nu$ ablation as the per-state-Student-$t$ row, since the shared-$\nu$ row eliminates the IS-kurtosis overshoot completely without any penalty (Discussion, "Closing the kurtosis gap").
6. **Independent-decade validation is reported as "completed via CRSP day-pass" with the OoS pass-rate collapsing to 3-5%** ([discussion.tex](sections/discussion.tex), "Limitations"). This is a sharp negative result and deserves a brief paragraph in the body §3, not a one-sentence acknowledgment in the discussion.

### Questions for Authors
1. The shared-$\nu$ ablation in Discussion §5 produces simulated IS kurtosis of $6.25$ at $K=18$ vs. observed $7.68$ without any penalty, and the per-state $\nu_k$ design requires the $\lambda=20$ shrinkage to recover comparable behaviour. What is the substantive case for retaining per-state $\nu_k$ in the body when shared-$\nu$ is structurally cleaner and avoids the bracket-pinning artefact? "Comparability with Peel/Liu" feels like a pre-paper choice, not an ex-post justification.
2. The body $K^\star=3$ headline reports 80.5% CHMM-N OoS KS asymptotic, dropping to 58.6% under the L=20 block-bootstrap null. Should the L=20 column become the primary OoS KS column in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, with the asymptotic value relegated to a sanity-check second column?
3. Could you clarify the relationship between the $K^\star=3$ body headline and the $K=18$ "extended-state-resolution sensitivity reference"? The body retains both, but the cross-ticker generalisation in Table~\ref{tab:cross_ticker} shows the three operating points within $\sim 6$pp of each other on OoS KS at identical $11/30$ failure count. Is there a use case where $K=18$ is preferred over $K^\star=3$ that is not just kurtosis-residual cosmetic?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **Replace the in-house QuantGAN row with a faithful reference-implementation re-run** of Wiese et al. (2020) using the authors' public code (architecture, Lambert-W preprocessing, hyperparameters). If the published implementation also fails on this dataset, that is a result; if it passes, it materially changes the deep-generative comparison and would strengthen the paper.
2. **Promote the block-aware OoS KS column from Appendix~\ref{sec:ks_block_bootstrap_oos} to the body Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}.** A 25pp drop from asymptotic to L=20 on the OoS column is a large recalibration; readers should see both columns side by side in the headline panel.
3. **Add the cross-decade validation block from Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_decade_validation} as a body §3 paragraph**, not just a Limitations sentence. The 1994-2004 IS / 2004-2006 OoS construction is independent of the body window and the negative result there (3-5% OoS KS) constrains the scope claim materially.

### Minor Comments
- The keyword list at the end of the abstract includes "semi-Markov" but the body model is Markov; HSMM is a comparator, not a focal model. Consider removing.
- "Cont (2001)" stylized facts: the citation is to Cont (2001) but the discussion sometimes calls them "Cont stylized facts" without year. Standardise.
- Caption of Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} is roughly half a page long. Consider moving the four "reading guide" paragraphs into the surrounding prose and keeping only definitional caption material.
- "VWAP" is introduced in §2.1 without expansion at first use — for a finance audience this is fine, but for a methodology-oriented reviewer pool the first use should expand to "session volume-weighted average price."
- The author footnotes show two of three authors in the Smith School of Chemical and Biomolecular Engineering at Cornell. For a finance journal this is unusual; consider a one-line motivation in the introduction (synthetic data for biotech-financial pipelines, etc.) or expect cover-letter framing.

### Recommendation
**Minor Revision.** The empirical study is sound and the scaffold is a useful contribution. The headline framing needs tightening (HSMM dominance, abstract length, QuantGAN row), and the block-aware / cross-decade results should be visible in the body. None of these requires new modelling — only re-prioritising what is in the body vs. the appendices and running one reference-implementation control.

## Reviewer 2 (Hard) — econometrician focused on regime-switching estimation, VaR backtesting, and copula dependence

### Summary
The paper benchmarks four CHMM emission families against an extensive set of baselines (i.i.d. bootstrap, GARCH(1,1) with Gaussian and Student-$t$ innovations, MS-GARCH at $K \in \{2,3,4,6\}$ in two estimation flavours, ML HSMM under Pareto and Gamma sojourns, an in-house QuantGAN, and stochastic-volatility / MSM / Merton-jump baselines in appendix) on SPY and on a sector-balanced 30-ticker panel. The headline contribution is operational rather than methodological: a unified estimation scaffold and an empirical effective-rank application of the textbook bilinear ACF identity to recast the Rydén (1998) low-$K$ failure, plus a regime-conditional VaR that passes Christoffersen-cc on the bulk of the panel. Several inferential and design choices warrant scrutiny before publication.

### Strengths
1. **K-selection is unusually rigorous.** Six criteria (held-out per-observation log-likelihood, held-out KS, AIC, BIC, HQC, CAIC) on two slices, plus four-fold and six-fold rolling-origin CV on the strictly pre-2020 slice, plus a Newey-West HAC variance on the paired Diebold-Mariano-style differential across folds (Appendix~\ref{sec:k_selection_hac}). The $K^\star=3$ vs $K^\star=6$ resolution under k-fold CV ($|z| \le 0.07$ with sign flipping between fold designs) is the right way to set a state-resolution-robust default.
2. **Multiple-testing correction across the conditional-coverage panel** ([var_backtest.tex](sections/var_backtest.tex), "Multiple-testing correction") with both Benjamini-Hochberg and Bonferroni is correctly executed and addresses the obvious objection to the "$19/24$ pass" panel-level claim.
3. **Block-bootstrap KS recalibration on both windows** with critical values reported alongside the asymptotic value, anchored on both $R_{\text{IS}}$ and $R_{\text{OoS}}$.
4. **The Christoffersen-cc + Engle-Manganelli DQ pairing** is the right test combination at $T_{\text{OoS}}=572$. The DQ rejection at $K=18$, $\alpha=0.01$ ($p=0.017$) is honestly reported even though it contradicts the "passes cleanly" framing the body adopts at $\alpha=0.05$.

### Weaknesses
1. **The body headline "passes Christoffersen-cc cleanly at $\alpha = 0.05$" understates the $\alpha = 0.01$ DQ rejection.** The conditional-coverage Section §3.4 paragraph "Power caveat at $\alpha = 0.01$" and Table~\ref{tab:cond_var} footnote ‡ acknowledge that the higher-power DQ test rejects at $K=18$, $\alpha=0.01$. For a risk-management application the strict-tail $\alpha = 0.01$ row is the substantive metric, not the moderate-tail $\alpha = 0.05$ row. The abstract should report the DQ result, not the Christoffersen-cc result, at $\alpha=0.01$. The current abstract attributes the $\alpha = 0.01$ DQ rejection to the over-coupling of high-volatility states at $K=18$, but the rejection is on the *stationary* OoS slice, not on a stress fold. Fix: state in the abstract that conditional VaR coverage is supported at $\alpha=0.05$ but rejected by DQ at $\alpha=0.01$ for $K=18$.
2. **The cross-asset OoS off-diagonal MAE is 7-10x the IS value even after quarterly refit, but the abstract reports only the IS value.** §3.6 Table~\ref{tab:cross_asset} reports IS off-diag MAE $0.027$ vs OoS $0.209$ (static IS-fit) and $0.185$ (quarterly refit). The $0.185$ is still nearly $7\times$ the IS $0.027$. The per-pair breakdown in [cross_asset_appendix.tex](sections/cross_asset_appendix.tex) Table~\ref{tab:per_pair_offdiag_mae} shows the OoS deterioration is concentrated in JNJ-equity-factor pairs ($|\Delta| \in [0.244, 0.544]$), not distributed evenly. The body's "the dependence-family choice is dominated by the refit-cadence choice on OoS" reading is too generous — even the refit-cadence choice closes only $\sim 10\%$ of the IS-OoS gap in MAE units. The cross-asset construction should be presented as IS-calibrated only, with the OoS extension treated as a documented limitation analogous to the GLD/SLV stress test.
3. **The $K=18$ result is partly nominal: $K_{\text{eff}} = 11/18$ for CHMM-N and $12/18$ for CHMM-t under the standardized-distance single-linkage diagnostic, with $13/18$ CHMM-t states pinned at the upper $\nu$ bracket on SPY** ([discussion.tex](sections/discussion.tex), "Practical identifiability"). The discussion acknowledges this and reports a $K_{\text{nominal}}=11$ rebuild that "matches or slightly exceeds" the $K=18$ row on every axis. If $K=11$ is the structurally cleaner expression of the same model, $K=11$ should be the body row, not $K=18$. The current "$K=18$ extended-state-resolution sensitivity reference (multi-objective rule that uses the OoS window)" framing is a defensible last resort but it is not the cleanest scientific choice; the body should report $K_{\text{eff}}$ as the primary row.
4. **The IS-kurtosis overshoot at unpenalised CHMM-t ($14.91$ vs observed $7.68$) is a more serious mis-calibration than the discussion suggests.** The bracket-lift to $\nu_{\min}=4$ shaves only $\sim 1$ kurtosis unit (Remark~\ref{rem:bracket_lift}). The shared-$\nu$ ablation completely fixes the overshoot ($6.25$ at $K=18$ vs observed $7.68$, no penalty needed). The body retains the per-state $\nu_k$ design "for direct comparability with Peel/Liu" — but Peel-Liu is the standard for finite-mixture-of-$t$ in the time-series literature. The paper's empirical evidence is that the per-state $\nu_k$ design produces a kurtosis-overshoot artefact that requires hand-tuned shrinkage to compensate, and the shared-$\nu$ alternative produces a cleaner fit without any tuning. This argues against the per-state $\nu_k$ design as the body recommendation.
5. **The penalised CHMM-t at uniform $\lambda=20$ is over-shrunk on $3/6$ body tickers** (per-ticker tuning Table~\ref{tab:per_ticker_lambda}). The body table reports the uniform $\lambda=20$ result as a "headline match"; the per-ticker $\lambda^\star$ should be the body recipe and the uniform $\lambda$ should be a sensitivity reference. Currently the operational recipe is buried in a single paragraph in §3.5.
6. **The asymptotic-vs-block-bootstrap OoS KS gap is large enough to materially change the headline.** Table~\ref{tab:ks_block_body} reports CHMM-N OoS KS dropping from $79.8\%$ asymptotic to $58.6\%$ at $L=20$. The body abstract claims "$80.5\%$ OoS KS" — that is the asymptotic-pass-rate column under an iid null. Under a temporally-aware null, CHMM-N is at "passes about half the time." The abstract should report the L=20 number or, at minimum, both columns side by side.
7. **The DM-CRPS multi-day result at $h=20$ is admitted to be K=18-specific to SPY** ([results.tex](sections/results.tex), "Bootstrap as a non-parametric synthetic-data benchmark"). The body honestly reports that the result does not replicate at $K^\star=3$ ($p=0.244$) or across the six-asset universe ($p>0.45$ for every asset). This is appropriate disclosure; but the body still leans on the multi-day DM result as differentiating evidence in the panel-comparison narrative. The narrative should be tightened: at the body-headline operating point and across assets, the CHMM does not detectably beat the bootstrap on either 1-day KS or h-day cumulative-CRPS. The remaining advantage is on the structural use cases.

### Questions for Authors
1. **Why is the $\nu_{\min}=2.1$ bracket retained in the body when it sits below the kurtosis-finiteness threshold $\nu=4$?** The bracket-lift ablation Remark~\ref{rem:bracket_lift} shows the kurtosis overshoot persists even at $\nu_{\min}=4$, but it also shows IS / OoS KS are within sampling error. Why not run the body at $\nu_{\min}=4$ with simulated IS kurtosis well-defined?
2. **What is the operational case for reporting both $K^\star=3$ headline and $K=18$ sensitivity reference if they are within $\sim 6$pp on cross-ticker OoS KS at identical $11/30$ failure count?** The kurtosis residual difference (Table~\ref{tab:cross_ticker}: $7.18$ at $K^\star=3$ vs $0.61$ at $K=18$) is the only differentiator, but observed kurtosis itself sits in a bootstrap CI of $[0.90, 12.40]$ ([results.tex](sections/results.tex), "Descriptive Statistics"). At that CI width, both kurtosis values are inside the envelope. Why not collapse to a single body operating point?
3. **The full one-shot copula MLE in Appendix~\ref{sec:full_tcopula_mle} returns $\hat\nu_{\text{full}} = 6.40$ vs $\hat\nu_{\text{two-step}}=6.00$, a $+5.55$ log-likelihood improvement.** Why is the two-step Kendall's-$\tau$ estimator the body construction rather than the full MLE? The body cross-asset table currently reports the two-step result; the appendix justifies it by saying $|\Delta\rho_{ij}| \le 0.025$ for all 15 off-diagonals, but a $+5.55$ log-likelihood is not a small improvement.
4. **The conditional-VaR construction holds $(\mathbf{T}, \{\boldsymbol\theta_k\})$ fixed at the IS fit and propagates only the one-step-ahead state probability through the OoS observations.** Have you compared this to a fully refit conditional VaR (online-EM, Cappé 2011 cited but not used)? The paper notes "faster refit cadences do not close the W2 stress-fold rejection" in Appendix~\ref{sec:walkforward_refit_cadence}, but it is not clear whether online-EM (which updates $(\mathbf{T}, \{\boldsymbol\theta_k\})$ continuously, not at fixed cadences) was tried.

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **Promote the block-aware OoS KS column to the body Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}** alongside the asymptotic column. The 25pp gap is too large to report only the asymptotic.
2. **Re-run the body cross-ticker panel with per-ticker $\hat\lambda^\star$** (not uniform $\lambda=20$) and report the OoS KS distribution and kurtosis-residual distribution under that recipe. The current $11/30$ failure count under uniform $\lambda$ may shift under the validated recipe.
3. **Add a body comparison row for shared-$\nu$ CHMM-t at $K^\star=3$ and $K=18$** in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, alongside the per-state penalised version. This is currently in Appendix~\ref{sec:chmm_t_shared_nu}, Table~\ref{tab:chmm_t_shared_nu}; the headline kurtosis cleanliness claim attaches to this row, not to the per-state $\nu_k + \lambda=20$ row.
4. **Report the conditional-VaR DQ statistic alongside Christoffersen-cc in Table~\ref{tab:cond_var}** (currently only Christoffersen-cc is in the body and DQ is in Appendix~\ref{sec:engle_manganelli_dq}). At $T_{\text{OoS}}=572$, DQ is the substantive test at $\alpha=0.01$.

### Minor Comments
- §1 and §3.1 both quote IS excess kurtosis $7.68$ to two decimals; the bootstrap CI is $[2.17, 12.40]$. Quote to one decimal in the body to avoid implying false precision.
- The walk-forward body summary Table~\ref{tab:walkforward_body} reports median KS $62.1\%$ at $K^\star=3$ with $[Q_1, Q_3] = [7.2, 78.4]\%$. The IQR width of $\sim 71$pp is enormous and should be flagged in the prose; readers may not register that $Q_1 = 7.2\%$ is essentially a categorical fail.
- Caption of Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} contains nine paragraphs of metadata (the QuantGAN footnote alone is a full paragraph). Consider a separate "Methods notes for Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}" sub-section in §3.
- The phrase "operationally informative" appears repeatedly (abstract, §3, discussion). Pick one occurrence and excise the rest.
- §3.6 reports "the IS calibration distinction ($0.027$ vs $0.030$, $0.003$ difference) is an IS-only finding and the dependence-family selection on OoS is not supported at this $N_{\text{paths}}$." This is correct; consider also reporting the simulation-noise floor estimate (the appendix's bootstrap CI on the off-diagonal MAE) to make the "below the simulation-noise floor" claim quantitative.

### Recommendation
**Major Revision.** The empirical work is rigorous but several body-headline claims overstate what the data support: the Christoffersen-cc pass at $\alpha=0.01$ (DQ-rejected at $K=18$), the asymptotic OoS KS pass rate (drops 25pp under block-aware null), and the uniform $\lambda=20$ headline (over-shrunk on half the tickers). The K=18 vs K_eff=11 issue and the per-state vs shared-$\nu$ design choice both deserve body-level resolution rather than appendix relegation. The paper has the right experiments; it needs to re-prioritise what is in the body table.

## Reviewer 3 (Very Hard) — competing-method specialist (MS-GARCH, deep generators, regime-switching VaR)

### Summary
The paper presents a CHMM scaffold with four emission families as a synthetic-data generator for daily US-equity returns, claiming reproduction of the symmetric Cont (2001) stylized facts and a regime-conditional VaR pass at $\alpha=0.05$. Most of the body claims are heavily qualified in the discussion and appendices, and the cumulative effect of the qualifications is that the paper's distinctive positive contributions reduce to (i) a software-engineering scaffold around four well-known emission families and (ii) an empirical re-statement of the textbook bilinear ACF identity on one ticker. The novelty relative to the existing regime-switching and deep-generative literature is unclear, and several headline comparisons are weakened by the comparators being in-house re-implementations rather than reference runs. I recommend major revision pending substantive new evidence on at least the deep-generative and HSMM axes.

### Strengths
1. **Unusually transparent self-criticism.** The discussion explicitly reports that (i) i.i.d. bootstrap dominates raw 1-day OoS KS, (ii) ML HSMM beats every CHMM operating point on OoS KS, (iii) $K_{\text{eff}}=11$ at $K=18$ on SPY, (iv) cross-decade transfer collapses to 3-5% OoS KS, (v) non-equity transfer collapses to 0% OoS KS, (vi) the multi-day DM-CRPS result is K=18-specific to SPY, (vii) the conditional VaR is DQ-rejected at $\alpha=0.01$ for $K=18$. These admissions are appropriate but they raise the question of what positive claim the paper actually supports.
2. The k-fold rolling-origin CV with HAC variance adjustment for state-count selection is well-executed.
3. The $n=6$ per-sector ANOVA expansion (60-ticker panel) addresses a power objection cleanly and the result is honest ($\eta^2$ drops by half, no sector effect detectable at adequate power).

### Weaknesses
1. **The "spectral mechanism" contribution is acknowledged folklore.** §1 (Introduction) and §2.3 (Theory) explicitly cite Hamilton (1994) §22.2, Krolzig (1997) Ch. 3, and Timmermann (2000) for the bilinear ACF identity, then describe the contribution as "applying the textbook closed-form mixture-of-eigenvalues identity to the SPY 2014-2024 instance of the empirical Rydén (1998) low-$K$ failure and demonstrating empirically that the algebraic rank bound is non-binding at $K \ge 3$ on this instance." This is a single-ticker empirical observation. The cross-ticker panel ([introduction.tex](sections/introduction.tex)) reports the 30-ticker median dominant-mode share at $0.756$, IQR $[0.66, 0.86]$, minimum $0.326$ on NEM, with SPY's $0.936$ in the right tail. The "rank-non-binding at the cross-ticker median" claim is then the actual contribution; on $\sim 1/3$ of tickers in the panel the dominant mode carries less than $66\%$ of the lag-1 ACF and the rank bound is non-trivially binding. This is not a strong empirical claim and the body framing oversells it.
2. **The "unified four-family scaffold" novelty is software, not statistics.** Student-$t$ HMM (Peel/Liu 2000), Laplace-emission HMM (folklore), and GED-emission HMM (Subbotin 1923, Box-Tiao 1973, used widely in financial volatility modelling) are all pre-existing in the literature. The contribution is the unified ECM scaffold and the quantile-based initialisation. Within-CHMM Diebold-Mariano on OoS sample-CRPS gives $p > 0.45$ across all three pairs (abstract, [results.tex](sections/results.tex)). The four families are statistically indistinguishable on OoS predictive performance. The body's pivot to "the contribution is the unified swap interface itself rather than four substantively-distinct points on the OoS distributional axis" is candid but it reduces the contribution to a code library. For a methodological journal that is a thin claim.
3. **The QuantGAN comparison is uninformative.** Two in-house WGAN re-implementations (3-conv and 7-block TCN, with and without Lambert-W input pre-processing) both fail at $0\%$ IS / $0\%$ OoS KS. The body and discussion explicitly disclaim that "We do not claim that a faithful reference-implementation re-run of \citet{wiese2020quantgan} would also fail; that is a deferred follow-up." No deep-generative comparator other than QuantGAN is run. The deep-generative literature has moved well beyond QuantGAN: time-series diffusion models (Rasul et al. 2021, cited but not run), normalizing-flow-based generators, score-based models. None of these are exercised in the panel. The paper's claim of differentiating against deep generators rests on an admitted-failure in-house re-implementation of one architecture from one 2020 paper.
4. **HSMM dominates on the headline distributional metric and the framing as "complementary scaffolds" is rhetorical.** ML HSMM-N at $K^\star=3$ attains $98.4\%$ IS / $91.0\%$ OoS KS in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison}, beating every CHMM operating point on both axes. The body argues that HSMM's $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE matches the i.i.d. baseline; but the Gamma-sojourn HSMM at $K=18$ ([results.tex](sections/results.tex), "ML HSMM as a co-headline result") attains the lowest $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE in the panel ($0.0462$). The honest claim is that HSMM dominates CHMM on Cont stylized fact reproduction at every operating point that has been tried in this paper. The "complementary" framing relies on the regime-conditional VaR construction being CHMM-specific — but the construction is a state-filter $\to$ predictive-mixture pipeline that applies equally to HSMM with the obvious modifications. Why is HSMM not the body model?
5. **Scope is narrow and the within-scope failures are extensive.** The headline scope is "daily US equities." (a) Non-equity (GLD/SLV) collapses to $0\%$ OoS KS; (b) cross-decade (1994-2004 / 2004-2006) collapses to $3-5\%$ OoS KS; (c) two of six walk-forward folds (W2 COVID, W4 2022 rate-hike) collapse to $<10\%$ OoS KS; (d) within the 30-ticker panel, $11/30$ tickers fail OoS KS at the standard threshold. The model class is fragile to any regime not represented in IS, and the "operational deployment" recipe (quarterly refit) closes only part of the gap. The paper should either narrow the title scope to "stationary-window daily US equities" or present a more honest summary of the within-scope failure rate.
6. **The conditional VaR rejection at $\alpha=0.01$ should be in the abstract.** The Engle-Manganelli DQ test rejects conditional coverage at $K=18$, $\alpha=0.01$ ($p=0.017$) on the headline OoS window, and the body acknowledges DQ is the higher-power test at this $T_{\text{OoS}}$. For a paper marketed on a regime-conditional VaR contribution, the $\alpha=0.01$ result is the substantive risk-management diagnostic. The abstract reports the $\alpha=0.05$ pass and walks the $\alpha=0.01$ result back to the W2/W4 stress folds, but the K=18 OoS rejection is on the *stationary* slice, not on a stress fold.
7. **The per-state $\nu_k$ design is dominated by the shared-$\nu$ alternative on the body's own evidence.** Discussion §5 reports that shared-$\nu$ at $K=18$ produces simulated IS kurtosis $6.25$ vs observed $7.68$ without any penalty, while per-state $\nu_k$ requires the $\lambda=20$ shrinkage to land at IS kurtosis $8.56$. The bracket-lift ablation only shaves $\sim 1$ kurtosis unit. The retention of per-state $\nu_k$ "for direct comparability with Peel/Liu" is not a methodological argument; the paper's own evidence is that per-state $\nu_k$ produces a bracket-pinning artefact that requires hand-tuned shrinkage to compensate. The shared-$\nu$ row should be the body recommendation for the Student-$t$ family.
8. **The K=18 row reports against nominal $K$ rather than $K_{\text{eff}}=11$**, with $13/18$ CHMM-t states pinned at the upper $\nu$ bracket on SPY ([algorithms_appendix.tex](sections/algorithms_appendix.tex), §A.1.4 "CHMM-t per-state $\nu_k$"). The discussion acknowledges that AIC/BIC at nominal $K$ over-credits the model and that a $K=11$ rebuild matches or exceeds the $K=18$ row on every axis. The honest body row is $K=11$, not $K=18$.

### Questions for Authors
1. **Why not use HSMM as the body model?** The Gamma-sojourn HSMM at $K=18$ achieves lower $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE than any CHMM row in the panel and the Pareto-sojourn HSMM at $K^\star=3$ attains $91.0\%$ OoS KS, beating every CHMM operating point. The regime-conditional VaR construction extends to HSMM trivially (the state-filter is independent of the sojourn distribution). What is the empirical case for CHMM over HSMM on the body's own metrics?
2. **The headline cross-asset OoS off-diagonal MAE of $0.209$ (or $0.185$ under quarterly refit) versus IS $0.027$ is a factor of 7-10 in MAE units.** What is the operational interpretation of a model whose simulated correlation matrix differs from the realised correlation matrix by an order of magnitude more on OoS than on IS? Is this a synthetic-data generator or an IS-fit diagnostic dressed as one?
3. **How does CHMM compare to a vanilla GARCH(1,1)-Student-$t$ on the conditional VaR Christoffersen-cc test?** GARCH(1,1)-Student-$t$ is in Table~\ref{tab:model_comparison} (IS kurtosis $15.13$, OoS KS $80.8\%$, $|G_t|$ ACF-MAE $0.0316$ — best in the panel) but its conditional VaR backtest is not reported. Volatility-clustering models with heavy-tailed innovations are the standard regime-conditional VaR baseline; the comparison is missing.
4. **Why is the panel limited to MS-GARCH up to $K=6$ and the in-house QuantGAN?** Where are time-series diffusion models, normalizing flows, MS-skew-GARCH, factor stochastic volatility, the recent regime-switching deep models?
5. **The leverage-effect coverage in the discussion is borderline-rejected on IS and rejected on OoS** — so the paper's headline claim is "three of three symmetric Cont (2001) stylized facts at $K \ge 3$" but the asymmetric stylized facts are out of scope and the model rejects the OoS observed leverage at the $5\%$ level. Is the synthetic-data-generator claim actually well-defined under this scope restriction?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **A faithful reference-implementation re-run of Wiese et al. (2020) QuantGAN** with the published architecture, Lambert-W preprocessing, and hyperparameters. Without this, the deep-generative comparison is uninformative. If the published implementation also fails on this dataset, that is publishable; if it passes, the panel headline changes.
2. **A direct comparison with Markov-switching GARCH using the body's regime-conditional VaR construction** — i.e., apply the state-filter pipeline of Eq.~\ref{eq:filter} to the MS-GARCH(1,1) state forecast and run Christoffersen-cc on the resulting predictive quantile. This isolates the regime-switching value proposition from the CHMM scaffold and tests whether the conditional VaR result is CHMM-specific or generic to multi-state models.
3. **Replace the body $K=18$ row with the $K=K_{\text{eff}}=11$ rebuild** documented in Appendix~\ref{sec:k_eff_rebuild}, and report the AIC / BIC under $K_{\text{eff}}$ in the body. Retain $K=18$ only as a sanity check in the appendix.
4. **A unified body recommendation for the Student-$t$ family** that resolves the per-state $\nu_k$ vs shared-$\nu$ choice on the paper's own evidence. The shared-$\nu$ row should be the headline if the per-state $\nu_k$ design requires a hand-tuned penalty to recover comparable performance.

### Minor Comments
- The author affiliations show two of three authors in chemical and biomolecular engineering. For a finance-econometrics journal a brief framing in the introduction explaining why a CHMM scaffold is being benchmarked from this perspective would help reviewers calibrate.
- The reference list is heavy on classics (Hamilton 1989, Bollerslev 1986, Engle 1982) and lighter on the last decade of regime-switching, deep-generative finance, and conditional VaR literature. The cited range includes Ardia 2019 (MSGARCH package), Wiese 2020 (QuantGAN), Yoon 2019 (TimeGAN), Rasul 2021 (autoregressive diffusion), Kwon 2024, but no comparison to or citation of regime-switching deep models, score-based generators for time series, or recent (2022-2025) work on MS-GARCH or HSMM in finance.
- "Body headline" appears 30+ times. Pick one (e.g., "headline operating point") and standardise.
- The footnote daggerdaggerdagger marker on Table~\ref{tab:cond_var} for the bracket-lift row is a typesetting nuisance; consider re-organising the footnotes.
- The $5/24$ vs $19/24$ walk-forward Christoffersen-cc framing in [var_backtest.tex](sections/var_backtest.tex) shifts to "$32/32$ tests under BH excluding W2 and W4" in the multiple-testing paragraph. Pick one denominator for the abstract.

### Recommendation
**Major Revision** (borderline Reject). The empirical work is honest and thorough, but the cumulative weight of acknowledged limitations leaves the positive contribution as a software scaffold around well-known emission families. The cleanest path to acceptance is a substantive new comparison (HSMM as body model, faithful QuantGAN reference run, MS-GARCH-conditional VaR) that lets the paper report a positive differentiating result rather than a long list of qualifications around a weak baseline ranking.
