## Reviewer 1 — Moderate

*Senior researcher in financial econometrics and applied time-series modelling; sympathetic to regime-switching approaches and synthetic-data tooling; reads carefully for soundness and clarity but gives benefit of the doubt on borderline points.*

### Summary
The paper proposes a continuous-emission HMM (CHMM) at moderate state count ($K = 18$) as a single-scaffold synthetic generator for daily equity returns, contributes a closed-form spectral identity for the absolute-return ACF, and extends the unified Baum-Welch / ECM machinery across four emission families (Gaussian, Student-t, Laplace, GED). Empirical validation on SPY plus five additional tickers and a Student-t copula extension is thorough and the writing is clear. The contribution is incremental but real, and the paper is a useful addition to the regime-switching synthetic-data literature.

### Strengths
1. **The spectral identity in equation (\ref{eq:acf_normalised}) is clean, correct as stated, and pedagogically valuable.** The recasting of the Rydén (1998) low-$K$ limitation as a $K$-rank statement on $\mathbf{T}$ is the kind of structural result that helps a reader reason about regime-switching ACF behaviour. The full derivation in Appendix A (Theorem 1) is self-contained and the lag-zero discussion (within-state vs between-state variance ratio) is a nice touch.
2. **The unified four-family scaffold (CHMM-N / -t / -L / -GED) is well organized.** Algorithm 1 in `algorithms_appendix.tex` makes the architectural claim immediate: a single EM engine with an emission-family branch at three lines. The CHMM-GED variant is the genuinely novel emission, with a sensible three-stage ECM and clean boundary nesting at $p = 1, 2$.
3. **The empirical panel covers the right competitors.** The headline table includes the i.i.d.\ bootstrap (distributional ceiling), Gaussian / Laplace i.i.d., GARCH(1,1), GARCH(1,1)-$t$, and MS-GARCH; Appendix `baselines_appendix.tex` extends to EGARCH, GJR-GARCH, HAR-RV, MS-GARCH $K=3$, the SM-CHMM foil, and a QuantGAN deep-generative row. Reporting all of these on a single seven-metric panel under a uniform simulation pipeline is exactly what the synthetic-data literature needs.
4. **The block-bootstrap KS recalibration (`baselines_appendix.tex`, Table `tab:ks_block_bootstrap`) is a meaningful methodological contribution.** Acknowledging that the asymptotic two-sample KS test assumes i.i.d.\ samples and showing the block-bootstrap critical values, plus the mean p-value distribution, is exactly the kind of robustness check the field has been missing.
5. **Reproducibility infrastructure is well developed.** A single seed root (`20260420`), a public `CHMM-Model.jl` Julia package, a `Manifest.toml`, and a one-command rebuild script are all stated in `supplementary.tex` Section A.1. This is above the bar for the venue.

### Weaknesses

1. **Headline KS numbers in the abstract do not match any single row in Table `tab:model_comparison`.** The abstract reports "$93.8\%$ in-sample and $84.2\%$ out-of-sample" CHMM KS pass rates, but Table 1 shows CHMM-N at $K = 18$ is $94.1$ / $81.8$, CHMM-t is $95.6$ / $85.7$, CHMM-L is $93.6$ / $80.8$, and CHMM-GED is $95.2$ / $84.3$. The Discussion section (`discussion.tex`, line 1) repeats $93.8\%$ / $84.2\%$. Inside the Results section (`results.tex`, line 75) the prose cites "$94.6\%$ IS KS and $84.7\%$ OoS KS" for CHMM-N. None of these three sets of numbers agrees with the others. *Fix*: pick one canonical headline, point to the row in Table 1 that delivers it, and remove the discrepant numbers from the abstract and discussion. If $93.8 / 84.2$ is the four-variant mean, state that.

2. **The "$K = 18$ operating point chosen via a multi-objective criterion that uses the 2024-2026 OoS window" is acknowledged but the implication is under-stated.** The Supplementary Material (`supplementary.tex`, "Pre-OoS validation $K$-selection" paragraph) admits explicitly that the headline $K = 18$ was selected partly on the same window subsequently used to evaluate OoS KS and VaR, and that a clean re-selection on a non-OoS validation slice picks $K^\star = 3$ by held-out log-likelihood and BIC. This is information leakage. The manuscript handles it by reporting the $K = 3$ row alongside, which is fine, but the abstract still leads with $K = 18$ numbers. *Fix*: either lead with $K = 3$ as the primary operating point and treat $K = 18$ as the tail-fidelity ablation, or re-run the $K$-selection on a held-out slice that does not overlap the OoS window and re-report headline numbers at the held-out-selected $K$. Acknowledge this as a major caveat in the abstract.

3. **The "ACF-MAE within $0.003$ of GARCH" claim in the abstract is the closest CHMM row only.** Table 1 shows CHMM-N ACF-MAE of $0.0509$ versus GARCH $0.0485$ (gap $0.0024$, close to "$0.003$"), but CHMM-L is $0.0567$ (gap $0.0082$) and CHMM-GED is $0.0548$ (gap $0.0063$). GARCH(1,1)-$t$ is at $0.0316$ — strictly better than every CHMM row by a clear margin. The paper's narrative that CHMM "essentially matches GARCH on the temporal axis" is true for CHMM-N and overstates the case for the heavy-tailed CHMMs and against GARCH-$t$. *Fix*: revise the abstract sentence to read "an absolute-return ACF MAE within $0.003$ of GARCH(1,1) for the Gaussian-emission CHMM-N", and add one sentence acknowledging GARCH(1,1)-$t$ as the best ACF row in the panel.

4. **The cross-ticker generalization shows simulated kurtosis blow-ups of one to two orders of magnitude that the body does not address.** Table `tab:cross_ticker` shows simulated kurtosis of $46.40$ for NVDA (observed $5.5$), $99.66$ for JNJ (observed $9.0$), and $18.82$ for JPM (observed $7.6$). Only the SPY row ($14.68$ simulated vs $7.7$ observed) is discussed in the body. The discussion section (`discussion.tex`) frames the OoS KS cliffs on NVDA / JPM as a stationarity-scope issue, but the IS simulated kurtosis values themselves are an order of magnitude off observed — this is not a stationarity issue, it is a CHMM-t fitting artefact on these tickers. *Fix*: add a paragraph to Section 4.4 (`results.tex`, sec:cross_asset_univariate) explicitly addressing the cross-ticker simulated-kurtosis pattern, and either show that CHMM-N or CHMM-GED do not exhibit it (per-family per-ticker table in an appendix) or run the $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage prior at $\lambda = 20$ on every cross-ticker fit and update Table `tab:cross_ticker`.

5. **No conditional-coverage VaR test, only unconditional Kupiec.** Section `var_backtest.tex` reports $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}}$ values cleanly but the Christoffersen (1998) conditional-coverage test (already in `references.bib` as `christoffersen1998evaluating`) is missing. For a regime-switching VaR claim, conditional coverage and breach independence are the directly relevant diagnostics: a model can pass Kupiec by total breach count while clustering breaches in the wrong way. *Fix*: add the Christoffersen conditional-coverage statistic to Table `tab:var_es` for at least the IS window. The reference is already cited.

6. **The four-variant decision guide in Table `tab:variant_choice` over-promises.** The recommendation "CHMM-t for tail-conditional consumers" is hard to defend given that CHMM-t is the variant with the systematic kurtosis overshoot (14.4 IS, vs observed 7.7), the largest cross-ticker simulated-kurtosis blow-ups, and the one variant whose seed-to-seed std is more than ten times that of the other three (Table `tab:multiseed_headline`: $1.95$ for CHMM-t versus $0.05$--$0.09$ for the rest). *Fix*: either recommend the penalised CHMM-t (with $\lambda = 20$ shrinkage prior) as the per-state heavy-tail option, or replace the CHMM-t row with CHMM-GED in that line of Table `tab:variant_choice`.

7. **The figure caption typography of Figure `fig:stylized_facts` panels conflicts with the project-level convention recorded in the user's auto-memory** (subfigure labels at the bottom, no top titles). Most figures comply; spot-check the in-Julia-PDFs for residual top titles and update the captions to match `(a)/(b)/(c)/(d)` convention uniformly. (Out of scope for the science but flagged for consistency with project standards.)

### Questions for Authors
1. The held-out re-selection in Supp Section A.7 picks $K^\star = 3$ on the 2022-2023 validation slice, but the slice is "itself partly a structural-break period (the rate-hike cycle)". Have you tried selecting on a pre-2020 validation slice (e.g., 2018-2019) to remove the rate-hike confound? What $K^\star$ does that pick?
2. Table `tab:multiseed_headline` reports a CHMM-t simulated-kurtosis std of $1.95$ across 10 seeds. What is the seed-to-seed std of the per-state $\hat\nu_k$ at the lower bracket? Is the bracket-pinning behaviour stable, or does the identity of the pinned state(s) move?
3. The Student-t copula profile-MLE sweep is on the discrete grid $\nu \in \{2,3,4,5,6,8,10,15,20,30\}$. Is the optimum on a finer continuous search materially different from $\nu^\star = 6$? Or is the profile log-likelihood flat enough that $\nu^\star = 5$ or $\nu^\star = 8$ would be statistically indistinguishable?
4. The OoS off-diagonal correlation MAE jumps from $0.027$ IS to $0.209$ OoS, and the gap localises in three pairs involving JNJ. What does a rolling-window refit of the Student-t copula (e.g., quarterly cadence as suggested in Section 5) deliver on the same pairs? Even a single back-of-envelope number would clarify how much of the gap is genuine OoS regime shift versus a fixed-window artefact.

## Reviewer 2 — Hard

*Constraint-based / time-series methodologist with deep MLE, mixture-model, and copula expertise. Scrutinises mathematical formulation, identifiability claims, and baseline fairness; demands the paper defend its choices.*

### Summary
The paper claims a closed-form spectral identity for the absolute-return ACF of a stationary CHMM, four emission families under a unified Baum-Welch / ECM scaffold, and empirical reproduction of the three Cont stylized facts on SPY plus a six-asset Student-t copula extension. The mathematics is mostly correct but parts are over-claimed; the empirical comparisons have non-trivial fairness issues; and several "robustness" exercises are framed as checks but actually reveal genuine limitations of the proposed method that are not adequately surfaced in the headline.

### Strengths
1. The closed-form spectral identity (Theorem 1, Supp Section A.4) is correctly derived, the lag-zero discontinuity argument via eigenvalue completeness is technically clean, and the squared-return analogue and the non-diagonalisable extension are noted as remarks rather than swept under the rug.
2. The CHMM-GED three-stage ECM (`algorithms_appendix.tex`, Equations \ref{eq:ged_mu}--\ref{eq:ged_p}) is constructed correctly: the closed-form scale at fixed $(\mu, p)$, the bracketed location at fixed $(\sigma, p)$, and the bracketed shape at fixed $(\mu, \sigma)$ each preserve $Q$-monotonicity. The boundary cases at $p = 1$ and $p = 2$ being recovered exactly is a genuine architectural feature.
3. The CRPS column with Diebold-Mariano test on the loss differential (`metrics_appendix.tex`, Section A.3.1, Newey-West HAC variance) is the right complement to the KS pass-rate metric, and the result that CHMM-N is statistically indistinguishable from CHMM-t / CHMM-L on this proper scoring rule is methodologically honest.

### Weaknesses

1. **Theorem 1 (`supplementary.tex` Section A.4) is presented as the central technical contribution but the result is essentially folklore in the regime-switching literature.** Hamilton's "*Time Series Analysis*" textbook (1994) and Krolzig's "*Markov-Switching Vector Autoregressions*" (1997) both derive eigenvalue-based moment formulas for two-state and $K$-state Markov-switching models; Timmermann (2000), already cited in `references.bib` as `timmermann2000moments`, gives the moment generating function and absolute / squared-return autocovariance structure for general Markov-switching specifications, including the spectral content. The closed-form bilinear identity $\mathbf{m}^\top \mathbf{D}\, \mathbf{T}^\tau\, \mathbf{m}$ in equation (\ref{eq:supp_bilinear}) and its eigen-decomposition is direct from the Markov-chain spectral decomposition, which is textbook material (Levin-Peres, already cited). The novel content is the explicit recasting of Rydén's failure as a $K$-rank statement and the empirical demonstration that the rank constraint is the binding axis at moderate $K$ — that contribution is real, but it is a re-framing of an existing computational object, not a new identity. *Fix*: revise the "central technical contribution" framing in Section 3.3 (`theory.tex`) and the abstract to credit Timmermann (2000) and the textbook spectral derivations explicitly, and re-position the contribution as the rank-axis reframing plus the empirical instantiation, not as the identity itself.

2. **Identifiability of the CHMM-GED model with bracketed per-state $p_k$ does not follow as cleanly from Allman, Matias and Rhodes (2009) and Yakowitz-Spragins (1968) as Proposition A.2 claims.** The Allman-Matias-Rhodes 2009 result requires that the per-state emission densities are linearly independent in the space of densities on $\mathbb{R}$. For the GED family, location-scale-shape identifiability holds when the shape is fixed, but with a continuous per-state shape parameter $p_k$ the location and shape can trade off in subtle ways (a Gaussian-like emission with inflated scale can mimic a Laplace-like emission with deflated scale at intermediate quantiles), and the linear-independence argument requires a more careful joint identifiability proof. The same caveat applies to CHMM-t with per-state $\nu_k$ where the standard reference is Lindsay (1995) for finite $t$-mixtures, not Yakowitz-Spragins. The one-sentence "proof sketches" in Proposition A.2 are insufficient. *Fix*: either provide an explicit identifiability argument for CHMM-GED that handles the continuous $p_k$ axis (e.g., via the moment-generating function over the bracketed range) or downgrade Proposition A.2 to a conjecture and cite the closest available result with the gap acknowledged.

3. **The MS-GARCH baseline at $K = 2$ is unfair to MS-GARCH and to the regime-switching literature.** The CHMM is run at $K = 18$; the MS-GARCH is run at $K = 2$ in the headline panel and $K = 3$ in Appendix `tab:extended_baselines`. The Haas-Mittnik-Paolella 2004 specification scales to higher $K$ at modest cost (MS-GARCH is widely fit at $K = 3$ or $K = 4$ in the empirical risk literature). At $K = 2$ the MS-GARCH will trivially fail KS for the same Rydén-style mixture-rank reason that the paper diagnoses for low-$K$ HMM. The right comparison is MS-GARCH at the same $K$ as the CHMM, or at the largest $K$ at which MS-GARCH converges — not $K = 2$. The "regime-switching foil" framing is therefore weak. *Fix*: refit MS-GARCH at $K \in \{6, 9, 12\}$ using a standard package (e.g., `MSGARCH` in R, or the path-independent Haas variant from `haas2004new`), report the highest-$K$ converged fit in Table `tab:model_comparison`, and update the framing of MS-GARCH from "regime-switching foil" to "regime-switching baseline at matched $K$" or document the convergence ceiling.

4. **The SM-CHMM "foil" uses a Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in estimator, not maximum-likelihood HSMM estimation.** The Bulla-Bulla 2006 paper (cited prominently in the introduction as the resolution of Rydén's failure) uses ML estimation of the explicit-duration HSMM. The present paper's SM-CHMM is a much weaker construction: a Viterbi-state path under the converged CHMM fit, then per-state AR(1) emissions and an AIC-selected sojourn family. This is a one-step approximation that does not exploit the joint likelihood. Concluding from `tab:extended_baselines` that "the geometric-sojourn restriction of the standard CHMM is therefore not the bottleneck" is therefore not supported by the evidence: the bottleneck might be the plug-in estimator. *Fix*: replace the SM-CHMM rows with a properly ML-estimated HSMM (Yu 2010 review, already in `references.bib`), or remove the SM-CHMM rows from the headline narrative and downgrade the corresponding claim in `discussion.tex` paragraph 4 to "under the plug-in estimator, the SM-CHMM is weaker than the standard CHMM; an ML-estimated HSMM is left as a competing baseline."

5. **The OoS KS test-power calibration uses i.i.d. resamples of the IS series as the "nearly correct" reference, which is the wrong null distribution for autocorrelated data.** Section `baselines_appendix.tex` Table `tab:ks_power` reports a $90\%$ pass rate for "i.i.d.\ resamples of $R_{\text{IS}}$ at length $T_{\text{OoS}}$" as the practical ceiling, but the IS data is itself autocorrelated (the very volatility-clustering signature the paper is reproducing). The asymptotic two-sample KS test under autocorrelated samples does not have the $\alpha$ size advertised; the $90\%$ "ceiling" is anchored on a misspecified null. The block-bootstrap recalibration in `tab:ks_block_bootstrap` is the right object, but the paper uses the asymptotic-pass-rate metric in the headline and only the block-bootstrap metric in an appendix. *Fix*: either replace the headline KS pass-rate column with the block-bootstrap pass-rate at $L = 10$ from Table `tab:ks_block_bootstrap` (CHMM-GED $90.2\%$; CHMM-N $89.4\%$; GARCH $13.6\%$), or report both side-by-side with the asymptotic value flagged as the lenient diagnostic.

6. **The CHMM-t bracket pinning at $\nu_{\min} = 2.1$ is an estimation artefact that the paper acknowledges but does not properly resolve.** The Student-$t$ distribution at $\nu = 2.1$ has finite variance but infinite excess kurtosis; the population-level kurtosis target is undefined. Two states pinning at $\nu_2 = 2.19$ and $\nu_4 = 2.10$ (Figure `fig:nu_hist`) means that in expectation the simulated kurtosis is unbounded, and the reported sample kurtosis of $14.4$ is a finite-sample realisation under that ill-defined population moment. The bracket sweep in `tab:nu_bracket` shows that lifting $\nu_{\min}$ from $2.1$ to $\{2.5, 3.0, 4.0\}$ gives kurtosis in $\{12.25, 13.25, 15.12\}$ — these are not stable, and the variability is a sign that the per-state $\nu_k$ ECM is multimodal at this $K$. *Fix*: report the CHMM-t headline numbers under the penalised ECM ($\lambda = 20$ shrinkage prior, mentioned in `discussion.tex` and Supp Section A.7) as the primary CHMM-t row, with the unpenalised ECM as an ablation. The penalised version brings simulated kurtosis to $\approx 8$ (close to observed $7.69$) at a $1$pp KS cost — that is the operationally useful CHMM-t.

7. **The cross-asset off-diagonal MAE comparison is misleadingly favourable to the Student-t copula.** Table `tab:cross_asset_supp_summary` shows that on OoS the Gaussian copula attains a smaller off-diagonal MAE ($0.202$) than the Student-t copula ($0.209$), and on the median OoS KS pass rate the Gaussian copula ties the Student-t ($87.5\%$ vs $85.8\%$). The IS advantage of the Student-t copula ($0.027$ vs $0.030$) is a $10\%$ relative gap on a small absolute scale. With six assets and 15 unique pairs, distinguishing $0.027$ from $0.030$ at any reasonable confidence requires per-pair standard errors that the paper does not report. The "Student-t copula is the strongest dependence layer in every relevant metric" sentence in `model.tex` Section 3.2.5 is not supported by Table `tab:cross_asset_supp_summary`. *Fix*: revise the cross-asset narrative to "Student-t copula matches the Gaussian copula in IS and OoS off-diagonal MAE within sampling error; we report the Student-t copula in the body for its standard tail-dependence interpretation." Add per-pair standard errors (paired bootstrap over the 200 simulation paths) to Table `tab:cross_asset` to defend whatever ordering survives.

### Questions for Authors
1. The Allman-Matias-Rhodes 2009 identifiability theorem requires $\mathbf{T}$ of full rank. Estimated transition matrices at $K = 18$ on $T = 2{,}516$ observations frequently have several near-zero rows (Figure `fig:transition_families` shows a $10^{-10}$ floor). What is the smallest singular value of $\hat{\mathbf{T}}$ at $K = 18$ across the four families, and how close are the small-mass states to being unidentified?
2. The CHMM-t IS kurtosis is $14.35$ at $K = 18$ versus an observed $7.68$. Section A.7 reports that the penalised version with $\lambda = 20$ brings simulated kurtosis to "$\approx 8$ at a $1$pp IS KS cost." Why is the unpenalised CHMM-t the headline row in `tab:model_comparison` rather than the penalised version?
3. The headline raw-return ACF-MAE column shows every CHMM row at $0.0234$--$0.0240$ versus the i.i.d.\ baselines at $0.0235$--$0.0243$. What does the same column look like for the Bartlett $99\%$ band-width-corrected metric, since at $T = 2{,}516$ a raw-return ACF in $[-0.04, 0.04]$ is not statistically distinguishable from zero at any single lag?
4. The Pipeline-B KS pass rate falls from $98.8\%$ IS / $85.8\%$ OoS (Table `tab:cross_asset`) to substantially lower values for the underlying Pipeline-A CHMM-t fits in Table `tab:cross_ticker` ($95.2\%$ / $84.1\%$ for SPY). Is the Pipeline-B sanity-check column genuinely higher than the underlying marginal fits, or is the discrepancy a path-count effect ($200$ versus $1{,}000$ paths)?
5. The Student-t copula profile likelihood in Figure `fig:copula_profile` peaks at $\nu^\star = 6$ with neighbouring grid points within $15$ log-likelihood units. What is the AIC penalty for the additional $\nu$ parameter, and does AIC select the same $\nu^\star = 6$ versus the Gaussian copula ($\nu = \infty$ limit, no penalty)?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **Refit MS-GARCH at $K \in \{6, 9, 12\}$ using a standard package implementation** and add the highest-$K$ converged result to Table `tab:model_comparison`. The current $K = 2$ MS-GARCH is not a fair regime-switching foil for a $K = 18$ CHMM.
2. **Replace the Viterbi-AR(1) SM-CHMM rows with an ML-estimated HSMM** following Yu (2010) or one of the standard `mhsmm` / `HiddenMarkov` package implementations. Without this, the "spectral mechanism dominates the sojourn-family extension" claim is unsupported.
3. **Promote the penalised CHMM-t ($\lambda = 20$) to the headline row** in Table `tab:model_comparison` and move the unpenalised ECM to an ablation. The unpenalised version's kurtosis overshoot is a fitting artefact that the manuscript already documents in Section 5.
4. **Add Christoffersen (1998) conditional-coverage and breach-independence tests to Table `tab:var_es`**, plus a bootstrap distribution of the Kupiec $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}}$ statistic at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$. The current report of "$\text{LR}_{\text{uc}} = 3.83$" against the asymptotic $\chi^2_1$ critical value of $3.84$ is at the boundary of significance and the small-sample distribution may not be well approximated by the asymptotic chi-squared.

### Minor Comments
1. `theory.tex` paragraph "Spectral mechanism" introduces $\mathbf{m}$ before defining $M_k$; the lag-zero discussion later swaps to $M_k$. Please put both definitions adjacent at the start of the paragraph.
2. `model.tex` Section 3.1.1 states $r_f = 0$ for the excess growth rate; this is fine for a synthetic-data pipeline but the term "excess" is misleading for a quantitative-finance reader. Please explicitly note in the data section that the empirical study uses zero risk-free rate as a normalisation rather than a literal excess-return construction.
3. `algorithms_appendix.tex` Algorithm 1 line 22 references `\ref{eq:gamma_xi}` — please verify this label resolves correctly in the final PDF; in the current source the label is in `algorithms_appendix.tex` rather than `model.tex`, and forward-references can be lost in float reordering.
4. Table `tab:model_comparison` reports CRPS for only four rows (CHMM-N, CHMM-t, CHMM-L, plus baselines), while CHMM-GED is dashed out as "the CRPS run uses the cached $K = 18$ archive". Please explain why CHMM-GED was excluded from the cached run, and either backfill or document the omission as a future-work item.
5. The seed root $20260420$ is occasionally referred to as "global" and elsewhere as "deterministic per-experiment sub-seeds derived additively". Please add a one-paragraph reproducibility note in the supplementary describing the exact sub-seed derivation rule.

### Recommendation
**Major Revision.** The mathematics is mostly correct but the headline framing systematically over-states the contribution: the spectral identity is folklore re-framed (Weakness 1), MS-GARCH and SM-CHMM baselines are weaker than they should be (Weaknesses 3-4), the CHMM-t headline carries a known fitting artefact (Weakness 6), and the cross-asset narrative is not supported by the supporting tables (Weakness 7). The empirical study is solid in principle and the contribution is real once these are addressed, but in its current form the paper reads as overclaimed.

## Reviewer 3 — Very Hard

*Skeptical methodologist with publications in regime-switching estimation, copula-based risk modelling, and non-parametric synthetic-data evaluation. Reads for novelty relative to prior art, methodological gaps, and overclaim.*

### Summary
The paper repackages the standard continuous-emission HMM at higher $K$ as a "synthetic data generator" for equity returns and claims a closed-form ACF identity, four interchangeable emission families, and a Student-t copula multi-asset extension. The closed-form ACF identity is well-known. The $K = 18$ headline operating point is selected partly on the OoS evaluation window. The CHMM-t variant is unstable. The cross-asset construction matches the Gaussian copula on the OoS metric the paper actually cares about. The empirical comparison universe is six tickers. As currently positioned, the contribution is incremental at best; substantial framing changes are required before this is publishable in a serious venue.

### Strengths
1. The CHMM-GED ECM construction (`algorithms_appendix.tex` paragraph "CHMM-GED M-step") is the most novel component of the paper. The three-stage CM with bracketed location, closed-form scale, and bracketed shape is a clean implementation of an idea that has been suggested in the GED-emission HMM literature but rarely carried through to bracket-monotone ECM. The boundary-nesting structure ($p = 1, 2$) is genuine.
2. The reproducibility infrastructure is unusually complete for the venue: a Julia package, a single seed root, a dependency manifest, and a one-command rebuild.

### Weaknesses

1. **The "central technical contribution" — the closed-form mixture-of-eigenvalues identity for the absolute-return ACF — is not novel.** Timmermann (2000, *Quantitative Finance*, "Moments of Markov switching models", already cited as `timmermann2000moments`) gives the moment-generating function and the autocovariance structure of general Markov-switching models with conditional means and variances depending on the latent state, including the spectral decomposition that the present manuscript presents as Theorem 1. The bilinear form $\mathbf{m}^\top \mathrm{diag}(\bar{\boldsymbol\pi})\,\mathbf{T}^\tau\,\mathbf{m}$ for the lag-$\tau$ cross-moment is direct from the law of total expectation and the Markov property; the eigendecomposition of $\mathbf{T}^\tau$ via Perron-Frobenius is standard Markov-chain theory (Levin-Peres, already cited). The reframing of Rydén's $K = 2$--$3$ failure as a $K$-rank statement is a useful pedagogical contribution but is not a new mathematical result. The abstract claim "We show that... The mechanism is spectral: ... [the identity] recasts the low-$K$ limitation of regime-switching HMMs as a $K$-rank statement on $\mathbf T$" should be revised to credit the prior literature explicitly. *Fix*: cite Timmermann (2000), the Hamilton (1994) textbook chapter on Markov-switching moments, and Krolzig (1997) in `theory.tex` Section 3.3 and downgrade the contribution claim to "we apply the standard spectral decomposition to give an explicit closed form for the absolute-return ACF and use it to reframe Rydén's low-$K$ failure".

2. **The headline operating point $K = 18$ is selected using the OoS evaluation window, and the held-out $K^\star$ is $3$.** The Supplementary Section A.7 ("Pre-OoS validation $K$-selection") admits explicitly that held-out log-likelihood, BIC, HQC, and CAIC all select $K^\star = 3$ on a clean validation slice that avoids the OoS window. AIC selects $K^\star = 6$. *None* of the principled criteria that don't peek at the OoS window select $K = 18$. The justification for $K = 18$ in `results.tex` Section 4.2 is "it sits at the top of the IS KS plateau and inside the CAIC-minimizing band". The IS KS plateau is computed on the same window the model is fit to, so it is not a model-selection criterion in any standard sense. The CAIC argument is not reproducible from the supplied numbers (Section A.7 says CAIC selects $K^\star = 3$, not "a band including $K = 18$"). The headline numbers are therefore optimistically biased upward by a state-count selection that uses the evaluation window. *Fix*: make $K = 3$ the primary headline operating point and re-state the abstract numbers at $K = 3$ ($89.7\%$ IS KS, $80.1\%$ OoS KS, ACF-MAE $0.0467$ on $|G_t|$, raw $G_t$ ACF-MAE $0.0240$). Treat $K = 18$ as the tail-fidelity ablation. Move the current Discussion paragraph "we acknowledge $K = 18$ is best read as a multi-metric choice" to the abstract and the introduction.

3. **The CHMM-t variant is the manuscript's headline heavy-tail story but is operationally unusable.** The unpenalised ECM produces simulated IS kurtosis $14.4$ versus observed $7.68$ — an $87\%$ overshoot. The bracket-floor sweep in `tab:nu_bracket` shows the overshoot is robust to lifting $\nu_{\min}$ to $\{2.5, 3.0, 4.0\}$, so it is not a bracket artefact. Per-state $\hat{\nu}_k$ values are wildly bimodal (Figure `fig:nu_hist`: thirteen states pinned at the $\nu_{\max} = 50$ upper bracket, two pinned near the $\nu_{\min} = 2.1$ lower bracket, three intermediate). At $K = 18$ on $T = 2{,}516$ observations, the per-state samples for the heavy-tail states are tiny (with stationary mass distributed across $18$ states, and the heavy-tail states being concentrated in the high-volatility regime, the sample sizes for those states are in the low hundreds). The cross-ticker simulated kurtosis values reported in Table `tab:cross_ticker` are $46.4$ (NVDA, observed $5.5$), $99.7$ (JNJ, observed $9.0$), $18.8$ (JPM, observed $7.6$) — these are not minor overshoots, they are catastrophic on three of six tickers. The seed-to-seed std on simulated kurtosis is $1.95$ (Table `tab:multiseed_headline`) versus $0.05$--$0.09$ for the other three families. The variant decision guide in `discussion.tex` Table `tab:variant_choice` recommends CHMM-t as the per-state heavy-tail option for tail-conditional consumers — given the kurtosis instability documented in the manuscript itself, this recommendation is not defensible. *Fix*: either remove CHMM-t from the recommended-variant table entirely (CHMM-GED dominates it on every metric except a label-by-label match to a fixed shape parameter), or replace the "tail-conditional consumers" recommendation with the penalised CHMM-t ($\lambda = 20$ shrinkage) and report the penalised variant as the headline CHMM-t row.

4. **The cross-asset Student-t copula contribution does not survive the OoS metric the paper itself defines as the headline.** Table `tab:cross_asset_supp_summary` reports OoS off-diagonal MAE: SIM $0.252$, Gaussian copula $0.202$, Student-t copula $0.209$, truncated C-vine $0.235$. **The Gaussian copula beats the Student-t copula on OoS off-diagonal MAE.** On median OoS KS the Gaussian copula ($87.5\%$) ties the Student-t copula ($85.8\%$). The IS advantage of the Student-t copula ($0.027$ vs $0.030$) is $10\%$ on a small absolute scale and is not bracketed by per-pair standard errors anywhere in the manuscript. The `discussion.tex` paragraph "Why the Student-t copula beats SIM on cross-asset fidelity" is a strawman: the Student-t does not beat SIM by any meaningful margin once the OoS metric is the focus, and it is the Gaussian copula that beats SIM, not the Student-t. *Fix*: revise the abstract claim from "rank-based Student-t copula on the CHMM marginals: profile MLE selects $\nu^\star = 6$, reproducing the off-diagonal correlation matrix... with MAE $0.027$" to "rank-based copula on the CHMM marginals: Gaussian and Student-t copulas achieve indistinguishable OoS off-diagonal MAE ($0.202$, $0.209$); the IS-only metric favours the Student-t at $\nu^\star = 6$." The paper's actual cross-asset finding is that the choice between Gaussian and Student-t copula is not settled by this universe; pretending otherwise is overclaiming.

5. **The empirical universe (six tickers, one country, one asset class, no rates / FX / commodities apart from a single GLD appendix add) is far too narrow for a "reference synthetic-data generator" claim.** A serious reference generator for equity returns is evaluated on the S&P 500 universe, not on six tickers, of which two (NVDA, JPM) the paper itself acknowledges fail OoS by margins of $25$--$30$ percentage points on KS. The non-US-asset extension in Appendix `sec:non_us_asset_supp` is one ticker (GLD) and the paper itself notes it suffers a $0\%$ OoS KS pass rate. The paper's conclusion of "reference synthetic-data generator" is not supported by the breadth of the evaluation. *Fix*: either run the full-panel evaluation on at least the S&P 100 (101 tickers, all available from the same Polygon / Alpaca pipeline already in the data acknowledgments) and report the cross-sectional distribution of IS / OoS KS pass rates and the proportion of tickers that fail the OoS test, or downgrade the abstract from "reference synthetic-data generator" to "single-asset CHMM with cross-asset extension demonstrated on a six-ticker subset".

6. **The "spectral mechanism dominates the sojourn extension" empirical claim relies on a Viterbi plug-in HSMM, not an ML-estimated HSMM.** Bulla-Bulla (2006), the explicit competitor cited in the introduction as the prior resolution of Rydén's failure, uses ML estimation. The present paper's SM-CHMM rows in `tab:extended_baselines` ($K = 18$, $77$--$80\%$ IS KS, $63$--$69\%$ OoS KS) are weaker than the headline CHMM rows but the comparison is not apples-to-apples: the Viterbi-AR(1) plug-in is a one-shot estimator that does not iterate to the joint maximum-likelihood. Without an ML HSMM benchmark, the spectral-mechanism-vs-sojourn-extension story has no foundation. *Fix*: implement a proper ML HSMM (e.g., the explicit-duration HSMM via Yu's 2010 algorithm, already cited as `yu2010hidden`) and refit at the same $K = 18$. If the ML HSMM is competitive with or beats the CHMM, the manuscript's central comparative claim collapses; if not, the manuscript has a defensible result. The current SM-CHMM evidence does not settle the question.

7. **The OoS window ($T = 572$ days, January 2024 through April 2026) is a benign regime that contains no major equity-market stress event.** The IS window ($2014$-$2024$) contains the COVID crash. The OoS contains the rate-hike cycle and the AI rally on NVDA, both moderate-magnitude events relative to historical equity-market crises. A "synthetic-data generator for stress testing" claim, made explicitly in `introduction.tex` paragraph 1, requires evaluation on a window that contains a stress event genuinely outside the IS regime library. The 2008 financial crisis, 1987 Black Monday, or even the 2020 COVID crash itself (excluded from IS to make this comparison) are the relevant stress windows. *Fix*: either re-fit on a 2002-2007 IS window and evaluate on 2008-2010 OoS, or down-rank the "stress testing" framing from the abstract and introduction. The current OoS does not contain a stress event the IS does not.

### Questions for Authors
1. The Krolzig (1997) book and the Timmermann (2000) paper give the eigenvalue-decomposition autocovariance form for general Markov-switching models. What is novel in Theorem 1 (Supp Section A.4) relative to those references?
2. Why does the abstract lead with $K = 18$ when the held-out log-likelihood, BIC, HQC, and CAIC all pick $K^\star = 3$? Is there any held-out criterion (other than ones that use the OoS window) that selects $K = 18$?
3. Table `tab:cross_asset_supp_summary` reports the Gaussian copula at OoS off-diagonal MAE $0.202$ and the Student-t copula at $0.209$. On what objective metric does the Student-t copula outperform the Gaussian copula on OoS?
4. Why is the recommended CHMM-t variant (Table `tab:variant_choice`, "Per-state heavy tails for tail-conditional consumers") not the penalised version with the $1/\nu_k$ shrinkage prior at $\lambda = 20$ that the manuscript itself recommends in `discussion.tex` paragraph "Closing the kurtosis gap"?
5. The manuscript repeatedly cites the predecessor `alswaidan2026hybrid` (an unpublished or in-progress work by the same first author). What is the relationship between that paper's Laplace-quantile-binning + Poisson-jump construction and the present paper's CHMM, beyond shared notation, and how is the present paper a substantive advance rather than an incremental replacement of the discretisation step with continuous emissions?

### Requested Experiments / Analyses
1. **A full S&P 100 (or S&P 500) cross-sectional evaluation** of CHMM-N at $K = 3$ and $K = 18$, reporting the median, $25$th, and $75$th percentile of IS / OoS KS pass rates across the cross-section, plus the fraction of tickers that fail the OoS test at $\alpha = 0.05$. The current six-ticker universe is insufficient for a "reference generator" claim. With the existing data pipeline, this is a one-day rerun.

3. **An ML-estimated HSMM (Yu 2010 algorithm) at the same $K = 18$ on SPY** added to Table `tab:extended_baselines`, replacing or supplementing the Viterbi-plug-in SM-CHMM rows. The "spectral dominates sojourn extension" claim cannot be supported without this baseline.
4. **Conditional-coverage VaR backtests (Christoffersen 1998, already cited as `christoffersen1998evaluating`)** added to Table `tab:var_es`, plus a finite-sample bootstrap distribution of the Kupiec $\text{LR}_{\text{uc}}$ statistic at $T_{\text{OoS}} = 572$. ### Recommendation
**Major Revision bordering on Reject-and-Resubmit.** The contribution as positioned is incremental and overclaimed. A re-positioned manuscript that (i) credits Timmermann (2000) and the Hamilton / Krolzig textbook treatment for the spectral identity, (ii) leads with the held-out-selected $K^\star = 3$ headline, (iii) replaces the unpenalised CHMM-t with the penalised version, (iv) acknowledges that Gaussian and Student-t copulas are indistinguishable on the OoS metric, (v) extends the cross-sectional universe to at least the S&P 100, and (vi) adds an ML HSMM and Christoffersen conditional-coverage baselines, would be a substantively different paper that I could recommend for publication. As written, the manuscript over-states what the evidence actually demonstrates.
